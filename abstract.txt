We estimate the three Herschel-Bulkley parameters (yield stress sigma_Y, power-law index n, consistency parameter eta) of complex fluids -- pastes, chunky sauces, granular slurries -- for which conventional rheometers cannot provide useful measurements, by matching dam-break video footage against physics-based simulation, following the protocol of [Hamamichi et al. 2023]. The inverse is accurate but slow: each estimation requires hundreds of in-loop material-point-method evaluations and therefore takes around eight hours of GPU time per setup, so the method serves as a research tool rather than a routine measurement.

We compress this inverse from around eight hours to under a minute per setup by replacing the in-loop simulator with a mixture of Gaussian-process experts fitted to a corpus of MPM dam-break simulations. We further replace the manual camera-calibration workflow that precedes the inverse with an algorithmic two-stage pipeline based on ChArUco markers and silhouette-edge refinement. The rest -- its identifiability handling and multi-observation protocol -- follows the predecessor.

We validate our method by comparing against the simulation-in-the-loop predecessor on real-video captures and against parallel-plate rheometer measurements on materials the rheometers can handle, and find that the recovered parameters are on par with the predecessor at three orders of magnitude lower latency. The seconds-scale inversion enables three uses inaccessible at hours-per-setup cost: tracking parameter freshness for time-varying fluids across repeated captures, characterizing rheometer-infeasible materials as a routine task rather than a dedicated experiment, and a multi-observation protocol that approaches parallel-plate precision from phone videos alone.
