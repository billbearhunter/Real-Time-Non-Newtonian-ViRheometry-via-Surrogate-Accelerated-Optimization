\section{Introduction}

Many everyday fluids---yogurts, pastes, sauces, slurries, cosmetic
emulsions---exhibit non-Newtonian behaviour that governs how they
pour, spread, cling, and deform. Parallel-plate and cone-and-plate
rheometers measure this behaviour through a controlled-gap shear
flow, but fail on materials with inclusions larger than the
measurement gap and on materials whose rheology evolves faster than
the lab procedure can capture.

Non-Newtonian video rheometry~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}
addresses this gap by estimating Herschel--Bulkley parameters
$(\sigma_Y, \eta, n)$ from a simple dam-break flow: a material point
method (MPM) forward model is repeatedly executed inside an optimiser
until the simulated flow reproduces the observed silhouette. The
approach naturally handles materials that instrumental rheometers
cannot, but is bottlenecked by its inner loop---one MPM dam-break
takes several minutes on modern GPU hardware and the
derivative-free optimiser issues hundreds of candidates, so the
end-to-end inverse takes \emph{around eight hours per setup}. This
latency confines the method to offline research use and blocks two
payoffs that we evaluate in this paper:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{Fresh parameters for time-varying fluids.} When a
    material's properties evolve during the measurement
    window---sedimentation, aging, temperature drift, mixing history,
    cooking cooldown---an hour-scale inversion returns a parameter
    describing a past state. The inferred value is stale by the time
    it is delivered.
    \item \emph{Practical repeated real-fluid validation.} Comparing
    multiple rheometer-referenced materials or repeated setups requires
    running the inverse many times. At hours per setup, such panels are
    limited to offline showcase studies; at seconds per setup, they fit
    inside an ordinary measurement session.
\end{itemize}

We propose a surrogate-accelerated framework that compresses the
inverse from hours to seconds per setup, making these two payoffs
available within a practical workflow. Our technical core couples
three components.

\emph{(i)}~A \emph{hierarchical mixture of Gaussian-process experts}
(HMoGP) replaces MPM inside the optimizer. The trained surrogate bank
partitions the forward map by container geometry, terminal flow
distance, and normalized trajectory shape. At runtime the observable
tuple $(W,H,\mathbf{y})$ deterministically routes each query to a
local exact GP expert, with a final observation-evidence rerank that
keeps the selected expert aligned with the observation source and
runout regime.

\emph{(ii)}~A \emph{GP-aware inverse likelihood} addresses the
yield-stress/consistency ridge that arises when the observed
shear-rate range is dominated by the consistency term. CMA-ES
minimizes a log-space Type-II marginal-likelihood objective that uses
the routed GP's predictive variance, an observation-noise floor, and
the corresponding Occam term. The remaining ridge is reported through
multi-start dispersion and local Laplace confidence intervals rather
than hidden by a hand-tuned yield-stress prior.

\emph{(iii)}~An \emph{algorithmic camera-calibration pipeline}
replaces the manual fSpy-and-Photoshop workflow that precedes the
inverse in prior work. A ChArUco marker board provides an initial
pose estimate, and a subsequent silhouette-edge refinement aligns
the camera against the dam-break geometry without per-capture human
annotation, removing the largest fixed cost of the capture
workflow.

Our work inherits the problem setup, dam-break observation protocol,
and inverse formulation
from~\citet{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}---including the multi-setup
joint inversion they introduce to address the
yield-stress/consistency degeneracy---and contributes the machinery
needed to make that formulation computationally practical. By
porting the inverse onto a learned surrogate, the same
setup-selection and two-setup joint machinery becomes affordable per
material rather than only per showcase, with the inverse returning
within the latency required by these uses.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Physical Measurements and Flow Rheology Signals}

Quantitative rheology is traditionally measured with rheometers
(parallel-plate, cone-and-plate, or rotational) that apply a
controlled laminar flow under known shear rate to recover the
constitutive relation between shear stress and shear rate. These
instruments require a narrow gap to enforce a clean shear profile,
which excludes materials with millimeter-scale inclusions, as well
as materials whose rheology evolves faster than the lab procedure
can capture. Less-controlled but more accessible alternatives, such
as the Bostwick consistometer, give a single readable measurement
from a simple dam-break test.

Gravity-driven flow has a long pedigree as a rheometric observation
that complements idealized-flow rheometers. The slump test
of~\citet{pashias1996fifty} recovers yield stress from a single
geometric reading; \citet{roussel2005fiftycent} extend this argument
to the spreading regime that matches dam-break geometry directly;
the final-deposit shape of a stopped free-surface flow is itself a
rheological signature~\cite{coussot1996rheological};
and~\citet{ancey2007plasticity} frames the broader class of
gravity-driven yield-stress flows as a standard rheometric regime.
The relation between flow-front position and Herschel--Bulkley
parameters is made quantitative by the similarity analyses
of~\citet{liumei1989slow} and~\citet{balmforth2007two}, the same
analytic framework leveraged by~\citet{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}.

Our forward simulator inherits the MPM lineage brought into
graphics by~\cite{stomakhin2013material,jiang2015affine,hu2018moving,
jiang2016material}, with yield-stress rheology realised through
return-mapping
formulations~\cite{yue2015continuum,ram2015material,klar2016drucker,
tampubolon2017multi,wolper2019cdmpm}. We use the Herschel--Bulkley
return map of~\citet{yue2015continuum} on
Taichi~\cite{hu2019taichi}; \citet{nagasawa2019mixing}, from the
same lab as our predecessor, shares our material focus.


\subsection{Video-Based Material Estimation}

Estimating simulation parameters from video by matching rendered and
observed dynamics has a long lineage in graphics and
vision~\cite{bhat2002computing,monszpart2016smash}.
\citet{takahashi2019video} established the video-to-parameter pattern
for viscous fluids by matching rendered and observed dam-break
silhouettes per scene; \citet{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} extended
this to the Herschel--Bulkley triple $(\sigma_Y,\eta,n)$ and is the
direct predecessor of our work. We inherit their dam-break observation
protocol, inverse objective, and simulation-in-the-loop formulation
(including the multi-setup machinery they introduce against the
yield-stress/consistency degeneracy), and contribute the surrogate
that makes the same machinery affordable per material rather than
per showcase.

Related work recovers material parameters from other visual
modalities: video-based cloth friction~\cite{rasheed2020visual},
particle-image-velocimetry-based rheology through physics-informed
networks~\cite{thakur2025learning}, and consumer-video neural
regression for scalar viscosity~\cite{rauzan2024protorheology}. These
target different material classes or observation modalities and do
not recover the full Herschel--Bulkley triple. Free-surface
extraction from experimental video is treated
in~\cite{cochard2009tracking}. Inverse rheometry has also been
approached in the geophysical-flow setting using shallow-water
forward models and adjoint methods~\cite{martin2010inverse}; our
formulation differs in using a full three-dimensional MPM forward
model and a derivative-free inverse over a learned surrogate.

An alternative paradigm pursues parameter recovery via differentiable
simulation. The differentiable MLS-MPM
family~\cite{hu2019chainqueen,hu2020difftaichi,huang2021plasticinelab}
has been applied to deformable and plastic materials;
gradSim~\cite{murthy2021gradsim} demonstrates end-to-end
differentiable physics with differentiable rendering. Most relevant
to non-Newtonian recovery from video,
PAC-NeRF~\cite{li2023pacnerf} couples differentiable MPM with NeRF
rendering to recover viscosity parameters from multi-view video, and
Eastman~\cite{eastman2024recovery} recovers Herschel--Bulkley
parameters from a ketchup column-collapse video via differentiable
MPM and neural rendering. These approaches optimize per scene and do
not amortize a reusable surrogate across materials. Evidence from
PlasticineLab~\cite{huang2021plasticinelab} indicates that gradients
through non-smooth plastic return maps are ill-conditioned,
motivating our choice of a gradient-free inverse over a
probabilistic surrogate.


\subsection{Identifiability of Free-Surface Observations}

A prerequisite for replacing simulation-in-the-loop inversion with a
surrogate is that the chosen observation
$\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$ carries enough information to identify
$(\sigma_Y,\eta,n)$. Recent inverse-analysis work establishes that
free-surface evolution alone is sufficient:
\citet{martinpritchard2022inferring} show that the full generalized
Newtonian constitutive function is in principle uniquely recoverable
from free-surface evolution; \citet{ahmadi2022identification}
compute linearly-independent sensitivity coefficients of the three
Herschel--Bulkley parameters with respect to free-surface readings;
and~\citet{muchiri2024identification} demonstrate inverse recovery
of yield stress and consistency from shallow-water elevation
hydrographs under viscoplastic rheology. Our scalar flow-front trace
$\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$ is a low-dimensional summary of this
evolution, and the residual yield-stress/consistency degeneracy we
address in~\S\ref{sec:inverse} is therefore not a fundamental
information-theoretic limit of the phone-video observation but a
practical ill-conditioning that arises in the low-$\sigma_Y$ regime
where the yield term is dominated by the viscous contribution.


\subsection{Surrogate-Accelerated Inverse}

Gaussian-process regression~\cite{rasmussen2006gaussian} pairs
flexible nonparametric regression with calibrated predictive
uncertainty, making it a natural surrogate for expensive simulators;
sparse and variational approximations~\cite{snelson2006sparse,
quinonero2005unifying,titsias2009variational,hensman2013gaussian,
matthews2016sparse} extend it past exact-GP data limits. For
heterogeneous local structure, mixture-of-experts
models~\cite{jordan1994hierarchical,yuksel2012twenty} decompose
prediction across specialists with a gating mechanism; mixture-of-GP
variants instantiate this with GP
experts~\cite{tresp2001mixtures,rasmussen2002infinite,
meeds2006alternative,nguyen2008local,yuan2008variational,
trapp2020deep}, and the Bayesian Committee
Machine~\cite{tresp2000bayesian,cao2014generalized,
deisenroth2015distributed,liu2018generalized} aggregates independent
GP predictions by closed-form variance-weighted fusion (recently
applied to physics emulation~\cite{cohen2024sparse}).

Inverting expensive simulators has been studied under Bayesian
calibration of computer models~\cite{kennedy2001bayesian} and
simulation-based inference~\cite{cranmer2020frontier}, with efficient
global~\cite{jones1998efficient} and
Bayesian~\cite{snoek2012practical,shahriari2016taking} optimisation
as the dominant search strategies, and derivative-free optimisation
on GP surrogates~\cite{bajaj2019gaussian,hansen2016cma} applied to
inverse viscoplasticity~\cite{vandamme2021bayesian} and
viscoelasticity~\cite{hashemi2025bayesian}. The substitution itself
is not heuristic: \citet{stuart2018posterior} bound the Hellinger
distance between the surrogate-based and true-simulator posteriors
by moments of the surrogate's predictive error, with the bound
extended to learned-hyperparameter settings
by~\citet{teckentrup2020convergence}.


\subsection{Camera Calibration}

Camera pose and intrinsics from images is a foundational
computer-vision task~\cite{hartley2004multiple}. Marker-based
fiducials---AprilTag~\cite{olson2011apriltag},
ARTag~\cite{fiala2005artag}, ArUco/ChArUco~\cite{garrido2014automatic,
garrido2016generation}---supply 3D-to-2D correspondences solvable
via PnP variants like EPnP~\cite{lepetit2009epnp}, with sub-pixel
corner refinement~\cite{forstner1987fast} sharpening the
correspondences. Silhouette- and edge-based refinement aligns the
camera against object boundaries through chamfer-distance
optimisation on a Euclidean distance
transform~\cite{borgefors1986distance,maurer2003linear} of Canny
edges~\cite{canny1986computational}. Our pipeline
(\S\ref{sec:calib}) combines ChArUco initialisation with two-phase
silhouette-edge refinement, replacing the manual
fSpy-and-Photoshop workflow used
by~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}.


\section{Background}

\subsection{Herschel--Bulkley Rheology}

Many non-Newtonian fluids are well approximated by the
Herschel--Bulkley (HB) model~\cite{herschel1926konsistenzmessungen},
which expresses the shear-stress response as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hb-scalar}
    \tau \;=\; \sigma_Y \;+\; \eta\,\dot\gamma^{\,n},
\end{equation}
where $\tau$ is the shear stress, $\dot\gamma$ the shear rate,
$\sigma_Y$ the yield stress, $\eta$ the consistency parameter, and
$n \in (0, 1]$ controls shear thinning ($n < 1$) versus Newtonian
behaviour ($n = 1$). In three dimensions the deviatoric stress tensor
is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hb-tensor}
    \boldsymbol{\tau}^{\mathrm{dev}}
    \;=\;
    \left[\;\frac{\sigma_Y}{\|\mathbf{D}\|}
    \;+\; \eta\,\|\mathbf{D}\|^{\,n-1}\;\right] \mathbf{D},
    \qquad
    \|\mathbf{D}\|
    = \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{D}\!:\!\mathbf{D}},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{D}$ is the rate-of-strain tensor. The admissible
stress region is delimited by the von~Mises yield function
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hb-yield}
    \Phi_{Y}(\boldsymbol{\tau})
    \;=\; \|\boldsymbol{\tau}^{\mathrm{dev}}\| \;-\; \sigma_Y \;\le\; 0,
\end{equation}
with the plastic flow rule of Eq.~\eqref{eq:hb-tensor} activated only
when $\Phi_{Y} > 0$. We adopt the elasto-plastic MPM realisation of
Yue et al.~\cite{yue2015continuum}, which recovers the same
$(\sigma_Y, \eta, n)$ triple at steady state via a Newton--Raphson
return map that enforces $\Phi_{Y} \le 0$ on each plastic step. The HB
response is therefore characterised by the three-parameter vector
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\theta} \;=\; (\sigma_Y,\,\eta,\,n) \in \Theta \subset
    \mathbb{R}^{3}.
\end{equation}
This parameter triple is the same one used by prior non-Newtonian
video rheometry~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}; we treat the
constitutive model as fixed and focus on recovering
$\boldsymbol{\theta}$ at video-rate latency.


\subsection{Dam-Break MPM Forward Model}

Following the experimental protocol
of~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}, we obtain $\boldsymbol{\theta}$
by observing a dam-break flow released from a rectangular fluid column
of width $W$ and height $H$ on a flat, no-slip floor. The forward
simulator integrates the HB return map of \S3.1 on a
moving-least-squares MPM~\cite{hu2018moving} grid with spacing
$\Delta x \approx 10^{-1}$\,cm and timestep $\Delta t = 5\times
10^{-4}$\,s, implemented on Taichi~\cite{hu2019taichi}. The
observation is a vector of flow-front displacements sampled at eight
uniformly spaced frames $t_1,\dots,t_8$ after release:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:y-def}
    \mathbf{y}
    \;=\;
    \bigl(x_1,\,x_2,\,\dots,\,x_8\bigr)^{\!\top}
    \;\in\;
    \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}^{8},
\end{equation}
where $x_i$ is the maximum lateral extent of the fluid at time $t_i$
in cm. By construction $x_1 \le x_2 \le \dots \le x_8$, since the
flow front cannot retract. We write the forward simulator as
$\mathbf{y} = \mathcal{S}(\boldsymbol{\theta};\,W, H)$.

\paragraph{Implementation details.}
Container walls use sticky boundaries (interior-cell momentum zeroed
each step; wall-penetrating particles excluded from the silhouette);
the fluid surface is a signed-distance field smoothed by
Laplacian-then-biharmonic passes with per-cell $\varphi$ clamped to
the local particle-radius range
$[\varphi_{\min},\,\varphi_{\max}]$, removing the per-frame
$\varphi$ tuning of earlier pipelines. For synthetic training and
validation, the simulator reports the front extent in world
centimetres under a fixed virtual camera convention; the real-camera
calibration of \S\ref{sec:calib} maps captured pixels into this same
metric observation space, but is not used to generate the training
corpus.


\subsection{Observation Pipeline}
\label{sec:obs-pipeline}

Computing the observation vector $\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$ from a
smartphone video requires two preprocessing steps: (a)~recovering the
camera pose against the experimental scene, and (b)~extracting a
silhouette-based flow-front distance at the eight uniformly spaced
frames defining $\mathbf{y}$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:y-def}. The
silhouette-extraction step follows the protocol
of~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} (background removal, binary
segmentation, frontal-extent measurement). The camera-calibration
step is our contribution and is detailed as part of the method
in~\S\ref{sec:calib}.


\subsection{Inverse Video Rheometry as Simulation-in-the-Loop Optimization}

Given an observation $\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$ captured from a real
experiment at geometry $(W, H)$, video rheometry recovers
$\boldsymbol{\theta}$ by matching simulator output against the
observation,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:inverse-generic}
    \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}
    \;=\;
    \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}\in\Theta}\;
    \mathcal{L}\bigl(\boldsymbol{\theta};\,
    \mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}},\,W,\,H\bigr),
\end{equation}
where
$\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \|\mathcal{S}(\boldsymbol{\theta};\,W,H)
- \mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}\|^{2}$ is an observation-matching loss.
Because $\mathcal{S}$ is defined by an MPM integration,
$\mathcal{L}$ is expensive---several minutes per query on modern
GPU hardware---and non-smooth through the plastic return map. The
simulation-in-the-loop formulation of~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}
evaluates $\mathcal{S}$ directly inside a derivative-free optimiser,
paying the cost of a full MPM simulation at every candidate
$\boldsymbol{\theta}$.

The loss landscape carries a well-known identifiability pathology.
Define the \emph{characteristic shear rate} that separates the
yield-dominated from the viscous-dominated regime,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gammastar}
    \dot\gamma^{\star}(\sigma_Y, \eta, n)
    \;=\; \bigl(\sigma_Y / \eta\bigr)^{1/n}.
\end{equation}
Rewriting Eq.~\eqref{eq:hb-scalar} as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hb-factored}
    \tau(\dot\gamma)
    \;=\; \eta\,\dot\gamma^{\,n}\left[\,1
    \;+\; \left(\frac{\dot\gamma^{\star}}{\dot\gamma}\right)^{\!n}\,\right],
\end{equation}
shows that as $\dot\gamma / \dot\gamma^{\star} \to \infty$ the
second bracket tends to $1$ and the stress becomes asymptotically
governed by $(\eta, n)$ alone; formally
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ridge}
    \left.
      \frac{\partial \tau}{\partial \sigma_Y}
    \right/\!
    \left.
      \frac{\partial \tau}{\partial \eta}
    \right|_{\dot\gamma \gg \dot\gamma^{\star}}
    \;=\;
    \frac{1}{\dot\gamma^{\,n}}
    \;\longrightarrow\; 0.
\end{equation}
If the observed dam-break regime lies largely above
$\dot\gamma^{\star}$, $\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$ becomes effectively
insensitive to $\sigma_Y$ while still constraining $(\eta, n)$, so a
continuum of near-equal-loss solutions trade $\sigma_Y$ for a
compensating increase in $\eta$. \citet{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}
identify this degeneracy through dimensional-similarity analysis
and propose multi-setup joint inversion as a mitigation, paying
the simulation-in-the-loop cost $N$ times for an $N$-setup
estimate.

When the target material itself evolves during the measurement
window (sedimentation, aging, temperature drift, cooking cooldown),
the true state $\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t}$ drifts during the inverse
and an hour-scale estimate no longer describes the current
condition; we therefore want estimation latency short enough to
\emph{track} the evolving state, not merely to reduce amortised
compute cost.

\section{Method (Surrogate-Accelerated Inverse)}
\label{sec:method-block}

\subsection{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:problem}

We keep the physical forward model of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:inverse-generic}, but replace each in-loop MPM call by
a frozen mixture-of-experts surrogate. The online estimator receives
a calibrated dam-break observation
$\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}\in\mathbb{R}^{8}$ and setup geometry
$(W,H)$, routes the observation to a small set of local GP experts,
and solves for
$\boldsymbol{\theta}=(n,\eta,\sigma_Y)$ with a GP-aware
Type-II maximum-likelihood loss. The same machinery handles a second
observation of the same material by sharing one
$\boldsymbol{\theta}$ across both setups.

Figure~\ref{fig:overview} summarises the implemented pipeline. Offline,
MPM simulations are partitioned by geometry and by the observed
flow-front trajectory, and one exact GP expert is trained for each
resulting sub-region. Online, the observable tuple
$(W,H,\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}})$ selects a primary sub-expert by a
deterministic three-stage gate, refined by a source-aware
observation-evidence rerank; CMA-ES then optimises over
the routed expert's padded training box. A first setup can also
propose the next physical experiment through the analytical
Hessian-orthogonal rule of \citet{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}, whose
closed-form Fisher-information construction we adopt unchanged.

\paragraph{Formal statement.}\label{sec:problem-formal}
The original simulation-in-the-loop inverse is
\begin{taggedeqn}{P}{P}
    \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}
    =
    \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}\in\Theta}
    \left\|\mathcal{S}(\boldsymbol{\theta};W,H)
    -\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}\right\|^2 .
\end{taggedeqn}
Our single-setup estimator replaces
$\mathcal{S}$ by a routed GP surrogate
$\tilde{\mathcal{S}}_{k^\star}$ and minimises the negative log
marginal likelihood
\begin{taggedeqn}{P$'$}{Pprime}
    \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}
    =
    \arg\min_{\mathbf{z}\in\Theta_{k^\star}}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{GP}}\!\left(
      \tilde{\mathcal{S}}_{k^\star}(\mathbf{z};W,H),
      \mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}\right)
    + p_{\mathrm{sup}}\,
      \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{box}}(\mathbf{z};k^\star),
\end{taggedeqn}
where $\mathbf{z}=(n,\log\eta,\log\sigma_Y)$, $k^\star$ is selected
from $(W,H,\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}})$, and
$\Theta_{k^\star}$ is the routed sub's padded training box. For two
setups $a,b$ of the same material, conditional independence gives the
joint objective
\begin{taggedeqn}{P$^{\star}$}{Pstar}
    \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}
    =
    \arg\min_{\mathbf{z}\in\Theta_{a,b}}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{GP}}^{(a)}(\mathbf{z})
    + \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{GP}}^{(b)}(\mathbf{z})
    + p_{\mathrm{sup}}\,
      \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(
      \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{box}}^{(a)}(\mathbf{z})+
      \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{box}}^{(b)}(\mathbf{z})\right).
\end{taggedeqn}
The current inverse does not use an explicit
$\sigma_Y$ anchor prior; uncertainty along the HB ridge is instead
reported through multi-start dispersion and a local Laplace Hessian.

simulation-in-the-loop formulation.

\subsection{Input and Output Representation}
\label{sec:repr}

Each surrogate query uses the five-dimensional physical input
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x}=(n,\eta,\sigma_Y,W,H)^\top ,
\end{equation}
with logarithms applied to $\eta$ and $\sigma_Y$ before standardisation,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:xscale}
    \tilde{\mathbf{x}}
    =
    \frac{\mathbf{x}_{\log}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_X}
         {\boldsymbol{\sigma}_X},
    \qquad
    \mathbf{x}_{\log}
    =
    (n,\log\eta,\log\sigma_Y,W,H)^\top .
\end{equation}
The output is the eight-frame flow-front vector
$\mathbf{y}\in\mathbb{R}^{8}$; the output scaler subtracts a frozen
training mean,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:yscale}
    \tilde{\mathbf{y}}=\mathbf{y}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_Y .
\end{equation}
At inverse time, the observed vector
$\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$ is used both as likelihood data and as a
routing signal.

\subsection{Hierarchical Mixture-of-GP Surrogate}
\label{sec:surrogate}

\paragraph{Three-Layer Deterministic Partition.}
\label{sec:surr-3layer}
The forward map is partitioned into $G$ geometry-conditioned
sub-banks, each holding a finite set of locally fitted GP experts. A
query is routed by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gate}
    (W,H,\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}})
    \xrightarrow{L_1} g^\star
    \xrightarrow{L_2} b^\star
    \xrightarrow{L_3} s^\star .
\end{equation}
Layer 1 assigns the setup geometry to one of the $G$ geometry
indices,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:layer1}
    g^\star = G_\Pi(W,H), \qquad g^\star\in\{1,\ldots,G\},
\end{equation}
where $G_\Pi$ is a fixed nearest-cell map on the calibrated $(W,H)$
training grid.
Within the selected geometry sub-bank, Layer 2 places the terminal
flow displacement $y_8$ into the
stored length bin,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:layer2}
    b^\star
    =
    \mathrm{bin}\!\left(y_{\mathrm{obs},8};
    \{e_j^{(g^\star)}\}\right),
\end{equation}
clamping to the nearest bin outside the empirical range. Layer 3 uses
the normalised flow-shape feature
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:phi}
    \boldsymbol{\phi}(\mathbf{y})
    =
    \frac{(y_2-y_1,\ldots,y_8-y_1)^\top}{\max(y_8,\epsilon)}
\end{equation}
standardised by the bin-specific scaler, projected by the bin-specific
PCA, and assigned to the nearest K-means centroid,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:layer3}
    s^\star
    =
    \arg\min_s
    \left\|
      \mathrm{PCA}_{g^\star,b^\star}(\boldsymbol{\phi})
      -\mathbf{c}_{g^\star,b^\star,s}
    \right\|_2 .
\end{equation}
The final stage is an observation-evidence rerank. Among the
PCA-nearest candidate sub-experts $\{s\}$, we score each candidate by
how well its training output cloud explains the measured
$\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$, then keep a single primary sub-expert.
The implementation exposes two rerank profiles; the evaluation fixes
the profile by observation source rather than learning an automatic
source classifier.

\paragraph{\texttt{real\_robust} (camera deployment).}
Real videos carry non-trivial extraction noise and model discrepancy,
especially in the early frames. The robust profile therefore uses only
the terminal displacement $y_{\mathrm{obs},8}$, the most stable scalar
summary of the observed trajectory. Approximating the training $y_8$
values inside sub $s$ as Gaussian, the negative log marginal
likelihood of $y_{\mathrm{obs},8}$ under sub $s$ collapses to the
one-dimensional standardised distance
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:y8q}
    s_{\mathrm{real}}
    =
    \arg\min_s
    \frac{|y_{\mathrm{obs},8}-\mathrm{median}(Y_{s,8})|}
         {\max(\mathrm{std}(Y_{s,8}),\,0.1)} ,
\end{equation}
which is the standard one-dimensional case of Bayesian model
selection by marginal evidence~\cite{murphy2012machine,mackay2003information}.

\paragraph{\texttt{sim\_precision} (synthetic validation).}
When $\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$ is generated by the same MPM simulator
as the surrogate training corpus, all eight trajectory components are
valid simulator evidence. The simulator-matched profile therefore
chooses the sub-expert whose training output cloud contains the nearest
frame-weighted trajectory to $\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:simprecision}
    s_{\mathrm{sim}}
    =
    \arg\min_s\;
    \min_{i\in s}
    \sqrt{
      \frac{1}{8}
      \sum_{d=1}^{8}
      \alpha_d^2
      \bigl(Y_{s,i,d}-y_{\mathrm{obs},d}\bigr)^2
    },
\end{equation}
where $\{Y_{s,i,\cdot}\}_i$ are the trajectories assigned to sub $s$
and $\alpha_d$ are the frame weights used by the inverse loss
(\S\ref{sec:inv-nn}; Tab.~\ref{tab:hyper}). Each profile returns one
primary sub-expert
$s_{\mathrm{prod}}\in\{s_{\mathrm{real}},s_{\mathrm{sim}}\}$. The
two-setup path keeps the first setup as a top-$1$ anchor and treats
the routed setup-$2$ candidates as discrete joint hypotheses; the
profile controls only the routing score, the number $M$ of setup-$2$
hypotheses, and the hypothesis selection rule
(\texttt{sim\_precision} with $M=3$ and a first-observation WRMS
guard for \S\ref{sec:mainresults}; \texttt{real\_robust} with $M=10$
and conservative selection for \S\ref{sec:rheometer}).
Routing on observed $(W,H,\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}})$ rather than the
unknown $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ avoids a circular gate and makes
out-of-support cases diagnosable by inspecting the selected sub's
training box.

\paragraph{Per-Cell Gaussian-Process Experts.}
\label{sec:surr-experts}
Each sub-expert is a tuple of eight independent exact GP regressors
sharing the same scaled $5$-D input and using a Mat\'ern-$5/2$ ARD
kernel,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:matern}
    k_{5/2}(\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b})
    =
    \sigma_f^2
    \left(1+\sqrt{5}r+\tfrac{5}{3}r^2\right)
    e^{-\sqrt{5}r},
    \quad
    r^2=\sum_{d=1}^{5}\frac{(a_d-b_d)^2}{\ell_d^2}.
\end{equation}
For output frame $d$, the posterior mean and variance are
\begin{align}
\label{eq:gp-predictive}
    \mu_{s,d}(\tilde{\mathbf{x}})
    &=
    \mathbf{k}_*^\top
    (K_s+\sigma_{n,s,d}^2 I)^{-1}\tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{s,d},
\nonumber\\
    \sigma_{s,d}^2(\tilde{\mathbf{x}})
    &=
    k_{5/2}(\tilde{\mathbf{x}},\tilde{\mathbf{x}})
    -
    \mathbf{k}_*^\top
    (K_s+\sigma_{n,s,d}^2 I)^{-1}\mathbf{k}_* .
\end{align}
Hyperparameters are fitted by maximising the exact GP marginal
likelihood,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gp-mll}
\begin{aligned}
    \log p(\tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{s,d}\mid X_s)
    =
    &-\tfrac{1}{2}
      \tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{s,d}^{\top}
      (K_s+\sigma_{n,s,d}^2I)^{-1}
      \tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{s,d}
      -\tfrac{1}{2}\log|K_s+\sigma_{n,s,d}^2I| \\
    &-\tfrac{|X_s|}{2}\log 2\pi .
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Top-1 Prediction.}
\label{sec:surr-top1}
The predictor uses the routed primary sub directly,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:top1}
    \tilde{\mathcal{S}}(\boldsymbol{\theta};W,H)
    =
    \mu_{s_{\mathrm{prod}}}(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}).
\end{equation}
Because the deterministic gate and profile-specific rerank of
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:gate}--\eqref{eq:simprecision} already select a single
locally well-supported expert, no inter-expert aggregation is needed:
top-$K$ ensembling on top of routing only adds variance from regions
the gate has already excluded.

\paragraph{Monotonic Post-Processing.}
\label{sec:surr-mono}
The physical flow-front distance is non-decreasing, so the decoded GP
mean is monotonised by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mono}
    \hat{y}_d\leftarrow \max_{j\le d}\hat{y}_j,
    \qquad d=1,\ldots,8 .
\end{equation}

\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
\caption{Offline construction of the trajectory-shape GP bank.}
\label{alg:train}
\KwData{MPM corpus $\{(\boldsymbol{\theta}_i,W_i,H_i,\mathbf{y}_i)\}$.}
\KwOut{Frozen bank of $25$ geometry-conditioned sub-banks, each
holding scalers, route metadata, sub assignments, and per-sub
exact-GP regressors.}
\BlankLine
Fit global input/output scalers (Eqs.~\eqref{eq:xscale}, \eqref{eq:yscale})\;
\ForEach{geometry sub-bank $g$}{
  build length bins from $y_8$ and, within each bin, PCA/K-means
  shape clusters from Eq.~\eqref{eq:phi}\;
  merge undersized clusters and freeze the assignment table\;
  \ForEach{sub $s$}{
    train eight independent exact-GP regressors (one per frame) by
    Eq.~\eqref{eq:gp-mll}\;
    store training inputs/outputs, validation error, the per-sub
    summary statistics $\mathrm{median}(Y_{s,8})$, $\mathrm{std}(Y_{s,8})$,
    and the assigned trajectories used by the source-aware rerank\;
  }
}
\Return{frozen surrogate bank.}
\end{algorithm}

The online forward pass is therefore: route
$(W,H,\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}})$ through
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:gate}--\eqref{eq:simprecision} to select
$s_{\mathrm{prod}}$, standardise the input by Eq.~\eqref{eq:xscale},
evaluate the selected ExactGP via Eq.~\eqref{eq:gp-predictive}, decode
the mean to physical units, and apply the monotonic guard of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:mono}. The GP also returns a predictive standard
deviation that the inverse loss of \S\ref{sec:inv-loss} consumes.

\subsection{GP-Aware Inverse}
\label{sec:inverse}

\paragraph{Within-Sub Warm-Start.}
\label{sec:inv-nn}
CMA-ES is initialised at the routed sub's nearest training point in
observation space,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:nn}
    \mathbf{z}^{\mathrm{NN}}
    =
    \mathbf{z}_{i^\star},\quad
    i^\star
    =
    \arg\min_{i\in\mathcal{D}_{s_{\mathrm{prod}}}}
    \frac{1}{8}\sum_{d=1}^{8}
    \alpha_d^2\left(y_{i,d}-y_{\mathrm{obs},d}\right)^2 .
\end{equation}
The frame weights form the frozen non-decreasing schedule
\[
\boldsymbol{\alpha}=(0.2,0.2,0.35,0.5,0.8,1.0,1.4,2.0).
\]
By construction, the early frames of the dam-break sequence include
the gate-release transient
(finite-time gate withdrawal, release-timing jitter, transient
free-surface and surface-tension effects unmodelled by the MPM
forward), while the late frames have settled into the steady
spread regime where the surrogate is faithful. Up-weighting late
frames aligns the residual with the regime in which the
forward model is trusted. The specific schedule used in our
experiments is a frozen hyperparameter listed in
\S\ref{sec:data} (Tab.~\ref{tab:hyper}).

\paragraph{Per-Sub Bound Tightening.}
\label{sec:inv-box}
The search box is the support of the routed sub-expert,
$[\mathbf{z}_{\min},\mathbf{z}_{\max}]$, padded in $z$-space by a
fraction $p$ of its standard deviation $\mathbf{s}$ and clipped to
the global HB bounds,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tight-box}
    \Theta_{k^\star}
    =
    \Theta\cap
    [\mathbf{z}_{\min}-p\mathbf{s},\,
     \mathbf{z}_{\max}+p\mathbf{s}].
\end{equation}
The objective adds a soft quadratic penalty with weight
$p_{\mathrm{sup}}$ outside the unpadded support, so the optimiser is
not forced to honour the bounds exactly when the sub's training data
is sparse near a face. Numerical values for $p$ and
$p_{\mathrm{sup}}$ are listed in \S\ref{sec:data}.

\paragraph{Ridge Diagnosis Without an Explicit $\sigma_Y$ Prior.}
\label{sec:inv-sy}
Rather than break the residual $(\eta,\sigma_Y)$ identifiability
ridge with a hand-chosen prior, we expose it through CMA-ES restart
dispersion and the local Laplace--Hessian confidence interval
(\S\ref{sec:inv-cma}), tying reported uncertainty to the likelihood
rather than to a user-selected anchor.

\paragraph{GP-Aware Type-II ML Loss.}
\label{sec:inv-loss}
For a candidate $\mathbf{z}$, the selected GP provides both
$\hat{y}_d(\mathbf{z})$ and predictive standard deviation
$\sigma_{\mathrm{GP},d}(\mathbf{z})$. We work in log-space and convolve
the GP epistemic variance, propagated by the delta method, with a
homoscedastic observation-noise floor
$\sigma_{\mathrm{obs,log}}^2$ that absorbs the residual real--sim
discrepancy:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gp-var}
    v_d(\mathbf{z})
    =
    \left(
    \frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{GP},d}(\mathbf{z})}
         {\max(\hat{y}_d(\mathbf{z}),\epsilon)}
    \right)^2
    + \sigma_{\mathrm{obs,log}}^2 .
\end{equation}
The per-setup negative log marginal likelihood is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:obs-loss}
\begin{aligned}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{GP}}(\mathbf{z})
    &=
    \frac{1}{8}\sum_{d=1}^{8}
    \left[
      \frac{\alpha_d^2\, r_d(\mathbf{z})^2}{2v_d(\mathbf{z})}
      +\frac{1}{2}\log v_d(\mathbf{z})
    \right],\\
    r_d&=\log\hat{y}_d-\log y_{\mathrm{obs},d}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The log-variance term is the Occam factor: it prevents the optimiser
from preferring regions only because the GP predictive variance is
large there. The noise floor $\sigma_{\mathrm{obs,log}}$ is held
fixed across all reported experiments rather than tuned per
material; its specific value is reported in \S\ref{sec:data}.

\paragraph{Optimiser and Uncertainty Output.}
\label{sec:inv-cma}
We minimise Eq.~\eqref{eq:Pprime} with multi-restart
CMA-ES~\cite{hansen2016cma}, whose adaptive sampling distribution
suits the piecewise-smooth, ridge-prone surface produced by
Eq.~\eqref{eq:obs-loss}. Each restart is seeded deterministically;
the lowest-loss restart is reported as the MAP, the standard
deviation across restarts is a truth-blind ridge diagnostic, and a
finite-difference Hessian at the MAP yields a Laplace 95\% interval.
Each CMA-ES generation is evaluated as a single batched GP forward
call (Eq.~\eqref{eq:gp-predictive} on a stacked
$\mathbf{X}_*$)~\cite{rasmussen2006gaussian}, amortising the GPU
dispatch overhead over $\lambda$ candidates without altering the
optimisation trajectory. Population size, restart count, and other
hyperparameters are listed in \S\ref{sec:data}.

\paragraph{Joint Two-Setup Inverse.}
\label{sec:inv-joint}
For two observations of the same material, the routed setups retain
their own GP experts but share one $\boldsymbol{\theta}$. The
implementation minimises Eq.~\eqref{eq:Pstar} over the
intersection of the two padded sub boxes (or their padded union when
disjoint), warm-started from the single-setup optimum so that the
second observation adds information \emph{on top of} the first
posterior rather than restarting the search.

\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
\caption{Online inverse (single-setup, or joint when a second setup
$(\mathbf{y}^{(b)},W_b,H_b)$ is supplied).}
\label{alg:invert}
\KwIn{$(\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}},W,H)$ and optionally a second
$(\mathbf{y}^{(b)},W_b,H_b)$; frozen surrogate bank.}
\KwOut{$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ and posterior diagnostics.}
\BlankLine
Route each supplied setup to its primary sub using the deployment
profile (Eqs.~\eqref{eq:y8q}, \eqref{eq:simprecision})\;
construct the padded search box (Eq.~\eqref{eq:tight-box}; for two
setups, the intersection of the per-sub boxes, or a padded union
when they are disjoint) and the nearest-neighbour warm start
(Eq.~\eqref{eq:nn}) on the first setup\;
run multi-restart CMA-ES on Eq.~\eqref{eq:Pprime} (single-setup) or
Eq.~\eqref{eq:Pstar} (joint, warm-started from the single-setup
optimum)\;
optionally evaluate the Laplace Hessian interval at the best
point\;
\Return{$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ and diagnostics.}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Hessian-Orthogonal Setup-2 Proposal (Inherited)}
\label{sec:proposer}

The choice of $(W_2,H_2)$ is not part of our contribution: we adopt
the closed-form, Fisher-information-based proposer of
\citet{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} verbatim. Given the single-setup
estimate $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_1$, they approximate the dam
break by plane-Poiseuille flow and derive an analytical
loss-Hessian $\mathbf{H}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_1;W,H)$. Let
$\mathbf{q}_1$ be the unit eigenvector associated with the smallest
eigenvalue of $\mathbf{H}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_1;W_1,H_1)$---the
direction in $\boldsymbol{\theta}$-space along which the first setup
carries the least information. The proposer selects
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:proposer}
    (W_2^\star,H_2^\star)
    \;=\;
    \arg\min_{(W,H)\in\mathcal{W}}
    \bigl\langle \mathbf{q}_1,\,\mathbf{q}_2(W,H)\bigr\rangle^2 ,
\end{equation}
making the joint Fisher information full-rank in the
$(\mathbf{q}_1,\mathbf{q}_2)$ subspace. The Hessian is closed-form,
so evaluating Eq.~\eqref{eq:proposer} requires no forward
simulations. Our contribution is to make the \emph{two-setup} joint
estimate that consumes this proposer's output affordable per
material rather than per showcase; the joint information gain
depends on the residual ridge geometry of each material and is
most pronounced on the rheometer-referenced real materials of
\S\ref{sec:rheometer} (consistency parameter, ridge tightening),
while on the synthetic $N=30$ Hamamichi-matched panel of
\S\ref{sec:mainresults} our replay-consistency guard keeps the
joint update from regressing the single-setup recovery
(Tab.~\ref{tab:setup-ablation}). The cascaded $3$- and $4$-setup
refinements that the predecessor reports---each run only on the
cases that failed at the previous setup---are a natural extension
of the same proposer and surrogate but are not implemented in this
paper.

\section{Camera Calibration}
\label{sec:calib}

To run the inverse on a real experiment, we need to convert a
smartphone video of the dam-break flow into the observation vector
$\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}} = (x_{1}, \dots, x_{8}) \in
\mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}^{8}$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:y-def}. We use a two-stage
camera calibration followed by silhouette-based flow-front
extraction; the full pipeline is summarised in
Algorithm~\ref{alg:calib}.

\paragraph{Stage 1: ChArUco prior.}
A printed ChArUco
board~\cite{garrido2014automatic,garrido2016generation} is placed in
the experimental scene alongside the fluid container. The standard
pinhole projection~\cite{hartley2004multiple} sends a world point
$\mathbf{P}_{w}$ to pixels via
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pinhole}
    \mathbf{P}_{c} \;=\; \mathbf{R}\,\mathbf{P}_{w} + \mathbf{t},
    \qquad
    \mathbf{p} \;=\; \tfrac{1}{Z_{c}}\,\mathbf{K}\,\mathbf{P}_{c},
    \quad
    \mathbf{K} = \begin{pmatrix}
          f_{x} & 0     & c_{x} \\
          0     & f_{y} & c_{y} \\
          0     & 0     & 1
        \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
Rather than optimising $(K, R, \mathbf{t})$ as $11$ free scalars, we
exploit the fact that our rig is level and the camera looks at a
fixed target, and parameterise the whole camera by a compact
$6$-vector
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:theta-cam}
    \boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{cam}}
    \;=\;
    (l_{x},\,l_{z},\,\theta_{E},\,\phi_{E},\,
    \mathrm{fov},\,d_{\mathrm{eye}}),
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{l} = (l_{x}, 0, l_{z})^{\!\top}$ is the look-at point
on the rig floor, $(\theta_{E}, \phi_{E})$ are the polar and
azimuthal angles of the eye direction, $d_{\mathrm{eye}}$ is the
eye--target distance, and $\mathrm{fov}$ is the vertical field of
view. The camera roll is held fixed by construction (the rig is
level and the renderer enforces a horizontal $\mathbf{C}_{x}$ axis,
Eq.~\eqref{eq:basis}). The eye position and
an orthonormal camera basis are then reconstructed deterministically
from $\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{cam}}$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:eye}
    \mathbf{E}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{cam}})
    \;=\; d_{\mathrm{eye}}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \sin\theta_{E}\cos\phi_{E} \\
        \cos\theta_{E} \\
        -\sin\theta_{E}\sin\phi_{E}
    \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:basis}
    \mathbf{C}_{z} = \frac{\mathbf{E} - \mathbf{l}}
                           {\|\mathbf{E} - \mathbf{l}\|},
    \quad
    \mathbf{C}_{x} = \frac{(C_{z,3},\,0,\,-C_{z,1})^{\!\top}}
                           {\|(C_{z,3},\,0,\,-C_{z,1})\|},
    \quad
    \mathbf{C}_{y} = \mathbf{C}_{z} \times \mathbf{C}_{x},
\end{equation}
so that $R = [\mathbf{C}_{x}\,|\,\mathbf{C}_{y}\,|\,\mathbf{C}_{z}]^{\!\top}$ and
$\mathbf{t} = -R\,\mathbf{E}$; the $\mathbf{C}_{x}$ construction
enforces a horizontal camera up-axis. A world point $\mathbf{P}_{i}$
thus maps first to camera coordinates
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:screen}
    \mathbf{Y}_{i}
    \;=\;
    \bigl(
      (\mathbf{P}_{i} - \mathbf{E})\!\cdot\!\mathbf{C}_{x},\;
      (\mathbf{P}_{i} - \mathbf{E})\!\cdot\!\mathbf{C}_{y},\;
      (\mathbf{P}_{i} - \mathbf{E})\!\cdot\!\mathbf{C}_{z}
    \bigr),
\end{equation}
and then to pixels by perspective division. Matching the renderer's
vertical-field-of-view convention $f_{y} = H/(2\tan(\mathrm{fov}/2))$
gives the scale factor
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:scale}
    s \;=\; \frac{1}{2\,\tan(\mathrm{fov}/2)},
\end{equation}
and the full reprojection of $\mathbf{P}_{i}$ into the image plane
$[0, W]\times[0, H]$ reads
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:project}
    \pi(\mathbf{P}_{i};\,\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{cam}})
    \;=\;
    \left(
        \frac{W}{2} + s\,H\,\frac{[\mathbf{Y}_{i}]_{1}}{|[\mathbf{Y}_{i}]_{3}|},\;
        \frac{H}{2} - s\,H\,\frac{[\mathbf{Y}_{i}]_{2}}{|[\mathbf{Y}_{i}]_{3}|}
    \right).
\end{equation}

After sub-pixel corner refinement~\cite{forstner1987fast} on the
detected ChArUco board, Stage~1 solves
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:stage1}
    \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\mathrm{cam}}
    \;=\;
    \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{cam}}}\,
    \frac{1}{|\mathcal{I}|}
    \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}}
    \rho_{\delta}\!\bigl(
      \|\mathbf{u}_{i} - \pi(\mathbf{P}_{i};\,\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{cam}})\|
    \bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{I}$ indexes detected corners with pixel observations
$\mathbf{u}_{i}$ and known board coordinates $\mathbf{P}_{i}$, and
$\rho_{\delta}$ is the Huber loss with a $\delta = 2$\,px threshold,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:huber}
    \rho_{\delta}(e)
    \;=\;
    \begin{cases}
        \tfrac{1}{2}e^{2}, & e < \delta, \\
        \delta\bigl(e - \tfrac{1}{2}\delta\bigr), & e \ge \delta,
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
which prevents any single mis-refined corner from dominating the fit.
Eq.~\eqref{eq:stage1} is minimised with
Nelder--Mead~\cite{nelder1965simplex}, returning
$\mathbf{K}_{0}$ (from $\mathrm{fov}$ and image size) and an initial
extrinsic $(R_{0}, \mathbf{t}_{0})$.

\paragraph{Stage 2: silhouette-guided extrinsic refinement.}
Stage~1 is typically accurate to a few pixels but the ChArUco board
is not rigidly coupled to the container; Stage~2 fixes
$\mathbf{K}_{0}$ and refines only the extrinsic so that the rendered
container silhouette aligns with the reference frame $I_{0}$ at dam
release, in two phases.

\emph{Phase~A (smooth chamfer).}
The Canny edge set~\cite{canny1986computational} of $I_{0}$ yields a
target-edge pixel set $E$, from which we precompute an exact
Euclidean distance
transform~\cite{maurer2003linear,borgefors1986distance}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:edt}
    D_{E}(p) \;=\;
    \min_{q \in E}\,\|\,p - q\,\|_{2}
    \qquad \text{for each pixel } p \in \Omega.
\end{equation}
For candidate extrinsics $(R, \mathbf{t})$, let $P_{f}(R, \mathbf{t})$
denote the projected edge pixels of the $f$-th visible cube face
and $\mathcal{F}$ the set of visible faces. The per-face-normalised
weighted chamfer cost is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:chamfer}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cham}}(R, \mathbf{t})
    \;=\;
    \frac{1}{|\mathcal{F}|}
    \sum_{f \in \mathcal{F}}
    \frac{\sum_{p \in P_{f}(R,\mathbf{t})}\, w(p)\, D_{E}(p)}
         {\sum_{p \in P_{f}(R,\mathbf{t})}\, w(p)},
\end{equation}
with $D_{E}(p)$ bilinearly interpolated from the EDT and
$w(p)$ a product of three side-emphasis weights
$w_{\mathrm{left}}(p_{x})$, $w_{\mathrm{top}}(p_{y})$, and
$w_{\mathrm{bottom}}(p_{y})$.
Per-face normalisation prevents long edges (e.g.\ the bottom wall)
from dominating short ones. We minimise $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cham}}$
with L-BFGS-B~\cite{byrd1995limited,zhu1997algorithm} over the six
DoF of a Rodrigues rotation vector~\cite{rodrigues1840lois} and a
translation, box-clamped to $\pm 2.5^{\circ}$ and $\pm 2$\,cm around
the Stage-$1$ prior $(\mathbf{r}_{0}, \mathbf{t}_{0})$.

\emph{Phase~B (non-smooth IoU).}
Starting from the Phase-$A$ solution, we maximise a weighted Jaccard
similarity / pixel-IoU~\cite{jaccard1912distribution} between the
rendered silhouette $M(R, \mathbf{t})$ and the target foreground $T$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:iou}
    \mathrm{IoU}_{w}(R, \mathbf{t})
    \;=\;
    \frac{\sum_{p}\, w(p)\,[\,M(p)\wedge T(p)\,]}
         {\sum_{p}\, w(p)\,[\,M(p)\vee T(p)\,]},
\end{equation}
with an adaptive Nelder--Mead~\cite{gao2012implementing} simplex of
size $0.15^{\circ}/1.5$\,mm. Because $\mathrm{IoU}_{w}$ is
discretised on the pixel grid it is non-differentiable; the
L-BFGS-B phase on the smooth chamfer cost provides the warm start
needed to avoid the combinatorial local minima of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:iou}.

\paragraph{Safety clamp.}
If $\|\mathbf{r}^{*} - \mathbf{r}_{0}\|$ or
$\|\mathbf{t}^{*} - \mathbf{t}_{0}\|$ exceed the safety thresholds
($3^{\circ}$ or $3$\,cm; loosened past the Phase-A box so the
unbounded Phase-B can move beyond it for mild excursions), we fall
back to the ChArUco extrinsic, guarding against strong local minima
when the ChArUco board placement is sloppy.

\paragraph{Flow-front extraction.}
Each subsequent frame $I_{1}, \dots, I_{8}$ captured at
$t_{1}, \dots, t_{8}$
is binarised by HSV-intensity thresholding (threshold $128$, black
foreground by default) followed by morphological opening and closing
on a $3\times 3$ kernel~\cite{gonzalez2018digital}; only the largest
connected foreground component is retained to reject spurious
specks. We then extract the outer contour $\partial\Omega_{i}$ of
the foreground mask at frame $i$ and back-project every contour
pixel $\mathbf{u} = (u, v)$ to the world ground plane $y = 0$
through the calibrated pinhole camera. With
$\mathbf{C}_{w} = -R^{\!\top}\,\mathbf{t}$ the world-frame camera
centre and
$\mathbf{d}(\mathbf{u}) = R^{\!\top}\,\mathbf{K}_{0}^{-1}\,(u, v, 1)^{\!\top}$
the world-frame ray direction, the ray
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:backproj}
    \mathbf{P}_{w}(\tau)
    \;=\; \mathbf{C}_{w} + \tau\,\mathbf{d}(\mathbf{u})
\end{equation}
meets the floor at
$\tau^{\star} = -[\mathbf{C}_{w}]_{y}/[\mathbf{d}(\mathbf{u})]_{y}$.
Let $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^{2}$ be the unit flow direction in
the world $xz$-plane ($\mathbf{v} = (1, 0)$ for our apparatus), and
let $x^{\mathrm{front}}_{\mathrm{box}}$ be the container's
front-wall position read from the experiment manifest. The flow
distance at frame $t_{i}$ is the farthest signed projection of
$\partial\Omega_{i}$ onto $\mathbf{v}$ relative to that front wall,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:xi}
    x_{i} \;=\;
    \max_{\mathbf{u} \in \partial\Omega_{i}}
    \Bigl[\mathbf{v}^{\!\top}
          \bigl(\mathbf{P}_{w}(\tau^{\star}(\mathbf{u}))\bigr)_{xz}\Bigr]
    \;-\; x^{\mathrm{front}}_{\mathrm{box}}.
\end{equation}
Finally, residual binarisation noise can introduce occasional
backward jumps across frames; we guard against this with a monotone
pass $x_{i} \leftarrow \max(x_{i}, x_{i-1})$ (equivalent to
Eq.~\eqref{eq:mono} applied in physical space), producing the
observation
$\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}} = (x_{1}, \dots, x_{8})
\in \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}^{8}$ that the inverse of
Algorithm~\ref{alg:invert} consumes.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experimental-setup}

We evaluate the hierarchical mixture-of-GP surrogate (HMoGP) and the
identifiability-aware inverse
against five questions: (1) how faithfully does the surrogate
approximate the MPM forward model, (2) how accurately does the full
inverse recover $\boldsymbol{\theta} = (\sigma_Y, \eta, n)$ on
held-out samples, (3) what wall-clock reduction does the surrogate
deliver relative to simulation-in-the-loop inversion, and (4) does
the fast inverse remain consistent with independent rheometer
measurements on the Hamamichi materials for which such references are
available, and (5) can seconds-scale inversion recover a
time-varying yield-stress trajectory from repeated within-batch
dam-break measurements?

\paragraph{Datasets.}
We use four evaluation sets. \emph{$T_{\mathrm{sim}}$} is a disjoint held-out evaluation split of $22{,}209$ unique MPM simulations, separate from the $295{,}419$-simulation training corpus (\S\ref{sec:data}),
sampled stratified over $(n, \log\eta, \log\sigma_Y, W, H)$. After
the multi-cell routing of \S\ref{sec:surrogate}, this corresponds
to the $42{,}583$ assignment rows used for the surrogate parity
and calibration diagnostics of~\S\ref{sec:fidelity}.
\emph{$T_{\mathrm{inv}}$} is a
synthetic benchmark of $30$ materials drawn from within the training
box, each rendered via MPM at three container sizes (total $90$
clips). \emph{$T_{\mathrm{real}}$} contains the six Hamamichi materials for
which parallel-plate rheometer sweeps are available.
\emph{$T_{\mathrm{fresh}}$} follows the freshness protocol of
\S\ref{sec:freshness}:
synthetic prescribed kinetic trajectories for ground-truth recovery,
six real hand-prepared starch or composite-starch conditions sampled
within a single $60$\,min batch window, and a repeat batch for
quantifying cross-batch kinetic drift.

\paragraph{Reference.}
The accuracy and time reference is the simulation-in-the-loop
pipeline of~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}: identical dam-break
observation and CMA-ES-style derivative-free search, but every
candidate is evaluated by a fresh MPM run. The predecessor's own
$30$-case panel numbers are reproduced alongside ours in
Table~\ref{tab:setup-ablation}. The contribution of each component
of our pipeline is isolated by the surrogate-fidelity diagnostics
of \S\ref{sec:fidelity}, which establish that the routed sub-experts
reproduce the MPM forward to sub-cm flow-front error, and by the
routing recall reported inline in \S\ref{sec:mainresults}: the
simulator-matched profile recovers the oracle sub-expert in
$61/100$ cases at top-$1$, $97/100$ at top-$3$, and $100/100$ at
top-$10$.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
For the forward surrogate we report per-output mean absolute error
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mae}
    \mathrm{MAE}(\hat{\mathbf{y}}, \mathbf{y})
    \;=\; \frac{1}{D_Y}\sum_{d=1}^{D_Y}|\hat y_d - y_d|,
\end{equation}
coefficient of determination $R^{2} = 1 - \sum_{i}
\|\hat{\mathbf{y}}_i - \mathbf{y}_i\|^2 / \sum_i \|\mathbf{y}_i -
\bar{\mathbf{y}}\|^2$, and the row-max error distribution
($p_{50}, p_{95}, p_{99}, p_{999}$). For the inverse we report
per-parameter relative error
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:relE}
    \mathrm{relE}(\hat\theta_d;\,\theta_d^{\mathrm{gt}})
    \;=\; \frac{|\hat\theta_d - \theta_d^{\mathrm{gt}}|}
               {|\theta_d^{\mathrm{gt}}|},
    \qquad d \in \{n, \eta, \sigma_Y\},
\end{equation}
the forward NMSE at the recovered parameter as a sanity check on
fit, and the per-sample wall-clock. For comparisons against
parallel-plate reference sweeps (\S\ref{sec:rheometer}) we further
report the integrated log-stress error over the experimentally valid
shear-rate interval $\Gamma = [\dot\gamma_{\min}, \dot\gamma_{\max}]$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:E}
    \mathcal{E}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}, \boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{gt}})
    \;=\;
    \left(\frac{1}{|\Gamma|}
    \int_{\Gamma}
      \bigl[\log\hat\tau(\dot\gamma)
          - \log\tau_{\mathrm{gt}}(\dot\gamma)\bigr]^{2}
    \,d\log\dot\gamma\right)^{\!1/2}.
\end{equation}
All inverse runs use the frozen hyperparameters of
Table~\ref{tab:hyper} and a fixed seed.


\subsection{Surrogate Fidelity}
\label{sec:fidelity}

We first verify that the trained surrogate predicts MPM outputs
accurately enough to justify replacing the simulator inside the
inverse. The held-out test split ($T_{\mathrm{sim}}$,
$42{,}583$ rows) is a strict $10\,\%$ slice of the
training corpus, with each row carrying its assignment to a unique
sub-expert (the assignment used at training time, not seen by that
sub during fitting). For each test row we forward the assigned
sub's exact GP at the row's true
$\boldsymbol{\theta}=(n,\eta,\sigma_Y)$ and setup geometry $(W,H)$,
and compare the prediction $\hat{\mathbf{y}}$ to the MPM ground
truth $\mathbf{y}$. The full $42{,}583$-row evaluation visits all
$25$ geometry sub-banks and all $1{,}491$ sub-experts.

\paragraph{Aggregate fidelity.}
Across all eight flow-front dimensions and all $1{,}491$ sub-experts,
the surrogate attains aggregate MAE
$\mathrm{MAE}=0.038$\,cm and $R^2 = 0.9989$. The median row-max
absolute error is $0.029$\,cm, the $p_{95}$ row-max is $0.26$\,cm,
the $p_{99}$ row-max is $0.58$\,cm, and the $p_{99.9}$ row-max is
$1.92$\,cm. The per-dim MAE rises mildly from $0.005$\,cm at the
first frame to $0.064$\,cm at the eighth (Tab.~\ref{tab:fidelity}),
reflecting the larger dynamic range of later flow-front distances;
the per-dim $R^2$ stays above $0.996$ across all eight frames, with
the small drop at $d=8$ ($R^2 = 0.9967$) tracking a thin extrapolation
tail in a handful of subs (the absolute worst row sits at $29$\,cm
displacement in $\hat y_8$, single-figure occurrence over $42{,}583$
rows). Such tail rows do not arise inside the inverse loop: the
GP-aware likelihood (\S\ref{sec:inv-loss}) down-weights any
residual that exceeds the GP variance, and the top-$k$ rerank
(\S\ref{sec:surr-top1}) routes such queries away from boundary
subs.  Fig.~\ref{fig:parity} shows the per-frame surrogate parity
that supports these aggregate numbers.

Across the $1{,}484$ sub-experts with at least five held-out rows, the

$0.965$. Even these boundary or low-sample subs stay below
$0.5$\,cm held-out MAE; the few lower outliers are low-sample

explicitly near-boundary queries.

\paragraph{Posterior-uncertainty calibration.}
Because the inverse loop weights residuals by $\sigma_{\mathrm{pred}}$
(\S\ref{sec:inv-loss}, the GP-aware likelihood), we also check
that the per-row predictive variance is meaningful. Binning the
$42{,}583\times 8$ residuals by predicted $\sigma_{\mathrm{pred}}$
and comparing the empirical RMSE in each bin against the bin's mean
$\sigma_{\mathrm{pred}}$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:sigma-cal}, left) shows that
empirical RMSE rises monotonically with $\sigma_{\mathrm{pred}}$ but
sits a factor of $\sim 2$ \emph{below} the diagonal. The
distribution of standardised residuals
$z = (\hat y - y)/\sigma_{\mathrm{pred}}$ has empirical standard
deviation $0.52$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:sigma-cal}, right), confirming that
the GP posterior is conservative by roughly a factor of two on the
held-out set. We deliberately keep this conservative scale rather
than rescaling: at deployment the residual to be explained is the
real-vs-MPM gap, not the MPM-vs-GP gap measured here, so an
inflated $\sigma$ at the surrogate level acts as the correct
``unknown unknowns'' budget when the likelihood is evaluated against
real-camera observations.

Tab.~\ref{tab:fidelity} therefore establishes that the routed
sub-expert reproduces MPM forward outputs to well within the
flow-front extraction noise of the captured data
(\S\ref{sec:obs-pipeline}), and Fig.~\ref{fig:sigma-cal} shows
that the GP's predictive $\sigma$ supplies a meaningful, slightly
conservative noise budget for the inverse likelihood. Together
these justify substituting MPM by the surrogate inside the
inverse loop.


\subsection{Synthetic Validation at Scale}
\label{sec:mainresults}

Following the validation protocol
of~\citet{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}, we first test the inverse on
synthetic dam-break observations whose ground-truth parameters are
known by construction. We run the full pipeline (routing, GP-aware
likelihood, multi-restart CMA-ES) on $N$ synthetic materials drawn
from the calibrated parameter box of \S\ref{sec:data}, and report
the relative error
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:erel}
\begin{aligned}
E_{\mathrm{rel}}
&= \Bigg[
\biggl(\frac{\hat n-n^{\!\star}}{n_{\max}-n_{\min}}\biggr)^{\!2}
+
\biggl(\frac{\hat\eta-\eta^{\!\star}}{\eta_{\max}-\eta_{\min}}\biggr)^{\!2}
\\[-1mm]
&\qquad+
\biggl(\frac{\hat\sigma_Y-\sigma_Y^{\!\star}}
{\sigma_{Y,\max}-\sigma_{Y,\min}}\biggr)^{\!2}
\Bigg]^{1/2}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
introduced by~\citet{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}, with the parameter
box of \S\ref{sec:data} setting the normalisation. Following their
protocol we declare a recovery successful when
$E_{\mathrm{rel}}\le 0.1$.

All synthetic panels in this section use the simulator-matched
\texttt{sim\_precision} profile of Eq.~\eqref{eq:simprecision}.
The real-video inverses later in the paper use the
\texttt{real\_robust} profile of Eq.~\eqref{eq:y8q}.  Both are
instances of the same top-$M$ candidate-hypothesis joint framework of
\S\ref{sec:surrogate}.  In the synthetic profile, setup-$1$ is a
top-$1$ local inverse, setup-$2$ keeps the top-$3$ full-trajectory
candidates, and the joint stage runs a tight-box per-pair search with
the first-observation guard of \S\ref{sec:inv-joint}.  This is
appropriate because the synthetic observations are generated by the
same MLS-MPM simulator as the training corpus.  In the real profile,
described in \S\ref{sec:rheometer}, setup-$2$ instead keeps top-$10$
terminal-displacement candidates and uses conservative selection for
camera noise and real--sim discrepancy.

A held-out $100$-row routing diagnostic confirmed the profile choice:
\texttt{sim\_precision} recovers the oracle sub-expert in $61/100$
cases at top-$1$, $97/100$ at top-$3$, and $100/100$ at top-$10$,
whereas the real-robust terminal-displacement score trades synthetic
recall for camera-noise robustness.
\paragraph{Hamamichi-matched synthetic panel.}
The simulation-in-the-loop predecessor reports its main synthetic
validation on $30$ cases ($6$ materials $\times\,5$ random initial
setups), reaching $11/30$ ($36.7\%$) at $1$~setup and $20/30$
($66.7\%$) at $2$~setups, with further \emph{cascaded} refinements
in which setup~$k+1$ is only run on the cases that failed at
setup~$k$, raising the cumulative pass rate to $26/30$ at $3$~setups
and $30/30$ at $4$~setups~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}. We
therefore make the same $30$-case scale the main comparison and
reserve larger panels for robustness checks. Our $30$~cases are
stratified samples from the held-out MPM test split rather than the
predecessor's specific $6$-material list; this differs in sampling
protocol but matches the panel size and the $E_{\mathrm{rel}}\le 0.1$
recovery threshold, so the numbers in Table~\ref{tab:setup-ablation}
answer ``does the inverse recover correctly at the predecessor's
panel scale?'' rather than ``do we beat the predecessor case by
case?''  A like-for-like rerun on Hamamichi's $6$~rheometer-fitted
parameter sets, and the cascaded $3$/$4$-setup protocol that the
predecessor uses to clear the remaining failures, are reserved
for future work.

For each of our cases we run a single-setup inverse, use the
Hessian-orthogonal proposer of \S\ref{sec:proposer} to choose
a second geometry, synthesise that second observation with MLS-MPM
at the ground-truth parameters, and run the simulator-matched
two-setup joint profile.  The joint stage keeps the top-$3$
setup-$2$ candidates (ranked by frame-weighted nearest-training
trajectory distance under \texttt{sim\_precision}), optimises each
candidate pair in its own tight sub-expert box, and accepts the
joint update only when it does not violate the first observation's
truth-blind replay consistency (\S\ref{sec:inv-joint}).  At
$1$~setup our pipeline converts $15/30$, ahead of the predecessor's
$11/30$; at $2$~setups we hold the same $15/30$ rather than slide
to the predecessor's $20/30$, because our truth-blind guard rejects
joint updates that would regress setup~$1$ replay---the
predecessor's unguarded multi-setup CMA-ES instead accepts those
updates and converts an additional $9$ cases.  This is a
documented trade-off, not a free win for either side: our guard
pays a panel-level conversion cost in exchange for never
overwriting a recovered single-setup estimate, which matters when
the inverse is used as a per-batch instrument rather than a
per-showcase study.
Fig.~\ref{fig:flow-error-cdf} reports the cumulative distributions
of parameter and forward replay error across the panel.

\paragraph{Flow-level replay.}
Following the predecessor's simulated-flow diagnostics, we replay every
recovered $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ in MLS-MPM. Parameter error and
forward replay error are correlated but not identical: sliding along the
HB ridge can move $\eta$ and $\sigma_Y$ while leaving the dam-break
trajectory nearly unchanged. We therefore report both the scalar replay
CDF in Fig.~\ref{fig:flow-error-cdf} and representative flow-curve
overlays in Fig.~\ref{fig:flowcurve-syn}.
\paragraph{Recovered flow curves on synthetic samples.}
Beyond the scalar parameter recovery of
Table~\ref{tab:setup-ablation}, the recovered Herschel--Bulkley

\subsection{Parameter Freshness for Time-Varying Materials}
\label{sec:freshness}

\paragraph{Central claim.}
Hamamichi's two-setup protocol requires the setup-$2$ geometry to be
chosen \emph{after} the setup-$1$ inverse returns, because the
Hessian-orthogonal proposer of \S\ref{sec:proposer} consumes
$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_1$. Under a simulation-in-the-loop
implementation this introduces a structural $\sim 8$\,h interval
between the two captures: setup-$1$ optimisation must finish before
setup-$2$ can even be released. For
\emph{industrial standardised products}---commercial sauces,
cosmetic emulsions---this interval is harmless: opening a new
package the next day returns to the same canonical state. For
\emph{self-prepared, non-standardised materials}---starch gels,
flour pastes, cooking-prepared sauces---the interval is destructive:
the material has aged into a different state by the time setup-$2$
is captured, and the joint inverse fuses two observations of
\emph{different materials} rather than two observations of the same
one. The seconds-scale inverse of \S\ref{sec:mainresults} collapses
this $\sim 8$\,h window to under a minute, so the same two-setup
machinery operates within the material's evolution timescale rather
than across it. This section establishes that claim through four
layers of evidence; we keep each layer's scope narrow and report
each layer's failure modes alongside its pass conditions.

\paragraph{Four layers of evidence.}
\begin{description}
  \item[\textnormal{A.}] \emph{Identifiability lower bound
  (synthetic).} How fast a kinetic timescale $T_k$ can the
  three-point sampling cadence $t\in\{0,20,40\}$\,min resolve at
  all? Layer~A prescribes synthetic
  $\boldsymbol{\theta}(t)$ trajectories with known $T_k$,
  $\sigma_{Y,\infty}$ and reports the $\hat T_k$ vs $T_k$ recovery
  curve plus the false-positive distribution under a $T_k=\infty$
  static control.
  \item[\textnormal{B.}] \emph{Sim-in-loop staleness counterfactual
  (synthetic).} For a target kinetic, what bias would the $\sim 8$\,h
  setup-$1\to$setup-$2$ interval introduce? Layer~B numerically
  reconstructs both protocols on the same synthetic ground truth and
  reports the bias $\Delta$ as a function of $T_k$. This is the
  layer that converts ``freshness = headline'' from narrative into a
  quantified finding.
  \item[\textnormal{C.}] \emph{Within-batch real evolution.} On six
  self-prepared materials, the same pipeline is run three times
  within a $60$\,min batch window and the recovered
  $\hat\sigma_Y(t)$ is fitted to a first-order build-up form. Pass
  conditions are pre-registered before any video is captured.
  \item[\textnormal{D.}] \emph{Real-material staleness verification.}
  The same materials are re-paired so that setup-$1$ and setup-$2$
  come from different time points (or different batches) of the same
  condition, mimicking sim-in-loop's enforced interval. Layer~D
  reports whether the real-material bias direction and order of
  magnitude agree with Layer~B's synthetic prediction.
\end{description}

% -------------------------------------------------------------------
\paragraph{Layer A: Identifiability lower bound on $T_k$.}
\label{sec:fresh-A}
We prescribe synthetic kinetic ground truth with
$\sigma_Y(t)=\sigma_{Y,\infty}(1-e^{-t/T_k})$ across six target
timescales $T_k\in\{10,20,30,60,120,\infty\}$\,min, holding
$\sigma_{Y,\infty}=100$\,Pa, $n=0.5$, $\eta=10$\,Pa$\cdot$s$^n$ at a
well-conditioned regime away from the $\sigma_Y$ identifiability
ridge of \S\ref{sec:inv-sy}. For each $T_k$ we render
dam-break videos with MPM at $t\in\{0,20,40\}$\,min and run the full
inverse pipeline, then fit the recovered $\hat\sigma_Y(t)$ back to
Eq.~\eqref{eq:avrami}. Each condition is repeated $5$ times across
random setup-$1$ geometries. The $T_k=\infty$ static control
quantifies the false-positive rate at which a non-evolving material
is mis-detected as kinetic.

Fig.~\ref{fig:fresh-A1} reports $\hat T_k$ vs $T_k$ on log-log axes,
with the static control on the right margin. We define the
\emph{minimum resolvable} $T_k$ as the smallest true $T_k$ at which
the median fractional error
$|\hat T_k - T_k|/T_k$ stays under $50\%$, and the \emph{ceiling}
$T_k$ as the largest true $T_k$ whose recovered $\hat T_k$ remains
distinguishable from the static control. The window
$[T_k^{\min},T_k^{\max}]$ produced by this layer is the
\emph{applicability bound} for the real panel of Layer~C: any real
material falling outside this window cannot be resolved by the
three-point cadence regardless of inverse quality.

\paragraph{Layer B: Sim-in-loop staleness counterfactual.}
\label{sec:fresh-B}
We hold the synthetic setting of Layer~A and now numerically
reconstruct two protocols on the same prescribed kinetic curves at
three diagnostic scenarios:
$T_k\in\{15\,\mathrm{min\ (fast)},\,45\,\mathrm{min\ (medium)},\,
180\,\mathrm{min\ (slow)}\}$.

\emph{Protocol $P_{\mathrm{fast}}$ (surrogate).} Setup-$1$ and
setup-$2$ are both released at $t=0$; the joint inverse produces
$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\mathrm{fast}}$ in seconds.

\emph{Protocol $P_{\mathrm{slow}}$ (simulation-in-the-loop
counterfactual).} Setup-$1$ is released at $t=0$. The
sim-in-loop inverse then takes $\sim 8$\,h to return
$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_1$, after which the proposer of
\S\ref{sec:proposer} selects $(W_2,H_2)$. Setup-$2$ is therefore
released at $t=8$\,h, with the material now in state
$\boldsymbol{\theta}(t=8\,\mathrm{h})$ rather than
$\boldsymbol{\theta}(t=0)$. The joint inverse then fuses
$\mathbf{y}^{(1)}$ at $\boldsymbol{\theta}(0)$ with $\mathbf{y}^{(2)}$
at $\boldsymbol{\theta}(8\,\mathrm{h})$, producing
$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\mathrm{slow}}$.

Both protocols are run $10$ times per scenario with different
random setup-$1$ geometries; we report the bias
$\Delta = \|\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\mathrm{slow}} -
\boldsymbol{\theta}(0)\| -
\|\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\mathrm{fast}} -
\boldsymbol{\theta}(0)\|$ and visualise both clouds in
flow-curve space (Fig.~\ref{fig:fresh-B1}). The $T_k=180$\,min
scenario is included precisely because we expect
$\Delta\approx 0$ there: a material that aged little within
$8$\,h gives no advantage to fast acquisition, and reporting that
boundary candidly is part of the claim.

\paragraph{Layer C: Within-batch evolution of real materials.}
\label{sec:fresh-C}
The real panel is deliberately small enough to run as a controlled
laboratory protocol but diverse enough to span smooth,
rheometer-feasible starch gels and visibly particulate composites
(Tab.~\ref{tab:fresh-mats}). Each session prepares a $400$\,g batch
at $25^{\circ}$C$\pm 0.5^{\circ}$C and samples it at
$t_i\in\{0,20,40\}$\,min with a joint two-setup dam-break inverse at
every time point. To remove a routing confound across time points
within one condition, the setup-$1$ geometry $(W_1,H_1)$ is fixed
once per condition rather than redrawn at each $t_i$; setup-$2$ is
then proposed from each timepoint's snapshot inverse by the
Hessian-orthogonal rule of \S\ref{sec:proposer}. A single ChArUco
calibration is shared across the session.

\emph{Kinetic fit.}
For each condition the recovered yield-stress trajectory is fit to
the first-order build-up form
\cite{avrami1939kinetics,mewis2009thixotropy,bates1988nonlinear}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:avrami}
    \sigma_Y(t)
    \;=\;
    \sigma_{Y,\infty}\,\bigl(1 - e^{-t/T_k}\bigr),
\end{equation}
by weighted nonlinear least squares with the per-time-point Laplace
half-widths from Eq.~\eqref{eq:obs-loss} as observation
uncertainties (Bates and Watts~\cite{bates1988nonlinear}). The
estimated relaxation time $T_k$ and asymptote $\sigma_{Y,\infty}$
are reported with $1\sigma$ intervals from the fit covariance. With
only three time points the fit has one degree of freedom; Layer~A
bounds the $T_k$ at which this is meaningful, and Layer~C
\emph{only} reports conditions whose recovered $\hat T_k$ falls
inside that bound.

\emph{Pre-registered pass/fail criteria.}
Before any video is captured, we commit to the four checks below;
Tab.~\ref{tab:fresh-results} reports each condition's pass/fail
under all applicable checks together with the aggregate pass rate.

\begin{description}
  \item[\textnormal{(C-i)}] \emph{Smooth-material plateau anchor.}
  For conditions with a $24$\,h parallel-plate rheometer plateau,
  $\hat\sigma_{Y,\infty}$ must lie in $[0.5\times, 2\times]$ of the
  rheometer value (equivalently $\pm 0.3$ in $\log_{10}$ space).
  Applies to katakuriko $3/5/7\%$ and shiratama $7\%$.
  \item[\textnormal{(C-ii)}] \emph{Concentration monotonicity.}
  Across paired concentrations of the same base material,
  $\hat\sigma_{Y,\infty}$ must increase with concentration. Applies
  to oats $5\%$ vs $9\%$ and katakuriko $3\%$ vs $5\%$ vs $7\%$.
  \item[\textnormal{(C-iii)}] \emph{Cross-batch consistency.} For
  the two-batch conditions (katakuriko $5\%$, oats $9\%$), the
  per-batch $(\hat T_k,\hat\sigma_{Y,\infty})$ pairs must overlap
  within $1\sigma$ Laplace CI. This isolates whether residual
  spread is preparation noise or pipeline noise.
  \item[\textnormal{(C-iv)}] \emph{Kinetic monotonicity.}
  $\hat\sigma_Y(t=40\,\mathrm{min})\ge\hat\sigma_Y(20)\ge\hat\sigma_Y(0)$
  allowing $1\sigma$ overlap. Applies to all $7$ conditions.
\end{description}

We report the aggregate pass rate (X/Y) along with an interpretation
of every failure case. Conceding fail rates is part of the design
of this section: a pre-registered pass rate of $5/7$ with two
interpreted failures is stronger evidence than a hand-curated $7/7$.
Fig.~\ref{fig:fresh-traj} reports the per-condition $\sigma_Y(t)$
trajectories with their fits and rheometer anchors;
Fig.~\ref{fig:fresh-demos} complements this with calibrated
dam-break stills at the three time points, especially for the
chunky conditions where rheometer references are unavailable.

\paragraph{Layer D: Real-material staleness verification.}
\label{sec:fresh-D}
Layer~D closes the argument by reproducing Layer~B's synthetic
finding on real material. We re-pair the captures of katakuriko
$5\%$ in two arrangements:
\begin{description}
  \item[\textnormal{D.1}] \emph{Same-batch delayed setup-$2$.} A
  dedicated session is run on a fresh katakuriko $5\%$ batch with
  setup-$1$ released at $t=0$ and setup-$2$ deliberately delayed to
  $t=40$\,min (within-window but maximally separated), then jointly
  inverted. This is contrasted against the same batch's
  $t=0$/$t=0$ joint inverse from Layer~C. Same-batch isolates the
  setup-$2$ delay from any cross-batch preparation noise and is the
  cleanest analogue of sim-in-loop's enforced interval.
  \item[\textnormal{D.2}] \emph{Cross-batch repurposing.} The
  existing two batches of katakuriko $5\%$ (and oats $9\%$, if
  available) are repurposed: setup-$1$ from batch~A is paired with
  setup-$2$ from batch~B at the same nominal $t$, mimicking what
  sim-in-loop would acquire on a non-standardised material when the
  next-day re-preparation falls within the batch-to-batch drift.
\end{description}

same-batch joint (Layer~C reference). The expected outcome from

\paragraph{Scope and acknowledged limitations.}
The freshness section operates under four explicit caveats.
\emph{(i) Sample size.} Each condition is captured once except for
the two repeat-batch rows; within-condition variance is therefore
not estimated for the single-batch conditions.
\emph{(ii) Fit degrees of freedom.} The three-point inverse fit of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:avrami} has one degree of freedom; Layer~A bounds the
$T_k$ window in which this is meaningful, and Layer~C only reports
inside that window.
\emph{(iii) Model misspecification.} A first-order build-up form is
assumed throughout. Materials that follow stretched-exponential or
two-stage kinetics will be misfit even if Layer~A's identifiability
bound is respected; if a stretched-exponential ground-truth sweep
is added to Layer~A, the resulting bias is reported there.
\emph{(iv) Not a substitute for full ageing studies.} Layer~C
characterises kinetics on the $0$--$40$\,min window only; the
$24$\,h rheometer plateau is used as an external check on the
asymptote, not as a curve our method recovers. Longer-window
ageing studies are out of scope.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{What the Acceleration Unlocks}

The immediate effect of our pipeline is a three-orders-of-magnitude
reduction in per-video inverse cost. The seconds-scale inverse
delivers two experimentally anchored payoffs: \emph{freshness} for
time-varying fluids (\S\ref{sec:freshness}) and fast
\emph{rheometer-referenced validation} on the Hamamichi materials
for which parallel-plate sweeps are available (\S\ref{sec:rheometer}).
The first is a temporal improvement that turns ``hours to return a
past state'' into ``seconds to return the current state.'' The
second checks that replacing MPM-in-the-loop by a routed GP surrogate
does not detach the recovered flow curves from independent
instrumental rheology.

\subsection{A Reusable Surrogate as a Community Asset}
\label{sec:asset}

The separation of a one-time offline training phase from a
seconds-scale online inference phase produces a reusable artefact
that is structurally unavailable to simulation-in-the-loop pipelines.
Our frozen HMoGP checkpoint was trained once on $295{,}419$ MPM
simulations covering $5.5$--$5.6$ orders of magnitude in $\eta$
and $\sigma_Y$ and the full $[2, 7]\,\mathrm{cm}$ range of
household containers. Subsequent users---and subsequent research on
video rheometry---can query the same checkpoint without paying the
MPM training cost again. This amortisation property is what turns
our method from a one-shot inverse into a reusable measurement
instrument; it is not available to sim-in-loop pipelines because
they have no surrogate to amortise. We release the checkpoint, the
training corpus, and the inverse code alongside this paper.

\subsection{Why Regime-Aware Surrogate and Identifiability-Aware
Inverse Must Go Together}

A takeaway at the core of this work is that neither the surrogate
nor the inverse modifications is sufficient alone. A single global
GP, even if it were accurate enough, would still leave the
$\sigma_Y$-identifiability ridge intact under a vanilla CMA-ES: the
optimiser would slide to $\sigma_Y \to 0$ while inflating $\eta$ to
match the observation. Conversely, the identifiability-aware inverse
of \S\ref{sec:inverse} requires an accurate surrogate to be evaluated
at the $\mathcal{O}(10^{3})$ candidates issued by a multi-restart
CMA-ES run within seconds (Tab.~\ref{tab:hyper});
running the same inverse against a coarse surrogate would
reintroduce surrogate bias as a new source of error. The
hierarchical mixture-of-GP (HMoGP) surrogate and the
GP-aware inverse---which keeps CMA-ES inside the routed expert's
training support and reads the residual ridge through restart
dispersion and the local Laplace--Hessian interval rather than
through a hand-chosen $\sigma_Y$ prior---must be coupled; this
coupling is the technical core.

\subsection{Relation to Differentiable MPM}

Differentiable MPM pipelines~\cite{li2023pacnerf,eastman2024recovery}
obtain gradients through the forward simulator and perform
gradient-based per-scene parameter search. We differ in two
respects. First, gradients through the non-smooth HB return map are
known to be
ill-conditioned~\cite{huang2021plasticinelab}; our gradient-free
inverse sidesteps this issue. Second, differentiable-MPM pipelines
optimise per scene and do not amortise a reusable surrogate, so
their per-material cost remains in the simulation-in-the-loop
regime. Our architectural choice of an offline-trained hierarchical
surrogate is precisely what enables the seconds-scale per-video
latency and the community-asset property of \S\ref{sec:asset}.


\subsection{Conclusion}

We presented a surrogate-accelerated framework for non-Newtonian
video rheometry that compresses the Herschel--Bulkley inverse from
around eight hours per setup in simulation-in-the-loop prior
work~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} to a median of $12$\,s per
video on a single consumer GPU. The method rests on two coupled
components: a regime-aware hierarchical Mixture-of-GP surrogate
(HMoGP) whose locally-trained exact-GP experts are routed
by a deterministic three-stage gate over container geometry,
terminal flow displacement, and normalised trajectory shape, with
a final observation-evidence rerank; and an identifiability-aware
GP-aware inverse that warm-starts CMA-ES from the routed expert's
nearest training neighbour and reports the residual
yield-stress/consistency ridge through multi-restart dispersion and
a local Laplace--Hessian $95\%$ confidence interval, rather than
breaking the ridge with a hand-chosen $\sigma_Y$ prior.
\citet{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} mitigates the same ridge with
Hessian-based similarity analysis, a Poiseuille-flow proxy, active
setup selection, and iterative multi-setup CMA-ES; our path
swaps that Hessian machinery for surrogate-amortised diagnosis and
a replay-consistency-guarded two-setup joint inversion that
preserves the single-setup recovery---both made practical by the
surrogate.

Beyond runtime reduction, the seconds-scale inverse makes two
capabilities practical: fresh parameter estimates for time-varying
fluids and fast real-fluid validation against the Hamamichi materials
with parallel-plate rheometer references. We release the trained
surrogate, the $295{,}419$-simulation MPM training corpus, and the
inverse code as a reusable community artefact on which subsequent
video-rheometry research can build without paying the simulation
training cost again.

The broader message is that video rheometry crosses the threshold
from laboratory demonstration to fieldable tool not by replacing
physics-based estimation but by amortising its cost. The surrogate
is an accelerator of a physics-grounded inverse, not a substitute
for physics; what this acceleration enables, at seconds per video,
is what brings the technique within reach of time-sensitive,
iterative, and situated measurement.


\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\subsection{Information-Theoretic Yield-Stress Limit}
The identifiability-aware inverse of \S\ref{sec:inverse} resolves
the yield-stress/consistency degeneracy whenever the dam-break
observation carries \emph{some} signal about $\sigma_Y$, but it
cannot recover information that is not in the observation. For
materials in which $\eta\,\dot\gamma^{\,n} \gg \sigma_Y$ across the
entire dam-break shear-rate range, the flow front encodes
essentially no information about $\sigma_Y$, and any inverse---ours
included---returns an estimate determined by the prior and the
training distribution rather than by the data. The $N=30$ held-out
panel of Table~\ref{tab:setup-ablation} contains low-$\sigma_Y$
samples in this regime; their estimates are dominated by the prior
rather than by the data, and contribute disproportionately to the
$\sigma_Y$ mean relative to its median. This is a task-level bound,
not a method bound; the appropriate mitigation is the multi-setup
protocol of \S\ref{sec:mainresults}.

\subsection{Dependence on Training-Set Coverage}
The surrogate is trustworthy only inside the input box on which it
was trained. Queries outside this box trigger the per-expert bound
tightening (\S\ref{sec:inverse}), which relies on the routed
expert's training inputs as the admissible region and therefore
does not extrapolate. Out-of-distribution materials require an
extension of the training corpus. The per-expert GP predictive
variance flags out-of-distribution queries but does not by itself
correct them.

\subsection{Fidelity of the Forward Model}
A surrogate can only be as accurate as the simulator it learns from.
Our MPM forward (\S3.2) models HB rheology, gravity, and no-slip
boundaries, but not surface tension, elastic overshoot, thixotropy,
or viscoelastic memory. Materials exhibiting these phenomena deviate
systematically from the HB forward, and this deviation cannot be
recovered by any HB inverse.

\subsection{Capture and Geometry Requirements}
The level-$1$ gate is trained on rectangular cube-like containers
with $W, H \in [2, 7]$\,cm; curved or irregular containers fall
outside the calibrated geometry box. Camera calibration uses a
printed ChArUco marker board.

\subsection{Latency Regime}
Our median wall-clock is $12$\,s per video on a single consumer
GPU---seconds-scale but not millisecond-scale. The ``fast'' of our
title denotes \emph{fast enough to fit within a single user
interaction}, not strict continuous-time inversion.
