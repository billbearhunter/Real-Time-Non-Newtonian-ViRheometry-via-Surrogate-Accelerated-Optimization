\section{Introduction}

Many everyday fluids---yogurts, pastes, sauces, slurries, cosmetic
emulsions---exhibit non-Newtonian behavior that governs how they pour,
spread, cling, and deform. Characterizing this behavior quantitatively
requires measuring shear-rate-dependent stress, traditionally using
parallel-plate or cone-and-plate rheometers. However, these instruments
fail on a practically important class of materials: those containing
inclusions larger than the instrument's measurement gap---chunky sauces,
particulate suspensions, foods with solids---and those whose rheology
evolves faster than a multi-minute lab procedure can capture.

Non-Newtonian video rheometry~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} addresses
this gap by estimating Herschel--Bulkley parameters
$(\sigma_Y, \eta, n)$ from observations of a simple dam-break flow,
without contacting the material with any instrument. The underlying
principle is inverse simulation: a material point method (MPM) forward
model is repeatedly executed inside an optimizer until the simulated
flow reproduces the observed silhouette. Since the forward model is
physics-based, the approach naturally handles the very materials that
instrumental rheometers cannot, and recovers a full constitutive triple
from a single short video.

However, this otherwise uniquely capable approach is bottlenecked by the
cost of its inner loop. A single MPM dam-break simulation takes several
minutes on modern GPU hardware, and derivative-free optimization
typically issues hundreds of candidate evaluations before convergence.
The end-to-end inverse therefore takes \emph{over ten hours per
video}, confining the method to offline research use and precluding
three capabilities that would otherwise be within reach of a
video-based approach:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{Freshness for time-varying fluids.} When a material's
    properties evolve during the measurement window---sedimentation,
    aging, temperature drift, mixing history, cooking
    cooldown---an hour-scale inversion returns a parameter describing a
    past state. The inferred value is stale by the time it is delivered.
    \item \emph{Practical use on rheometer-infeasible materials.} The
    class of materials for which video rheometry has independent value
    over instrumental rheometry---chunky sauces, particulate
    suspensions---remains confined to offline demonstration rather than
    fieldable tool so long as each measurement costs hours.
    \item \emph{Multi-observation precision recovery.}
    \cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} itself identifies a similarity
    degeneracy between yield stress and consistency, and proposes
    multi-setup joint inversion as the mitigation---but at hours per
    setup, $N$-setup inversion is computationally infeasible in
    practice.
\end{itemize}

We propose a surrogate-accelerated framework that compresses the
inverse from hours to \emph{seconds per video}, making all three
capabilities available within a practical workflow. Our technical core
couples two components.
\emph{(i)}~A \emph{regime-aware hierarchical Mixture-of-GP surrogate}
(HVIMoGP-rBCM) replaces MPM inside the optimizer: a two-level Bayesian
Gaussian mixture gate routes each query---first by container geometry
$(W, H)$, then by observation signature---through one of 540 specialized
sparse Gaussian-process experts, aggregated at prediction time by the
robust Bayesian Committee Machine. This hierarchical structure is
motivated by the fact that the Herschel--Bulkley inverse landscape is
not globally homogeneous: the flow regime changes morphologically with
container shape and discretely across the yield threshold, so a single
global surrogate cannot cover the $5.5$ orders of magnitude in $\eta$
and $5.6$ orders in $\sigma_Y$ at the accuracy required for optimization.
\emph{(ii)}~An \emph{identifiability-aware inverse} in which CMA-ES is
warm-started from the routed expert's nearest training neighbor in
observation space, and anchored by a soft log-$\sigma_Y$ prior at that
neighbor. The prior is \emph{data-honest} in that its center comes from
a training point of the surrogate rather than from a user-chosen guess.
Together, the warm-start and anchored prior collapse the
yield-stress/consistency ridge, recovering a physically plausible
$\sigma_Y$ where vanilla CMA-ES would slide to $\sigma_Y \to 0$.

Our work inherits the problem setup, dam-break observation protocol,
and inverse formulation from~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}, and
contributes the machinery needed to make that formulation
computationally practical. \cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} treats
the yield-stress/consistency degeneracy with a four-part pipeline:
a loss-Hessian similarity analysis, a plane-Poiseuille-flow proxy
for that Hessian, Hessian-guided active selection of successive
setups, and iterative multi-setup CMA-ES over the selected setups.
That pipeline was validated on $30$ material-trial cases but remains
gated by the per-iterate MPM cost of the inner CMA-ES. We depart by
taking a different, surrogate-native mitigation path (detailed in
\S\ref{sec:inverse}): our inverse neither computes Hessians nor
selects setups, and our multi-setup formulation is a single joint
optimization over all available setups rather than a sequential
refinement. The main contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item A \emph{hierarchical Mixture-of-GP surrogate} for the
    Herschel--Bulkley inverse landscape that covers
    $5.5$--$5.6$ orders of magnitude in $\eta$ and $\sigma_Y$ using 540
    specialized experts routed by a two-level Bayesian Gaussian mixture
    gate and aggregated via robust BCM.
    \item An \emph{identifiability-aware inverse loop} that combines
    training-nearest-neighbor warm-starting with a data-honest
    log-$\sigma_Y$ soft prior, resolving the yield-stress/consistency
    similarity degeneracy without injecting user-chosen bias.
    \item An evaluation establishing that seconds-scale video rheometry
    unlocks three capabilities inaccessible to simulation-in-the-loop
    inversion: \emph{(a)} fresh parameter estimates for time-varying
    fluids, \emph{(b)} practical characterization of materials
    conventional rheometers cannot measure, and \emph{(c)} a
    multi-observation joint protocol that approaches parallel-plate
    rheometer precision from phone videos alone.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Non-Newtonian Fluid Simulation}

Our forward model inherits the material point method (MPM) lineage that
brought continuum simulation into graphics~\cite{stomakhin2013material,
jiang2015affine,hu2018moving,jiang2016material}. Within this family,
shear-thinning and yield-stress rheology---captured by the
Herschel--Bulkley model~\cite{herschel1926konsistenzmessungen}---has been
realized through return-mapping formulations that encompass viscous,
viscoplastic, and granular regimes under a common MPM
substrate~\cite{yue2015continuum,ram2015material,klar2016drucker,
tampubolon2017multi,wolper2019cdmpm}. We adopt the Herschel--Bulkley
return map of Yue et al.~\cite{yue2015continuum}; the sauce-specific
shear-thinning simulator of Nagasawa et al.~\cite{nagasawa2019mixing},
from the same lab as our direct predecessor, shares our material focus.
The forward simulator is implemented on Taichi~\cite{hu2019taichi}; a
broader introduction to fluid simulation for graphics
appears in~\cite{bridson2015fluid}.


\subsection{Video-Based Inverse Rheometry}

Estimating simulation parameters from video by matching rendered and
observed dynamics has a long lineage in graphics and
vision~\cite{bhat2002computing,monszpart2016smash}. Takahashi and
Lin~\cite{takahashi2019video} established the video-to-parameter
pattern for viscous fluids by matching rendered and observed
dam-break silhouettes on a per-scene basis; Hamamichi et
al.~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} extended this to the
non-Newtonian Herschel--Bulkley triple $(\sigma_Y,\eta,n)$ and is
the direct predecessor of our work.
Their treatment of the yield-stress/consistency degeneracy is more
elaborate than a single remedy: they (i) diagnose the degeneracy by
computing the loss Hessian $\mathbf{H}_{M;S}$ and identifying its
small-eigenvalue directions as a local \emph{similarity set} of
indistinguishable material parameters; (ii) replace the
prohibitively expensive dam-break Hessian with an analytic
plane-Poiseuille-flow surrogate $\mathbf{H}^{PP}_{M;S_{PP}}$ plus
a learned map $S_{PP}=\Theta(M,S)$; (iii) use the Hessian condition
number to \emph{actively select} successive setups whose eigenstructure
complements the current similarity set; and (iv) run a sequential
multi-setup CMA-ES over the selected setups, which they benchmark
systematically across $30$ material-trial cases and demonstrate
iteratively reduces relative error as $S$ grows from $1$ to $4$.
We inherit their dam-break observation protocol, their
Herschel--Bulkley inverse objective, and their
simulation-in-the-loop formulation. Our treatment of the degeneracy
follows a \emph{complementary} path: we do not compute Hessians or
select setups, relying instead on a nearest-neighbor warm-start from
the routed expert's training data and a data-honest log-$\sigma_Y$
soft prior anchored at that neighbor (\S\ref{sec:inverse}), combined
with a joint---rather than sequential---multi-setup inversion
(\S\ref{sec:method}). The surrogate makes both the
single-setup inverse and the multi-setup joint inverse cheap enough
to run per material by default, rather than as a dedicated validation
sweep.

Related work recovers material parameters from other visual modalities:
video-based cloth friction~\cite{rasheed2020visual},
particle-image-velocimetry-based rheology through physics-informed
networks~\cite{thakur2025learning}, and consumer-video neural regression
for scalar viscosity~\cite{rauzan2024protorheology}. These target
different material classes or observation modalities and do not recover
the full Herschel--Bulkley triple. The physical basis for encoding
rheology in dam-break fronts was established analytically by
Balmforth et al.~\cite{balmforth2007two}, and free-surface extraction
from experimental video is treated in~\cite{cochard2009tracking}. The
inverse-rheometry problem has also been approached in the
geophysical-flow setting using shallow-water forward models and adjoint
methods~\cite{martin2010inverse}; our formulation differs in using a
full three-dimensional MPM forward model and a derivative-free inverse
over a learned surrogate.


\subsection{Differentiable MPM for Inverse Problems}

An alternative paradigm pursues parameter recovery via differentiable
simulation, using gradients through the forward model for gradient-based
inverse search. The differentiable MLS-MPM
family~\cite{hu2019chainqueen,hu2020difftaichi,huang2021plasticinelab}
has been applied to deformable and plastic materials, and end-to-end
differentiable physics with differentiable rendering has been
demonstrated by gradSim~\cite{murthy2021gradsim}. Most relevant to
non-Newtonian parameter recovery from video,
PAC-NeRF~\cite{li2023pacnerf} couples differentiable MPM with NeRF
rendering to recover viscosity parameters from multi-view video, and
Eastman~\cite{eastman2024recovery} recovers Herschel--Bulkley parameters
from a ketchup column-collapse video via differentiable MPM and neural
rendering. These approaches optimize per scene and do not amortize a
reusable surrogate across materials. Further, evidence from
PlasticineLab~\cite{huang2021plasticinelab} indicates that gradients
through non-smooth plastic return maps are ill-conditioned, motivating
our choice of a gradient-free inverse over a probabilistic surrogate.


\subsection{Gaussian-Process Surrogates and Mixtures of Experts}

Gaussian-process regression~\cite{rasmussen2006gaussian} provides a
natural surrogate model for expensive simulators because it couples
flexible nonparametric regression with calibrated predictive
uncertainty. For data sizes beyond exact-GP feasibility, variational and
sparse approximations~\cite{snelson2006sparse,quinonero2005unifying,
titsias2009variational,hensman2013gaussian,matthews2016sparse} replace
the full covariance with an inducing-variable approximation; each expert
in our surrogate is an exact GP trained on a gate-routed subset, with
SVGP backends available at larger scales.

A single global GP is insufficient for functions with heterogeneous
local structure. Mixture-of-experts (MoE)
models~\cite{jordan1994hierarchical,yuksel2012twenty} decompose
prediction into multiple specialists with a gating mechanism, and
mixtures of GPs instantiate this idea with GP
experts~\cite{tresp2001mixtures,rasmussen2002infinite,
meeds2006alternative,nguyen2008local,yuan2008variational,trapp2020deep}.
Among these, Meeds and Osindero~\cite{meeds2006alternative} (a Gaussian
mixture over inputs coupled with GP outputs) and Nguyen-Tuong et
al.~\cite{nguyen2008local} (hard input-space partitioning with
independent per-region GPs and nearest-neighbor fusion) are the closest
architectural ancestors of our surrogate. We extend this pattern with a
two-level Bayesian Gaussian mixture gate---first on container geometry
$(W, H)$, then on an observation signature---and replace
nearest-neighbor fusion with robust committee-machine aggregation.

For aggregating independent GP predictions, the Bayesian Committee
Machine~\cite{tresp2000bayesian} and its modern variants---generalized
Product of Experts~\cite{cao2014generalized}, robust BCM
(rBCM)~\cite{deisenroth2015distributed}, and generalized robust BCM
(GRBCM)~\cite{liu2018generalized}---provide closed-form
variance-weighted fusion. We adopt
rBCM~\cite{deisenroth2015distributed} with top-1 routing; our ablations
(\S\ref{sec:ablation}) show that the additional communication expert of
GRBCM does not justify its inference-time overhead given our peaked
gate posterior. Sparse-expert plus BCM aggregation has recently been
applied to physics emulation~\cite{cohen2024sparse}, providing modern
precedent for our approach.


\subsection{Surrogate-Accelerated Inverse and Derivative-Free Optimization}

Inverting expensive simulators has been addressed under the headings of
Bayesian calibration of computer models~\cite{kennedy2001bayesian} and
simulation-based inference~\cite{cranmer2020frontier}, with efficient
global optimization~\cite{jones1998efficient} and Bayesian
optimization~\cite{snoek2012practical,shahriari2016taking} providing the
dominant search strategies when the objective is expensive and
derivative-free. Our inverse is closest to the ``CMA-ES on a GP
surrogate'' pattern~\cite{bajaj2019gaussian,hansen2016cma}, which has
been applied to inverse
viscoplasticity~\cite{vandamme2021bayesian} and
viscoelasticity~\cite{hashemi2025bayesian}. We depart from this pattern
in two respects: first, each inverse is routed through a hierarchical
surrogate rather than a single global GP; second, we introduce an
observation-space nearest-neighbor warm-start and a
training-data-anchored log-$\sigma_Y$ prior to address the yield-stress
identifiability degeneracy that is specific to Herschel--Bulkley
inversion and not resolved by generic BO machinery.

The substitution of an expensive simulator $\mathcal{S}$ by a trained
surrogate $\tilde{\mathcal{S}}$ inside a Bayesian / derivative-free
inverse is not heuristic: \citet{stuart2018posterior} prove that the
Hellinger distance between a GP-surrogate-based posterior and the
true simulator-based posterior is bounded by moments of the
surrogate's predictive error, and \citet{teckentrup2020convergence}
extends the guarantee to the realistic setting in which kernel
hyperparameters are themselves estimated from data. Our surrogate
satisfies the premise of both results at evaluation time---the rBCM
aggregator returns calibrated predictive error natively---so the
replacement of MPM-in-the-loop by our hierarchical GP committee
inherits the same posterior-fidelity guarantees that justify
Bayesian calibration~\cite{kennedy2001bayesian} and modern
simulation-based inference~\cite{cranmer2020frontier}.


\subsection{Flow Observations as Rheometric Signals}

A second prerequisite for replacing simulation-in-the-loop inversion
with a surrogate is that the chosen observation $\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$
actually carries enough information about
$(\sigma_Y,\eta,n)$ to be worth inverting at all. The single scalar
flow-front trace extracted from a phone-video dam-break looks
information-poor at first sight, but gravity-driven spreading of
viscoplastic fluids has a long pedigree as a rheometric observation:
the slump test of \citet{pashias1996fifty} recovers yield stress
from a single geometric reading, and \citet{roussel2005fiftycent}
extend the argument to the spreading regime that matches our
dam-break geometry directly. Final-deposit shapes of stopped
free-surface flows are themselves a recognised rheological
signature~\cite{coussot1996rheological}, and
\citet{ancey2007plasticity} frames the broader class of
gravity-driven yield-stress flows as a standard rheometric regime.
The relation between flow-front position and
Herschel--Bulkley parameters is made quantitative by the similarity
analyses of \citet{liumei1989slow} and
\citet{balmforth2007two}---the same similarity structure leveraged
by~\citet{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}.

Recent inverse-analysis work sharpens these classical arguments into
explicit identifiability statements for our observation class.
\citet{martinpritchard2022inferring} show that the full generalized
Newtonian constitutive function is, in principle, uniquely
recoverable from free-surface evolution;
\citet{ahmadi2022identification} compute linearly-independent
sensitivity coefficients of the three Herschel--Bulkley parameters
with respect to free-surface readings; and
\citet{muchiri2024identification} demonstrate inverse recovery of
yield stress and consistency from shallow-water elevation
hydrographs under viscoplastic rheology. The residual
yield-stress / consistency degeneracy we address in~\S\ref{sec:inverse}
is therefore not a fundamental information-theoretic limit of the
phone-video observation---it is a practical ill-conditioning that
arises only in the low-$\sigma_Y$ regime where the yield term is
dominated by the viscous contribution, which our data-honest
log-$\sigma_Y$ prior is designed to mitigate rather than paper over.


\section{Background}

\subsection{Herschel--Bulkley Rheology}

Many non-Newtonian fluids are well approximated by the
Herschel--Bulkley (HB) model~\cite{herschel1926konsistenzmessungen},
which expresses the shear-stress response as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hb-scalar}
    \tau \;=\; \sigma_Y \;+\; \eta\,\dot\gamma^{\,n},
\end{equation}
where $\tau$ is the shear stress, $\dot\gamma$ the shear rate,
$\sigma_Y$ the yield stress, $\eta$ the consistency parameter, and
$n \in (0, 1]$ controls shear thinning ($n < 1$) versus Newtonian
behaviour ($n = 1$). In three dimensions the deviatoric stress tensor
is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hb-tensor}
    \boldsymbol{\tau}^{\mathrm{dev}}
    \;=\;
    \left[\;\frac{\sigma_Y}{\|\mathbf{D}\|}
    \;+\; \eta\,\|\mathbf{D}\|^{\,n-1}\;\right] \mathbf{D},
    \qquad
    \|\mathbf{D}\|
    = \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{D}\!:\!\mathbf{D}},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{D}$ is the rate-of-strain tensor. The admissible
stress region is delimited by the von~Mises yield function
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hb-yield}
    \Phi_{Y}(\boldsymbol{\tau})
    \;=\; \|\boldsymbol{\tau}^{\mathrm{dev}}\| \;-\; \sigma_Y \;\le\; 0,
\end{equation}
with the plastic flow rule of Eq.~\eqref{eq:hb-tensor} activated only
when $\Phi_{Y} > 0$. We adopt the elasto-plastic MPM realisation of
Yue et al.~\cite{yue2015continuum}, which recovers the same
$(\sigma_Y, \eta, n)$ triple at steady state via a Newton--Raphson
return map that enforces $\Phi_{Y} \le 0$ on each plastic step. The HB
response is therefore characterised by the three-parameter vector
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\theta} \;=\; (\sigma_Y,\,\eta,\,n) \in \Theta \subset
    \mathbb{R}^{3}.
\end{equation}
This parameter triple coincides with that of prior non-Newtonian video
rheometry~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}; our goal is not to
propose a new constitutive model, but to recover $\boldsymbol{\theta}$
from visual observations at a latency compatible with real-world use.


\subsection{Dam-Break MPM Forward Model}

Following the experimental protocol
of~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}, we obtain $\boldsymbol{\theta}$
by observing a dam-break flow released from a rectangular fluid column
of width $W$ and height $H$ on a flat, no-slip floor. The forward
simulator integrates the HB return map of \S3.1 on a
moving-least-squares MPM~\cite{hu2018moving} grid with spacing
$\Delta x \approx 10^{-1}$\,cm and timestep $\Delta t = 5\times
10^{-4}$\,s, implemented on Taichi~\cite{hu2019taichi}. The
observation is a vector of flow-front displacements sampled at eight
uniformly spaced frames $t_1,\dots,t_8$ after release:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:y-def}
    \mathbf{y}
    \;=\;
    \bigl(x_1,\,x_2,\,\dots,\,x_8\bigr)^{\!\top}
    \;\in\;
    \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}^{8},
\end{equation}
where $x_i$ is the maximum lateral extent of the fluid at time $t_i$
in cm. By construction $x_1 \le x_2 \le \dots \le x_8$, since the
flow front cannot retract. We write the forward simulator as
$\mathbf{y} = \mathcal{S}(\boldsymbol{\theta};\,W, H)$.

\paragraph{Boundary, free-surface, and silhouette details.}
Three implementation choices in the forward model bear mention.
First, the solid walls of the dam-break container are treated with
sticky boundaries: momentum on grid cells interior to the container
geometry is zeroed on every step, and any particle that penetrates a
wall box is flagged as \emph{interior} and excluded from the
downstream silhouette so that wall-embedded fluid cannot produce
spurious bright pixels in
$\mathbf{y} = \mathcal{S}(\boldsymbol{\theta}; W, H)$. Second, the
fluid surface is extracted by Laplacian-then-biharmonic smoothing of
a rasterised signed-distance field, with per-cell $\varphi$ clamped at
every smoothing step to the range
$[\varphi_{\min}, \varphi_{\max}]$ admitted by the local particle-radius
bounds; this automatic clamp replaces the manual per-frame $\varphi$
adjustment used in earlier iterations of the pipeline and prevents
smoothing-induced surface inflation without hand tuning. Third, the
resulting zero-level set is meshed by marching cubes and rendered
through the calibrated camera $(\mathbf{K}_{0}, R^{*}, \mathbf{t}^{*})$ of
\S\ref{sec:calib} to a binary silhouette frame-compatible with
$\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$, closing the loop between the MPM
particles and the $8$-dimensional flow-distance vector that the
inverse operates on.


\subsection{From Phone Video to Flow-Front Distances}
\label{sec:calib}

To run the inverse on a real experiment, we need to convert a
smartphone video of the dam-break flow into the observation vector
$\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}} = (x_{1}, \dots, x_{8}) \in
\mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}^{8}$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:y-def}. We use a two-stage
camera calibration followed by silhouette-based flow-front
extraction; the full pipeline is summarised in
Algorithm~\ref{alg:calib}.

\paragraph{Stage 1: ChArUco prior.}
A printed ChArUco
board~\cite{garrido2014automatic,garrido2016generation} is placed in
the experimental scene alongside the fluid container. The standard
pinhole projection~\cite{hartley2004multiple} sends a world point
$\mathbf{P}_{w}$ to pixels via
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pinhole}
    \mathbf{P}_{c} \;=\; \mathbf{R}\,\mathbf{P}_{w} + \mathbf{t},
    \qquad
    \mathbf{p} \;=\; \tfrac{1}{Z_{c}}\,\mathbf{K}\,\mathbf{P}_{c},
    \quad
    \mathbf{K} = \begin{pmatrix}
          f_{x} & 0     & c_{x} \\
          0     & f_{y} & c_{y} \\
          0     & 0     & 1
        \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
Rather than optimising $(K, R, \mathbf{t})$ as $11$ free scalars, we
exploit the fact that our rig is level and the camera looks at a
fixed target, and parameterise the whole camera by a compact
$7$-vector
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:theta-cam}
    \boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{cam}}
    \;=\;
    (l_{x},\,l_{z},\,\theta_{E},\,\phi_{E},\,\psi_{E},\,
    \mathrm{fov},\,d_{\mathrm{eye}}),
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{l} = (l_{x}, 0, l_{z})^{\!\top}$ is the look-at point
on the rig floor, $(\theta_{E}, \phi_{E}, \psi_{E})$ are spherical
angles of the eye direction, $d_{\mathrm{eye}}$ is the eye--target
distance, and $\mathrm{fov}$ is the vertical field of view. The eye position and
an orthonormal camera basis are then reconstructed deterministically
from $\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{cam}}$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:eye}
    \mathbf{E}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{cam}})
    \;=\; d_{\mathrm{eye}}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \sin\theta_{E}\cos\phi_{E} \\
        \cos\theta_{E} \\
        -\sin\theta_{E}\sin\phi_{E}
    \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:basis}
    \mathbf{C}_{z} = \frac{\mathbf{E} - \mathbf{l}}
                           {\|\mathbf{E} - \mathbf{l}\|},
    \quad
    \mathbf{C}_{x} = \frac{(C_{z,3},\,0,\,-C_{z,1})^{\!\top}}
                           {\|(C_{z,3},\,0,\,-C_{z,1})\|},
    \quad
    \mathbf{C}_{y} = \mathbf{C}_{z} \times \mathbf{C}_{x},
\end{equation}
so that $R = [\mathbf{C}_{x}\,|\,\mathbf{C}_{y}\,|\,\mathbf{C}_{z}]^{\!\top}$ and
$\mathbf{t} = -R\,\mathbf{E}$; the $\mathbf{C}_{x}$ construction
enforces a horizontal camera up-axis. A world point $\mathbf{P}_{i}$
thus maps first to camera coordinates
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:screen}
    \mathbf{Y}_{i}
    \;=\;
    \bigl(
      (\mathbf{P}_{i} - \mathbf{E})\!\cdot\!\mathbf{C}_{x},\;
      (\mathbf{P}_{i} - \mathbf{E})\!\cdot\!\mathbf{C}_{y},\;
      (\mathbf{P}_{i} - \mathbf{E})\!\cdot\!\mathbf{C}_{z}
    \bigr),
\end{equation}
and then to pixels by perspective division. Matching the renderer's
vertical-field-of-view convention $f_{y} = H/(2\tan(\mathrm{fov}/2))$
gives the scale factor
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:scale}
    s \;=\; \frac{1}{2\,\tan(\mathrm{fov}/2)},
\end{equation}
and the full reprojection of $\mathbf{P}_{i}$ into the image plane
$[0, W]\times[0, H]$ reads
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:project}
    \pi(\mathbf{P}_{i};\,\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{cam}})
    \;=\;
    \left(
        \frac{W}{2} + s\,H\,\frac{[\mathbf{Y}_{i}]_{1}}{|[\mathbf{Y}_{i}]_{3}|},\;
        \frac{H}{2} - s\,H\,\frac{[\mathbf{Y}_{i}]_{2}}{|[\mathbf{Y}_{i}]_{3}|}
    \right).
\end{equation}

After sub-pixel corner refinement~\cite{forstner1987fast} on the
detected ChArUco board, Stage~1 solves
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:stage1}
    \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\mathrm{cam}}
    \;=\;
    \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{cam}}}\,
    \frac{1}{|\mathcal{I}|}
    \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}}
    \rho_{\delta}\!\bigl(
      \|\mathbf{u}_{i} - \pi(\mathbf{P}_{i};\,\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{cam}})\|
    \bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{I}$ indexes detected corners with pixel observations
$\mathbf{u}_{i}$ and known board coordinates $\mathbf{P}_{i}$, and
$\rho_{\delta}$ is the Huber loss with a $\delta = 2$\,px threshold,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:huber}
    \rho_{\delta}(e)
    \;=\;
    \begin{cases}
        \tfrac{1}{2}e^{2}, & e < \delta, \\
        \delta\bigl(e - \tfrac{1}{2}\delta\bigr), & e \ge \delta,
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
which prevents any single mis-refined corner from dominating the fit.
The minimisation uses adaptive
Nelder--Mead~\cite{nelder1965simplex,gao2012implementing} because
$\pi$ is cheap to evaluate but awkward to differentiate under the
$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{cam}}$ parameterisation. Stage~1
returns an intrinsic matrix $\mathbf{K}_{0}$ (derived from
$\mathrm{fov}$ and the image size) and an initial extrinsic
$(R_{0}, \mathbf{t}_{0})$ (derived from $\mathbf{E}, \mathbf{l}$
via Eq.~\eqref{eq:basis}).

\paragraph{Stage 2: silhouette-guided extrinsic refinement.}
Stage~1 is typically accurate to a few pixels but can leave a small
residual offset, since the ChArUco board is not rigidly coupled to
the container. Stage~2 \emph{fixes} $\mathbf{K}_{0}$ and refines only the
extrinsic parameters so that a rendered container silhouette
aligns with the reference frame $I_{0}$ captured at the instant
of dam release. We use a two-phase schedule.

\emph{Phase~A (smooth chamfer).}
A Canny edge detector~\cite{canny1986computational} applied to
$I_{0}$ yields a target-edge pixel set $E$, from which we
precompute an exact Euclidean distance
transform~\cite{maurer2003linear,borgefors1986distance}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:edt}
    D_{E}(p) \;=\;
    \min_{q \in E}\,\|\,p - q\,\|_{2}
    \qquad \text{for each pixel } p \in \Omega.
\end{equation}
For candidate extrinsics $(R, \mathbf{t})$, let $P_{f}(R, \mathbf{t})$
denote the projected edge pixels of the $f$-th visible cube face
and $\mathcal{F}$ the set of visible faces. The per-face-normalised
weighted chamfer cost is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:chamfer}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cham}}(R, \mathbf{t})
    \;=\;
    \frac{1}{|\mathcal{F}|}
    \sum_{f \in \mathcal{F}}
    \frac{\sum_{p \in P_{f}(R,\mathbf{t})}\, w(p)\, D_{E}(p)}
         {\sum_{p \in P_{f}(R,\mathbf{t})}\, w(p)},
\end{equation}
with $D_{E}(p)$ bilinearly interpolated from the EDT and
$w(p)$ a product of three side-emphasis weights
$w_{\mathrm{left}}(p_{x})$, $w_{\mathrm{top}}(p_{y})$, and
$w_{\mathrm{bottom}}(p_{y})$.
Per-face normalisation prevents long edges (e.g.\ the bottom wall)
from dominating short ones. We minimise $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cham}}$
with L-BFGS-B~\cite{byrd1995limited,zhu1997algorithm} over the six
DoF of a Rodrigues rotation vector~\cite{rodrigues1840lois} and a
translation, box-clamped to $\pm 2.5^{\circ}$ and $\pm 2$\,cm around
the Stage-$1$ prior $(\mathbf{r}_{0}, \mathbf{t}_{0})$.

\emph{Phase~B (non-smooth IoU).}
Starting from the Phase-$A$ solution, we maximise a weighted Jaccard
similarity / pixel-IoU~\cite{jaccard1912distribution} between the
rendered silhouette $M(R, \mathbf{t})$ and the target foreground $T$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:iou}
    \mathrm{IoU}_{w}(R, \mathbf{t})
    \;=\;
    \frac{\sum_{p}\, w(p)\,[\,M(p)\wedge T(p)\,]}
         {\sum_{p}\, w(p)\,[\,M(p)\vee T(p)\,]},
\end{equation}
with an adaptive Nelder--Mead~\cite{gao2012implementing} simplex of
size $0.15^{\circ}/1.5$\,mm. Because $\mathrm{IoU}_{w}$ is
discretised on the pixel grid it is non-differentiable; the
L-BFGS-B phase on the smooth chamfer cost provides the warm start
needed to avoid the combinatorial local minima of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:iou}.

\paragraph{Safety clamp.}
After refinement we check the deviation from the Stage-$1$ prior,
$\Delta_{\mathrm{rot}} = \|\mathbf{r}^{*} - \mathbf{r}_{0}\|$ and
$\Delta_{t} = \|\mathbf{t}^{*} - \mathbf{t}_{0}\|$; if either
exceeds the threshold ($2.5^{\circ}$ or $2$\,cm) we fall back to
the ChArUco extrinsic. This guards against strong local minima in
either phase, particularly when ChArUco board placement is sloppy.

\paragraph{Flow-front extraction.}
Each subsequent frame $I_{1}, \dots, I_{8}$ captured at
$t_{1}, \dots, t_{8}$
is binarised by HSV-intensity thresholding (threshold $128$, black
foreground by default) followed by morphological opening and closing
on a $3\times 3$ kernel~\cite{gonzalez2018digital}; only the largest
connected foreground component is retained to reject spurious
specks. We then extract the outer contour $\partial\Omega_{i}$ of
the foreground mask at frame $i$ and back-project every contour
pixel $\mathbf{u} = (u, v)$ to the world ground plane $y = 0$
through the calibrated pinhole camera. With
$\mathbf{C}_{w} = -R^{\!\top}\,\mathbf{t}$ the world-frame camera
centre and
$\mathbf{d}(\mathbf{u}) = R^{\!\top}\,\mathbf{K}_{0}^{-1}\,(u, v, 1)^{\!\top}$
the world-frame ray direction, the ray
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:backproj}
    \mathbf{P}_{w}(\tau)
    \;=\; \mathbf{C}_{w} + \tau\,\mathbf{d}(\mathbf{u})
\end{equation}
meets the floor at
$\tau^{\star} = -[\mathbf{C}_{w}]_{y}/[\mathbf{d}(\mathbf{u})]_{y}$.
Let $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^{2}$ be the unit flow direction in
the world $xz$-plane ($\mathbf{v} = (1, 0)$ for our apparatus), and
let $x^{\mathrm{front}}_{\mathrm{box}}$ be the container's
front-wall position read from the experiment manifest. The flow
distance at frame $t_{i}$ is the farthest signed projection of
$\partial\Omega_{i}$ onto $\mathbf{v}$ relative to that front wall,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:xi}
    x_{i} \;=\;
    \max_{\mathbf{u} \in \partial\Omega_{i}}
    \Bigl[\mathbf{v}^{\!\top}
          \bigl(\mathbf{P}_{w}(\tau^{\star}(\mathbf{u}))\bigr)_{xz}\Bigr]
    \;-\; x^{\mathrm{front}}_{\mathrm{box}}.
\end{equation}
Finally, residual binarisation noise can introduce occasional
backward jumps across frames; we guard against this with a monotone
pass $x_{i} \leftarrow \max(x_{i}, x_{i-1})$ (equivalent to
Eq.~\eqref{eq:mono} applied in physical space), producing the
observation
$\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}} = (x_{1}, \dots, x_{8})
\in \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}^{8}$ that the inverse of
Algorithms~\ref{alg:invert} and~\ref{alg:joint} consumes.

\subsection{Inverse Video Rheometry as Simulation-in-the-Loop Optimization}

Given an observation $\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$ captured from a real
experiment at geometry $(W, H)$, video rheometry recovers
$\boldsymbol{\theta}$ by matching simulator output against the
observation,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:inverse-generic}
    \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}
    \;=\;
    \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}\in\Theta}\;
    \mathcal{L}\bigl(\boldsymbol{\theta};\,
    \mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}},\,W,\,H\bigr),
\end{equation}
where
$\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \|\mathcal{S}(\boldsymbol{\theta};\,W,H)
- \mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}\|^{2}$ is an observation-matching loss.
Because $\mathcal{S}$ is defined by an MPM integration,
$\mathcal{L}$ is expensive---several minutes per query on modern
GPU hardware---and non-smooth through the plastic return map. The
simulation-in-the-loop formulation of~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}
evaluates $\mathcal{S}$ directly inside a derivative-free optimiser,
paying the cost of a full MPM simulation at every candidate
$\boldsymbol{\theta}$.

The loss landscape carries a well-known identifiability pathology.
Define the \emph{characteristic shear rate} that separates the
yield-dominated from the viscous-dominated regime,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gammastar}
    \dot\gamma^{\star}(\sigma_Y, \eta, n)
    \;=\; \bigl(\sigma_Y / \eta\bigr)^{1/n}.
\end{equation}
Rewriting Eq.~\eqref{eq:hb-scalar} as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hb-factored}
    \tau(\dot\gamma)
    \;=\; \eta\,\dot\gamma^{\,n}\left[\,1
    \;+\; \left(\frac{\dot\gamma^{\star}}{\dot\gamma}\right)^{\!n}\,\right],
\end{equation}
shows that as $\dot\gamma / \dot\gamma^{\star} \to \infty$ the
second bracket tends to $1$ and the stress becomes asymptotically
governed by $(\eta, n)$ alone; formally
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ridge}
    \left.
      \frac{\partial \tau}{\partial \sigma_Y}
    \right/\!
    \left.
      \frac{\partial \tau}{\partial \eta}
    \right|_{\dot\gamma \gg \dot\gamma^{\star}}
    \;=\;
    \frac{1}{\dot\gamma^{\,n}}
    \;\longrightarrow\; 0.
\end{equation}
If the observed dam-break regime lies largely above
$\dot\gamma^{\star}$, the observation $\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$
becomes effectively insensitive to $\sigma_Y$ while still constraining
$(\eta, n)$, producing a continuum of nearly-equal-loss solutions
that trade $\sigma_Y$ for a compensating increase in $\eta$.
Hamamichi et al.~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} identify this
degeneracy by dimensional-similarity analysis and propose joint
inversion over multiple $(W, H)$ setups as the mitigation; however,
under their simulation-in-the-loop cost, multi-setup inversion is
$N$ times more expensive than the already hours-long single-setup
inverse.

Finally, in many practical scenarios the target material itself
evolves during the measurement window (sedimentation, aging,
temperature drift, mixing history, cooking cooldown), so that the
true parameter state $\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t}$ drifts during the
inverse. A delayed estimate may therefore no longer correspond to the
current material condition, motivating estimation latency short
enough to track the evolving state rather than merely reduce
amortised compute cost.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Overview}

Our goal is to compress the inverse of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:inverse-generic} from hours to seconds without altering
the physics of the forward model. We replace expensive MPM queries
with predictions from a trained hierarchical Mixture-of-GP surrogate
$\tilde{\mathcal{S}}$, and augment the derivative-free inverse with
two pieces of problem-specific structure---an observation-space
warm-start and a training-data-anchored prior---that together resolve
the identifiability degeneracy discussed in \S3.3. The pipeline has
three phases, summarised in Figure~\ref{fig:overview}. In the
\emph{offline} phase we generate a library of MPM training runs and
fit the hierarchical surrogate (\S\ref{sec:surrogate},
Algorithm~\ref{alg:train}). In the \emph{online forward} phase
each query $(\boldsymbol{\theta}, W, H)$ is routed through the
hierarchical gate to a specialised expert and predicted in
milliseconds (Algorithm~\ref{alg:forward}). In the
\emph{online inverse} phase each observation is routed to its
expert and the surrogate is driven by an identifiability-aware
CMA-ES loop (\S\ref{sec:inverse}, Algorithm~\ref{alg:invert}
for a single observation; Algorithm~\ref{alg:joint} for the joint
multi-setup variant that implements the paper's
\emph{two-stage} protocol of \S\ref{sec:multi}). The two-stage
variant---used for the real-fluid validation of
\S\ref{sec:rheometer}---runs a single-setup inverse on one
observation to obtain an anchor $\boldsymbol{\theta}_{1}$, then
re-optimises across all available setups with a stronger
log-$\sigma_{Y}$ prior centred on that anchor.

\subsection{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:problem}

We frame the entire paper around a single inverse problem and its
two successively more tractable forms. The canonical
simulation-in-the-loop inverse
of~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} reads
\begin{taggedeqn}{P}{P}
    \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}
    \;=\;
    \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}\in\Theta}
    \bigl\|\mathcal{S}(\boldsymbol{\theta};\,W, H)
           - \mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}\bigr\|^{2},
\end{taggedeqn}
and leaves three practical difficulties unaddressed:
\emph{(i)}~each evaluation of $\mathcal{S}$ requires a full MPM
integration, $O(\mathrm{minutes})$ per query;
\emph{(ii)}~the map $\mathcal{S}(\cdot;W,H)$ changes morphologically
with container geometry and discretely across the yield threshold,
so a single global surrogate or a single optimisation trajectory
cannot cover $\Theta$ at the accuracy required for inversion; and
\emph{(iii)}~the loss surface carries a $(\sigma_Y, \eta)$ ridge
whenever the observed shear-rate range is dominated by
$\eta\,\dot\gamma^{\,n}$ (\S3.3).

We transform (\ref{eq:P}) into the surrogate-driven form
\begin{taggedeqn}{P$'$}{Pprime}
    \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}
    \;=\;
    \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}\in\Theta_{k^{\star}}}\,
    \underbrace{\frac{\|\tilde{\mathcal{S}}(\boldsymbol{\theta};W,H)
                       - \mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}\|^{2}}
                      {\overline{\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}^{\,2}}}}_{\mathrm{NMSE}}
    \;+\;
    \lambda\!\left(\frac{\log\sigma_Y - \log\sigma_Y^{\mathrm{NN}}}
                        {w}\right)^{\!2},
\end{taggedeqn}
through four substitutions, each addressing a specific difficulty
above; Table~\ref{tab:subs} summarises the mapping and points to the
subsection that supplies each piece of machinery.

\begin{table}[!htbp]
\centering
\small

(\ref{eq:P}) into the tractable form~(\ref{eq:Pprime}).}
\label{tab:subs}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
Difficulty & Substitution & Section \\
\midrule
(i) Forward cost
  & $\mathcal{S} \mapsto \tilde{\mathcal{S}}$
    (hierarchical MoGP-rBCM)
  & \S\ref{sec:surrogate} \\
(ii) Non-stationarity
  & $\Theta \mapsto \Theta_{k^{\star}}$

  & $\|\cdot\|^{2} \mapsto \mathrm{NMSE}$
  & \S\ref{sec:inverse} \\
(iii) Identifiability
  & add $\lambda\!\left(\tfrac{\log\sigma_Y -

  & \S\ref{sec:inverse} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

In~(\ref{eq:Pprime}), the learned surrogate
$\tilde{\mathcal{S}}$ is a hierarchical Mixture-of-GP with robust
Bayesian Committee Machine aggregation (\S\ref{sec:surrogate}); the
anchor $\sigma_Y^{\mathrm{NN}}$ is the yield stress of the nearest
training neighbour in observation space
(\S\ref{sec:inverse}); the box $\Theta_{k^{\star}}$ is the
axis-aligned hull of that neighbour's routed-expert training inputs;
and the prior coefficients are $\lambda = 10^{-5}$, $w = 1$
(log-std).

When multiple observations
$\{(W_{s}, H_{s}, \mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}^{(s)})\}_{s=1}^{S}$
of the same material are available---a regime that
\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} addresses with Hessian-guided
active setup selection and a sequential $1\to 2\to 3\to 4$ CMA-ES
sweep gated by the per-iterate MPM cost---our surrogate instead
permits all $S$ setups to be combined in a single joint objective,
yielding
\begin{taggedeqn}{P$^{\star}$}{Pstar}
\begin{aligned}
    \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}
    \;=\;
    &\arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}\in\bigcap_{s}\Theta_{k_{s}^{\star}}}\,
    \sum_{s=1}^{S}\,
    \frac{\|\tilde{\mathcal{S}}(\boldsymbol{\theta}; W_{s}, H_{s})
          - \mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}^{(s)}\|^{2}}
         {\overline{(\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}^{(s)})^{2}}}
    \\
    &\qquad\;+\;
    \lambda\!\left(\frac{\log\sigma_Y - \log\sigma_Y^{\mathrm{NN}}}
                        {w}\right)^{\!2}.
\end{aligned}
\end{taggedeqn}
Sharing $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ across setups while each setup is
routed through its own expert tightens
(\ref{eq:Pstar}) along the $\sigma_Y$ axis in exact proportion to the
information the additional setups contribute
(\S\ref{sec:multi}).

The remainder of this section describes the machinery needed to
solve (\ref{eq:Pprime}) and (\ref{eq:Pstar}) at seconds-per-video
latency: the input and output representation (\S\ref{sec:repr}), the
hierarchical MoGP-rBCM surrogate (\S\ref{sec:surrogate}), the
identifiability-aware inverse (\S\ref{sec:inverse}), the algorithm
pseudocode (\S\ref{sec:algs}), and the training data and frozen
configuration that produces a single reusable checkpoint
(\S\ref{sec:data}).


\subsection{Input and Output Representation}
\label{sec:repr}

Each training sample pairs a parameter-geometry vector with its
forward observation
$\mathbf{y} = \mathcal{S}(\boldsymbol{\theta}; W, H) \in
\mathbb{R}^{8}$. We assemble the five-dimensional input
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x} \;=\; (n,\,\eta,\,\sigma_Y,\,W,\,H)^{\!\top}
    \in \mathbb{R}^{5},
\end{equation}
and apply per-component log transforms to $\eta$ and $\sigma_Y$
(which span $5.5$ and $5.6$ orders of magnitude respectively),
followed by per-dimension $z$-score standardisation,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:xscale}
    \tilde{\mathbf{x}}
    \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{x}_{\log} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_X}{\boldsymbol{\sigma}_X},
    \qquad
    \mathbf{x}_{\log}
    = \bigl(n,\;\log(\eta + \varepsilon),\;
    \log(\sigma_Y + \varepsilon),\;W,\;H\bigr)^{\!\top},
\end{equation}
with $\varepsilon = 10^{-8}$. For outputs we subtract the training
mean but keep the raw scale, preserving the monotone ordering in
physical units:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:yscale}
    \tilde{\mathbf{y}} \;=\; \mathbf{y} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_Y.
\end{equation}
The statistics
$(\boldsymbol{\mu}_X, \boldsymbol{\sigma}_X, \boldsymbol{\mu}_Y)$ are
fit once on the full training set and frozen.


\subsection{Hierarchical Mixture-of-GP Surrogate}
\label{sec:surrogate}

A single global GP cannot cover the $5.5$--$5.6$-order-of-magnitude
input box at the accuracy required for inversion: the dam-break
morphology changes discretely with container geometry and again
across the yield threshold, so the input-to-output map is not locally
stationary. We therefore partition the space hierarchically and fit a
specialised expert per partition cell.

\paragraph{Two-level Bayesian Gaussian mixture gate.}
The gate is a hard, top-1 routing over
$\sum_{g} K_{\phi}^{(g)}$ partition cells. The level-1 gate models
the density of the standardised geometry subspace as a Bayesian
Gaussian mixture with a Dirichlet-process weight prior and full
$2 \times 2$ covariances,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bgm-geo}
    p^{\mathrm{geo}}(W, H)
    \;=\; \sum_{g=1}^{K_{\mathrm{geo}}}
    \pi_{g}^{\mathrm{geo}}\,
    \mathcal{N}\!\bigl((W, H);\,
    \boldsymbol{\mu}_{g}^{\mathrm{geo}},\,
    \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{g}^{\mathrm{geo}}\bigr),
\end{equation}
and routes each query to its maximum-likelihood component,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:level1}
    g^{\star}(W, H)
    \;=\;
    \arg\max_{g \in \{1,\dots,K_{\mathrm{geo}}\}}\,
    \pi_{g}^{\mathrm{geo}}\,
    \mathcal{N}\!\bigl((W, H);\,
    \boldsymbol{\mu}_{g}^{\mathrm{geo}},\,
    \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{g}^{\mathrm{geo}}\bigr).
\end{equation} The level-2 gate further clusters
each geo group by an 18-dimensional observation signature
$\boldsymbol{\phi}$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:level2}
    k^{\star}\bigl(\boldsymbol{\phi}, g^{\star}\bigr)
    \;=\;
    \arg\max_{k \in \{1,\dots,K_{\phi}^{(g^{\star})}\}}\,
    p^{\phi\mid g^{\star}}_{k}\bigl(\boldsymbol{\phi}\bigr).
\end{equation}
We use $K_{\mathrm{geo}} = 12$ globally; the level-2 component count
is chosen per geo group by the Bayesian information criterion,
yielding $K_{\phi}^{(g)} \in \{30, 40, 45, 50\}$ and
$\sum_{g} K_{\phi}^{(g)} = 540$ cells in total (cf.~the hard-partition
GMM-gated pattern of~\cite{meeds2006alternative,nguyen2008local}).

\paragraph{Observation signature.}
The level-2 gate is driven by an 18-dimensional feature
$\boldsymbol{\phi}(\mathbf{y}, W, H)$ that summarises the shape of
the observed flow curve and the container regime:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:phi}
    \boldsymbol{\phi}
    = \Bigl(\,
        \underbrace{\mathbf{y}/y_8}_{8},\;
        \underbrace{\Delta(\mathbf{y}/y_8)}_{7},\;
        \underbrace{\log(y_8 + \varepsilon)}_{1},\;
        \underbrace{\log\!\sqrt{WH}}_{1},\;
        \underbrace{\log(W/H)}_{1}
    \,\Bigr)^{\!\top}\!\!.
\end{equation}
The first eight coordinates normalise the flow curve by its final
displacement, so materials with similar \emph{shape} but different
\emph{magnitude} cluster together; the next seven are first
differences; the last three encode container scale and aspect ratio.
At inverse time the gate uses
$\boldsymbol{\phi}(\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}, W, H)$; during training,
$\mathbf{y}$ is the MPM output.

\paragraph{Per-cell Gaussian-process experts.}
Each partition cell $(g, k)$ owns an exact Gaussian-process expert.
The standardised output is modelled by eight independent
one-dimensional GPs that share the same standardised input
$\tilde{\mathbf{x}}$, each with a Mat\'ern-$5/2$ kernel with
automatic relevance determination
(ARD)~\cite{rasmussen2006gaussian},
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:matern}
    k_{5/2}(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b})
    = \sigma_f^{\,2}\!\left(
    1 + \sqrt{5}\,r + \tfrac{5}{3}r^{2}\right) e^{-\sqrt{5}\,r},
    \qquad
    r^{2}\!=\!\sum_{d=1}^{5}\frac{(a_d - b_d)^{2}}{\ell_d^{\,2}},
\end{equation}
and a constant mean. Given training inputs $\mathbf{X}_{k}$ of
cell $k = (g, k)$ with standardised outputs
$\tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{k,d} \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathbf{X}_{k}|}$ for each
output dimension $d$, the predictive distribution at a query
$\tilde{\mathbf{x}}$ is Gaussian with mean and variance
\begin{align}
\label{eq:gp-predictive}
    \mu_{k,d}(\tilde{\mathbf{x}})
    &= \mathbf{k}_{*}^{\!\top}
       (K_{k} + \sigma_{n,k,d}^{\,2} I)^{-1}\,
       \tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{k,d},
\nonumber\\
    \sigma_{k,d}^{\,2}(\tilde{\mathbf{x}})
    &= k_{5/2}(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}, \tilde{\mathbf{x}})
       - \mathbf{k}_{*}^{\!\top}
       (K_{k} + \sigma_{n,k,d}^{\,2} I)^{-1}\,\mathbf{k}_{*},
\end{align}
where $[\mathbf{k}_{*}]_{i} = k_{5/2}(\tilde{\mathbf{x}},
\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_{k,i})$ and $[K_{k}]_{ij} = k_{5/2}(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_{k,i},
\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_{k,j})$. The kernel variance $\sigma_f^{\,2}$, the
five ARD lengthscales $\{\ell_d\}_{d=1}^{5}$, and the observation
noise $\sigma_{n,k,d}^{\,2}$ are learned per expert and per output
dimension by maximising the exact log marginal likelihood
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gp-mll}
\begin{aligned}
    \log p(\tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{k,d} \mid \mathbf{X}_{k})
    =\;
    &-\tfrac{1}{2}\,
        \tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{k,d}^{\!\top}
        (K_{k} + \sigma_{n,k,d}^{\,2} I)^{-1}
        \tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{k,d}
    \\
    &-\tfrac{1}{2}\log\!\bigl|K_{k} + \sigma_{n,k,d}^{\,2} I\bigr|
    \;-\; \tfrac{|\mathbf{X}_{k}|}{2}\log 2\pi
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
with Adam (learning rate $5\cdot 10^{-2}$, $100$ iterations).
Experts are trained independently in parallel across cells.

\paragraph{Robust Bayesian Committee Machine aggregation.}
Given a query $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}$ routed to an expert set
$\mathcal{R}$, we aggregate per-expert predictive moments
$(\mu_k, \sigma_k^{2})$ via the robust Bayesian Committee
Machine~\cite{deisenroth2015distributed}:
\begin{align}
\label{eq:rbcm}
    \sigma_\star^{-2}
    &= \Bigl(\,1 - \!\!\sum_{k\in\mathcal{R}}\! \beta_k\Bigr)\,
        \sigma_{\mathrm{prior}}^{-2}
       + \sum_{k\in\mathcal{R}}\! \beta_k\,\sigma_k^{-2},
\nonumber\\
    \mu_\star
    &= \sigma_\star^{\,2}\sum_{k\in\mathcal{R}}
       \beta_k\,\sigma_k^{-2}\,\mu_k,
    \qquad
    \beta_k = \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left[\log\sigma_{\mathrm{prior}}^{2}
                                  - \log\sigma_k^{2}\right]_{+},
\end{align}
where $\sigma_{\mathrm{prior}}^{2}$ is the empirical variance of the
expert's training outputs and $[\cdot]_{+}$ clips at zero. In our
top-1 configuration ($|\mathcal{R}| = 1$), rBCM reduces to the
single-expert predictive mean with a variance-weighted calibration;
we retain the full rBCM formula because the same code path supports
soft multi-expert aggregation for ablation
(\S\ref{sec:ablation}).

\paragraph{Polynomial residual correction.}
After all $540$ experts are trained, we fit a small global correction
to further suppress residuals near partition boundaries without
retraining the GPs. For each expert $(g, k)$ and output dimension
$d$, define the raw-parameter degree-$2$ monomial basis
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\psi}(\mathbf{x})
    = \bigl(1,\,n,\,\eta,\,\sigma_Y,\,W,\,H,\,
      n^{2},\,n\eta,\,\ldots,\,H^{2}\bigr)^{\!\top}
      \in \mathbb{R}^{21},
\end{equation}
and solve a ridge regression on the GP residuals,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:poly}
    \mathbf{c}_{(g,k),d}
    \;=\; \arg\min_{\mathbf{c}}\,
    \bigl\|\tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{d} - \mu_{(g,k),d} - \Phi\,\mathbf{c}\bigr\|^{2}
    + \alpha\,\|\mathbf{c}_{\backslash 0}\|^{2},
    \qquad \alpha = 10^{-3},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{c}_{\backslash 0}$ excludes the intercept from
regularisation. The closed-form solution is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:poly-closed}
    \mathbf{c}_{(g,k),d}
    \;=\;
    \bigl(\boldsymbol{\Psi}^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{\Psi} + \alpha\,\mathbf{D}\bigr)^{\!-1}\,
    \boldsymbol{\Psi}^{\!\top}\!\bigl(\tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{d} - \mu_{(g,k),d}\bigr),
    \qquad
    \mathbf{D} = \mathrm{diag}(0, 1, \dots, 1).
\end{equation}
At inference the corrected mean is
$\mu_{(g,k),d} + \boldsymbol{\psi}(\mathbf{x})^{\!\top}\mathbf{c}_{(g,k),d}$.

\paragraph{Monotonic post-processing.}
The dam-break observation is non-decreasing by construction, but
aggregation and numerical noise can introduce mild violations. After
inverse-standardisation we enforce monotonicity by a cumulative
maximum in physical space,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mono}
    \hat{y}_{d}
    \;\leftarrow\; \max_{j\le d}\,\hat{y}_{j},
    \qquad d = 1,\dots,8.
\end{equation}
We denote the full surrogate pipeline
(standardise $\to$ route $\to$ aggregate $\to$ correct $\to$
de-standardise $\to$ monotonise) by
$\tilde{\mathcal{S}}(\boldsymbol{\theta}; W, H)$.


\subsection{Identifiability-Aware Inverse}
\label{sec:inverse}

Replacing $\mathcal{S}$ with $\tilde{\mathcal{S}}$ in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:inverse-generic} reduces per-candidate cost from
minutes to milliseconds, but does not by itself resolve the
identifiability ridge of \S3.3. A vanilla CMA-ES running on the
surrogate can still slide to $\sigma_Y \to 0$ while inflating $\eta$
to match the observation. We inject three pieces of problem
structure.

\paragraph{Nearest-neighbour warm-start in observation space.}
Let $\mathcal{D}_{k^{\star}}$ denote the training set of the routed
expert. We initialise CMA-ES at
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:nn}
    \boldsymbol{\theta}^{\mathrm{NN}}
    \;=\; \boldsymbol{\theta}_{i^{\star}},
    \qquad
    i^{\star}
    \;=\;
    \arg\min_{i\,\in\,\mathcal{D}_{k^{\star}}}\;
    \frac{\|\mathbf{y}_i - \mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}\|^{2}}
         {\max\!\bigl(\overline{\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}^{\,2}},\,10^{-12}\bigr)},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{y}_i$ is the monotonised flow curve of training
sample $i$ and the denominator normalises by observation magnitude.
Since $\boldsymbol{\theta}^{\mathrm{NN}}$ is a training point, its
$\sigma_Y$ component lies on the data manifold, giving CMA-ES an
initial $\sigma_Y$ that is physically plausible rather than arbitrary.

\paragraph{Data-honest log-$\sigma_Y$ prior.}
The second additive term of~(\ref{eq:Pprime}) is a soft quadratic
prior on $\log\sigma_Y$ anchored at $\sigma_Y^{\mathrm{NN}}$, with
coefficients $\lambda = 10^{-5}$ and $w = 1$ (log-std). A factor of
$e$ deviation from $\sigma_Y^{\mathrm{NN}}$ contributes $10^{-5}$ to
the loss---on the same order as a typical converged NMSE---so the
prior becomes active precisely at the physical identifiability scale.
It is \emph{data-honest} in that its centre $\sigma_Y^{\mathrm{NN}}$
is a training sample of $\mathcal{D}_{k^{\star}}$, not a
user-specified guess. Removing the prior inflates $\sigma_Y$ error by
roughly an order of magnitude on low-flow cases; over-weighting it
($\lambda \gtrsim 10^{-3}$) pulls $\sigma_Y$ directly to
$\sigma_Y^{\mathrm{NN}}$ and overrides the data term
(ablation in \S\ref{sec:ablation}).

\paragraph{Per-expert bound tightening.}
We restrict CMA-ES search to the axis-aligned bounding box of the
routed expert's training inputs (plus a $2\%$ log-space pad on
$\eta$ and $\sigma_Y$, linear pad on $n$), intersected with the
global bounds $\Theta$. Let $\theta_d^{\,k^{\star},\min}$ and
$\theta_d^{\,k^{\star},\max}$ denote the per-dimension extrema of
$\mathcal{D}_{k^{\star}}$ along axis $d \in \{n, \eta, \sigma_Y\}$.
The tightened search box is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tight-box}
    \Theta_{k^{\star}}
    \;=\;
    \Theta
    \;\cap\;
    \left(
    \bigotimes_{d}\!\left[\,
      \theta_d^{\,k^{\star},\min}\!\cdot(1 - p_d),\;
      \theta_d^{\,k^{\star},\max}\!\cdot(1 + p_d)
    \,\right]\right),
\end{equation}
with $p_{\eta} = p_{\sigma_Y} = 0.02$ applied in log-space and
$p_{n} = 0.02$ applied linearly. This prevents the optimiser from
entering regions where the expert has no support and would
extrapolate into spurious near-zero-flow minima.

\paragraph{CMA-ES configuration.}
We use CMA-ES~\cite{hansen2016cma,bajaj2019gaussian} with a small
log-space population, initial step size $\sigma_{0} = 0.5$, and
early stopping on CMA's tol-x / tol-f criteria under a bounded
maximum-generation budget. The benchmark configuration used for our
headline synthetic numbers in \S\ref{sec:mainresults} runs with
population $P = 12$ and $T_{\mathrm{cma}} = 60$ (up to $720$
surrogate evaluations); a user-facing single-setup configuration
uses the looser $P = 16$ and $T_{\mathrm{cma}} = 700$, which rarely
matters in practice because early stopping fires well before the
budget. Parameter bounds are
enforced through CMA's boundary handler. On a single consumer GPU,
the full single-setup inverse---routing, NN warm-start, the bounded
CMA loop, and the final forward---takes a median of $12$\,s per
video; the joint multi-setup variant of \S\ref{sec:multi} roughly
doubles this cost (Algorithm~\ref{alg:joint}).

\paragraph{Joint multi-setup inverse.}
When several observations of the same material are available, we
solve (\ref{eq:Pstar}) in two stages. Stage~1 runs
Algorithm~\ref{alg:invert} on a single nominated setup to obtain a
well-scaled anchor $\boldsymbol{\theta}_{1}$. Stage~2 re-routes each
setup through the hierarchical gate independently, intersects the
per-expert boxes to form a joint search region
$\Theta_{\mathrm{joint}} = \Theta \cap \bigcap_{s}
\Theta_{k_{s}^{\star}}$, and runs CMA-ES on the
summed-NMSE objective (\ref{eq:Pstar}) anchored at $\sigma_Y^{(1)}$
with a heavier prior weight $\lambda_{\mathrm{joint}} = 5\cdot 10^{-2}$.
The stronger anchor is justified because $\boldsymbol{\theta}_{1}$
is itself already data-driven; the prior term now plays the role of
a \emph{stage-coupling regulariser} rather than a physical
identifiability prior.


\subsection{Algorithms}
\label{sec:algs}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:train} summarises offline construction of the
surrogate. Algorithm~\ref{alg:forward} is the online forward pass.
Algorithm~\ref{alg:invert} is the single-setup inverse; it solves
(\ref{eq:Pprime}).
Algorithm~\ref{alg:joint} is the two-stage joint multi-setup
inverse; it solves (\ref{eq:Pstar}) and is the configuration used
for our real-fluid validation in \S\ref{sec:rheometer}.

\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
\caption{Offline construction of the HVIMoGP-rBCM surrogate.}
\label{alg:train}
\KwData{Training corpus
  $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{all}}=\{(\boldsymbol{\theta}_{i}, W_{i}, H_{i},
  \mathbf{y}_{i})\}_{i=1}^{N}$, $N=295{,}419$.}
\KwIn{$K_{\mathrm{geo}}=12$; $\{K_{\phi}^{(g)}\}_{g=1}^{12}$ from
  per-geo BIC scan (Eqs.~\eqref{eq:bgm-geo},\,\eqref{eq:level2});
  expert training budget $T_{\mathrm{gp}}=100$, learning rate
  $5\cdot 10^{-2}$; cell size thresholds
  $N_{\min}=30$, $N_{\max}=2500$; polynomial degree $2$, ridge
  $\alpha=10^{-3}$; hotfix threshold $\tau_{\mathrm{mae}}=0.15$.}
\KwOut{Frozen checkpoint: scalers, two-level BGM gate, $540$
  per-cell GP experts, polynomial residual coefficients.}
\BlankLine

\tcp{\bf (1) Normalisation.}
Compute $(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{X}, \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{X})$ on
  $\{\mathbf{x}_{\log, i}\}$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:xscale})
  and $\boldsymbol{\mu}_{Y}$ on $\{\mathbf{y}_{i}\}$
  (Eq.~\eqref{eq:yscale})\;

\tcp{\bf (2) Level-1 Bayesian Gaussian mixture on geometry.}
Fit $p^{\mathrm{geo}}$ (DP weight prior, full covariance,
  $K=K_{\mathrm{geo}}$) on standardised $\{(W_{i},H_{i})\}$
  \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:bgm-geo}}
Assign each training point
  $g_{i}\leftarrow g^{\star}(W_{i}, H_{i})$
  via Eq.~\eqref{eq:level1}\;

\tcp{\bf (3) Per-geo level-2 BGM on observation signatures.}
\ForEach{geo group $g\in\{1,\dots,K_{\mathrm{geo}}\}$}{
  Compute
    $\boldsymbol{\phi}_{i}=\boldsymbol{\phi}(\mathbf{y}_{i},W_{i},H_{i})$
    for $i\in g$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:phi})\;
  Fit $p^{\phi\mid g}$ (DP weight prior, full covariance,
    $K=K_{\phi}^{(g)}$) on standardised
    $\{\boldsymbol{\phi}_{i}\}_{i\in g}$\;
  Assign $k_{i}\leftarrow k^{\star}(\boldsymbol{\phi}_{i},g)$
    via Eq.~\eqref{eq:level2}\;
}

\tcp{\bf (4) Train one ExactGP expert per cell.}
\ForEach{cell $(g,k)$}{
  $\mathcal{D}_{(g,k)}\leftarrow\{i:g_{i}=g\land k_{i}=k\}$\;
  \If{$|\mathcal{D}_{(g,k)}|<N_{\min}$}{skip cell (absorbed by
    neighbour at inference)\;}
  \If{$|\mathcal{D}_{(g,k)}|>N_{\max}$}{stratified subsample
    to $N_{\max}$ rows\;}
  \For{$d=1,\dots,D_{Y}=8$}{
    Initialise Mat\'ern-$5/2$ ARD kernel hyperparameters
      $(\sigma_{f}^{\,2},\{\ell_{\delta}\}_{\delta=1}^{5},
      \sigma_{n,k,d}^{\,2})$ and constant mean
      (Eq.~\eqref{eq:matern})\;
    Maximise the exact log-marginal likelihood
      (Eq.~\eqref{eq:gp-mll}) by Adam for $T_{\mathrm{gp}}$ iterations\;
  }
}

\tcp{\bf (5) Diagnostic + hotfix for high-MAE experts.}
On a held-out split, compute per-expert forward MAE
  $\bar{m}_{(g,k)}$\;
\ForEach{cell $(g,k)$ with $\bar{m}_{(g,k)}\ge\tau_{\mathrm{mae}}$}{
  Re-train expert $(g,k)$ with an \emph{isotropic-baseline}
    kernel (single shared lengthscale across $d$) for $300$
    iterations to reduce the MAE tail\;
}

\tcp{\bf (6) Polynomial residual correction.}
\ForEach{cell $(g,k)$}{
  Assemble degree-$2$ monomial basis
    $\boldsymbol{\Psi}\in\mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{D}_{(g,k)}|\times 21}$
    on $\mathcal{D}_{(g,k)}$\;
  \For{$d=1,\dots,D_{Y}$}{
    Compute GP residual
      $\mathbf{r}_{(g,k),d}=\tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{d}-\mu_{(g,k),d}$\;
    Solve ridge regression (\ref{eq:poly}) in closed form
      (Eq.~\eqref{eq:poly-closed}) to obtain
      $\mathbf{c}_{(g,k),d}$\;
  }
}

\Return{$(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{X},\boldsymbol{\sigma}_{X},\boldsymbol{\mu}_{Y},
  p^{\mathrm{geo}},\{p^{\phi\mid g}\}_{g=1}^{12},
  \{\mathrm{expert}_{(g,k)}\},\{\mathbf{c}_{(g,k),d}\})$.}
\end{algorithm}


\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
\caption{Online forward pass
  $\hat{\mathbf{y}}=\tilde{\mathcal{S}}(\boldsymbol{\theta};W,H)$.}
\label{alg:forward}
\KwIn{Parameter $\boldsymbol{\theta}=(n,\eta,\sigma_{Y})$, geometry
  $(W,H)$; optional observation signature $\boldsymbol{\phi}$
  (at inverse time derived from $\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$;
  in data-gen mode the MPM output is used instead).}
\KwOut{Predicted flow curve
  $\hat{\mathbf{y}}\in\mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}^{8}$ (monotone
  non-decreasing).}
\BlankLine

\tcp{\bf Input standardisation.}
$\mathbf{x}_{\log}\leftarrow
  (n,\log(\eta+\varepsilon),\log(\sigma_{Y}+\varepsilon),W,H)^{\!\top}$\;
$\tilde{\mathbf{x}}\leftarrow
  (\mathbf{x}_{\log}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_{X})/\boldsymbol{\sigma}_{X}$
  \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:xscale}}

\tcp{\bf Hierarchical top-1 routing.}
$g^{\star}\leftarrow\arg\max_{g}\pi_{g}^{\mathrm{geo}}
  \mathcal{N}\!\bigl((W,H);\boldsymbol{\mu}_{g}^{\mathrm{geo}},
                     \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{g}^{\mathrm{geo}}\bigr)$
  \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:level1}}
$k^{\star}\leftarrow\arg\max_{k}p_{k}^{\phi\mid g^{\star}}(\boldsymbol{\phi})$
  \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:level2}}

\tcp{\bf Expert prediction (eight one-dimensional GPs).}
\For{$d=1,\dots,8$}{
  $(\mu_{k^{\star},d},\sigma_{k^{\star},d}^{\,2})\leftarrow
    \text{GP predictive for expert }(g^{\star},k^{\star})$ at
    $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}$
    \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:gp-predictive}}
}

\tcp{\bf rBCM aggregation with top-1 routing.}
$(\mu_{\star,d},\sigma_{\star,d}^{\,2})\leftarrow
  (\mu_{k^{\star},d},\sigma_{k^{\star},d}^{\,2})$
  \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:rbcm} with $|\mathcal{R}|=1$}

\tcp{\bf Polynomial residual correction (if the cell was hotfixed).}
$\mu_{\star,d}\leftarrow
  \mu_{\star,d}+\boldsymbol{\psi}(\mathbf{x})^{\!\top}\mathbf{c}_{(g^{\star},k^{\star}),d}$
  \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:poly}}

\tcp{\bf De-standardise + enforce monotonicity.}
$\hat{y}_{d}\leftarrow\mu_{\star,d}+\mu_{Y,d}$ for $d=1,\dots,8$\;
$\hat{y}_{d}\leftarrow\max_{j\le d}\hat{y}_{j}$
  \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:mono}}

\Return{$\hat{\mathbf{y}}=(\hat{y}_{1},\dots,\hat{y}_{8})$.}
\end{algorithm}


\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
\caption{Online single-setup inverse
  $(\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}},W,H)\mapsto\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$.
  Solves~(\ref{eq:Pprime}).}
\label{alg:invert}
\KwIn{Observation $\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}\in\mathbb{R}^{8}$,
  geometry $(W,H)$, trained surrogate $\tilde{\mathcal{S}}$.
  CMA-ES: popsize $P=12$, $\sigma_{0}=0.5$,
  $T_{\mathrm{cma}}=60$ with tol-x / tol-f early stopping.
  Prior: $\lambda=10^{-5}$, $w=1$.
  Bound pad: $p_{\eta}=p_{\sigma_{Y}}=p_{n}=0.02$.}
\KwOut{$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}=(\hat{n},\hat{\eta},
  \hat{\sigma}_{Y})$ and forward prediction $\hat{\mathbf{y}}$.}
\BlankLine

\tcp{\bf (1) Compute observation signature, route through gate.}
$\boldsymbol{\phi}\leftarrow
  \boldsymbol{\phi}(\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}},W,H)$
  \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:phi}}
$(g^{\star},k^{\star})\leftarrow
  \text{hierarchical route}(W,H,\boldsymbol{\phi})$
  \tcp*{Eqs.~\eqref{eq:level1},\eqref{eq:level2}}

\tcp{\bf (2) Per-expert bound tightening.}
Compute
  $\theta_{d}^{\,k^{\star},\min},\theta_{d}^{\,k^{\star},\max}$
  for $d\in\{n,\eta,\sigma_{Y}\}$
  over $\mathcal{D}_{k^{\star}}$\;
$\Theta_{k^{\star}}\leftarrow\Theta\cap\bigotimes_{d}
  [\theta_{d}^{\,k^{\star},\min}(1-p_{d}),\,
   \theta_{d}^{\,k^{\star},\max}(1+p_{d})]$
  \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:tight-box}}

\tcp{\bf (3) Observation-space nearest-neighbour warm-start.}
$i^{\star}\leftarrow\arg\min_{i\in\mathcal{D}_{k^{\star}}}
  \|\mathbf{y}_{i}-\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}\|^{2}/
  \max(\overline{\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}^{\,2}},10^{-12})$
  \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:nn}}
$\boldsymbol{\theta}^{\mathrm{NN}}
  \leftarrow\boldsymbol{\theta}_{i^{\star}}$,\quad
$\sigma_{Y}^{\mathrm{NN}}\leftarrow
  [\boldsymbol{\theta}^{\mathrm{NN}}]_{\sigma_{Y}}$\;

\tcp{\bf (4) Loss oracle.}
Define
  $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}:\boldsymbol{\theta}\mapsto
    \frac{\|\tilde{\mathcal{S}}(\boldsymbol{\theta};W,H)
         -\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}\|^{2}}
         {\overline{\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}^{\,2}}}
    +\lambda
    \left(\tfrac{\log\sigma_{Y}-\log\sigma_{Y}^{\mathrm{NN}}}{w}\right)^{\!2}$
    \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:Pprime}}

\tcp{\bf (5) Bounded CMA-ES search.}
Initialise CMA-ES on $\Theta_{k^{\star}}$ with
  $\mathbf{x}_{0}=\boldsymbol{\theta}^{\mathrm{NN}}$,
  step size $\sigma_{0}$, popsize $P$\;
\For{$t=1,\dots,T_{\mathrm{cma}}$}{
  Sample $\{\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(p)}\}_{p=1}^{P}\subset\Theta_{k^{\star}}$\;
  Batch-evaluate $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(p)})$
    through $\tilde{\mathcal{S}}$\;
  Update CMA-ES mean, covariance, step size\;
  \If{tol-x or tol-f satisfied}{break\;}
}
$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\leftarrow$ best-ever sample\;
$\hat{\mathbf{y}}\leftarrow\tilde{\mathcal{S}}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}};W,H)$\;

\Return{$(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}},\hat{\mathbf{y}})$.}
\end{algorithm}


\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
\caption{Online joint multi-setup inverse
  $\{(\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}^{(s)},W_{s},H_{s})\}_{s=1}^{S}
  \mapsto\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$.
  Solves~(\ref{eq:Pstar}).}
\label{alg:joint}
\KwIn{$S\ge 2$ observations of the \emph{same} material under
  different geometries. Optional stage-$1$ result
  $(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{1},\sigma_{Y}^{(1)})$; otherwise
  obtained by running Algorithm~\ref{alg:invert}.
  Joint prior:
  $\lambda_{\mathrm{joint}}=5\cdot 10^{-2}$ (with a stage-$1$
  anchor) or $10^{-3}$ (fallback), $w=1$. CMA-ES settings as in
  Algorithm~\ref{alg:invert}.}
\KwOut{$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ shared across all $S$ setups.}
\BlankLine

\tcp{\bf Stage 1: single-setup inverse on setup~$1$.}
\If{$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{1}$ not supplied}{
  $(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{1},\hat{\mathbf{y}}_{1})\leftarrow
    \textbf{Algorithm}~\ref{alg:invert}
    (\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}^{(1)},W_{1},H_{1})$\;
}
$\sigma_{Y}^{\mathrm{anchor}}\leftarrow
  [\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{1}]_{\sigma_{Y}}$\;

\tcp{\bf Per-setup routing and per-setup tightened boxes.}
\ForEach{$s=1,\dots,S$}{
  $\boldsymbol{\phi}_{s}\leftarrow
    \boldsymbol{\phi}(\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}^{(s)},W_{s},H_{s})$\;
  $(g_{s}^{\star},k_{s}^{\star})\leftarrow
    \text{route}(W_{s},H_{s},\boldsymbol{\phi}_{s})$\;
  $\Theta_{k_{s}^{\star}}\leftarrow$ per-expert hull
    (Eq.~\eqref{eq:tight-box})\;
}
$\Theta_{\mathrm{joint}}\leftarrow
  \Theta\cap\bigcap_{s=1}^{S}\Theta_{k_{s}^{\star}}$\;
\If{$\Theta_{\mathrm{joint}}$ degenerate}{
  $\Theta_{\mathrm{joint}}\leftarrow\Theta$
  \tcp*{fallback to global bounds}
}

\tcp{\bf Joint loss (Eq.~\eqref{eq:Pstar}).}
Define
  $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_{\mathrm{joint}}:\boldsymbol{\theta}\mapsto
    \sum_{s=1}^{S}
    \frac{\|\tilde{\mathcal{S}}(\boldsymbol{\theta};W_{s},H_{s})
         -\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}^{(s)}\|^{2}}
         {\overline{(\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}^{(s)})^{2}}}
    +\lambda_{\mathrm{joint}}
    \left(\tfrac{\log\sigma_{Y}-
                  \log\sigma_{Y}^{\mathrm{anchor}}}{w}\right)^{\!2}$\;

\tcp{\bf Stage 2: CMA-ES over joint loss, anchored at $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{1}$.}
Initialise CMA-ES on $\Theta_{\mathrm{joint}}$ with
  $\mathbf{x}_{0}=\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{1}$, popsize $P$,
  $\sigma_{0}=0.5$\;
\For{$t=1,\dots,T_{\mathrm{cma}}$}{
  Sample $\{\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(p)}\}$; batch-evaluate
    $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_{\mathrm{joint}}$ across all $S$ setups\;
  Update CMA-ES; early stop on tol-x / tol-f\;
}
$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\leftarrow$ best-ever sample\;
$\hat{\mathbf{y}}_{s}\leftarrow
  \tilde{\mathcal{S}}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}};W_{s},H_{s})$
  for $s=1,\dots,S$\;

\Return{$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ and
  $\{\hat{\mathbf{y}}_{s}\}_{s=1}^{S}$.}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

We evaluate HVIMoGP-rBCM and the identifiability-aware inverse
against four questions: (1) how faithfully does the surrogate
approximate the MPM forward model, (2) how accurately does the full
inverse recover $\boldsymbol{\theta} = (\sigma_Y, \eta, n)$ on
held-out samples, (3) what wall-clock reduction does the surrogate
deliver relative to simulation-in-the-loop inversion, and (4) do the
three contributions of \S1---freshness, rheometer-infeasible
materials, and multi-observation precision---hold under experimental
validation?

\paragraph{Datasets.}
We use three evaluation sets. \emph{$T_{\mathrm{sim}}$} contains
$22{,}209$ MPM samples held out from the $295{,}419$-row training
corpus, sampled stratified over
$(n, \log\eta, \log\sigma_Y, W, H)$. \emph{$T_{\mathrm{inv}}$} is a
synthetic benchmark of $30$ materials drawn from within the training
box, each rendered via MPM at three container sizes (total $90$
clips). \emph{$T_{\mathrm{real}}$} consists of six to eight standard
non-Newtonian fluids (honey, ketchup, mayonnaise, shampoo, Carbopol
gel, and a lotion sample) captured on a smartphone in our dam-break
apparatus and accompanied by parallel-plate rheometer sweeps.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
\emph{Baseline A} is the simulation-in-the-loop pipeline
of~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}: identical observation, identical
CMA-ES hyperparameters, but every candidate evaluated by a fresh MPM
run rather than by the surrogate. Baseline~A defines the accuracy
upper bound and wall-time lower bound of the simulation-in-the-loop
family. \emph{Baseline B} is a reduced version of our own pipeline
that isolates the contribution of hierarchical gating and the
identifiability-aware inverse: it uses a flat Bayesian GMM over
$(W, H)$ with $K = 12$ and no second-level $\phi$ gate ($12$ experts
total), top-$2$ hard routing, and a CMA-ES inverse without warm-start
or $\sigma_Y$ prior.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
For the forward surrogate we report per-output mean absolute error
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mae}
    \mathrm{MAE}(\hat{\mathbf{y}}, \mathbf{y})
    \;=\; \frac{1}{D_Y}\sum_{d=1}^{D_Y}|\hat y_d - y_d|,
\end{equation}
coefficient of determination $R^{2} = 1 - \sum_{i}
\|\hat{\mathbf{y}}_i - \mathbf{y}_i\|^2 / \sum_i \|\mathbf{y}_i -
\bar{\mathbf{y}}\|^2$, and the row-max error distribution
($p_{50}, p_{95}, p_{99}, p_{999}$). For the inverse we report
per-parameter relative error
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:relE}
    \mathrm{relE}(\hat\theta_d;\,\theta_d^{\mathrm{gt}})
    \;=\; \frac{|\hat\theta_d - \theta_d^{\mathrm{gt}}|}
               {|\theta_d^{\mathrm{gt}}|},
    \qquad d \in \{n, \eta, \sigma_Y\},
\end{equation}
the forward NMSE at the recovered parameter as a sanity check on
fit, and the per-sample wall-clock. For comparisons against
parallel-plate reference sweeps (\S\ref{sec:rheometer}) we further
report the integrated log-stress error over the experimentally valid
shear-rate interval $\Gamma = [\dot\gamma_{\min}, \dot\gamma_{\max}]$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:E}
    \mathcal{E}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}, \boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{gt}})
    \;=\;
    \left(\frac{1}{|\Gamma|}
    \int_{\Gamma}
      \bigl[\log\hat\tau(\dot\gamma)
          - \log\tau_{\mathrm{gt}}(\dot\gamma)\bigr]^{2}
    \,d\log\dot\gamma\right)^{\!1/2}.
\end{equation}
All inverse runs use the frozen hyperparameters of
Table~\ref{tab:hyper} and a fixed seed.


\subsection{Surrogate Fidelity}
\label{sec:fidelity}

We first verify that the trained surrogate predicts MPM outputs
accurately enough to justify replacing the simulator inside the
inverse. On $T_{\mathrm{sim}}$, the full HVIMoGP-rBCM surrogate
attains aggregate MAE $0.042$\,cm and $R^2 > 0.999$ across all
eight flow-front dimensions. Errors concentrate near partition
boundaries of the hierarchical gate---where a slightly mis-routed
query lands between specialised experts---and in sparsely sampled
regions of the input box. The degree-$2$ polynomial residual
correction (Eq.~\eqref{eq:poly}) is specifically designed to suppress
the former, reducing the $p_{999}$ row-max error by roughly a factor
of two.

\subsection{Ablations}
\label{sec:ablation}

We ablate the three design choices that distinguish our inverse from
a vanilla CMA-ES-on-GP: the aggregation scheme, the
nearest-neighbour warm-start, and the data-honest $\sigma_Y$ prior.
All rows in Table~\ref{tab:ablation} use identical gate routing,
identical experts, the same $10$-sample inverse set, and the frozen
CMA-ES budget ($12\times 25 = 300$ surrogate calls).

Three findings.

\textbf{rBCM outperforms GRBCM at top-$1$ routing.}
GRBCM~\cite{liu2018generalized} augments our aggregation with an
additional geometry-wide baseline expert indexed by $0$ with
predictive moments $(\mu_0, \sigma_0^{2})$:
\begin{align}
\label{eq:grbcm}
    \sigma_{\mathrm{GRBCM}}^{-2}
    &= \sigma_0^{-2}
       + \sum_{k \in \mathcal{R}} \beta_k\,\sigma_k^{-2},
\nonumber\\
    \mu_{\mathrm{GRBCM}}
    &= \sigma_{\mathrm{GRBCM}}^{\,2}\!
       \left[\sigma_0^{-2}\,\mu_0
           + \sum_{k \in \mathcal{R}}
               \beta_k\,\sigma_k^{-2}\,\mu_k\right].
\end{align}
Computing $(\mu_0, \sigma_0^2)$ requires a second GP query per
sample, doubling inference cost ($21.6$\,s vs.\ $12.0$\,s). On our
peaked gate posterior the baseline blend contributes no independent
information and slightly worsens forward NMSE. We therefore adopt
pure rBCM with top-$1$ routing, which in our setting reduces
Eq.~\eqref{eq:rbcm} to the single-expert predictive mean with
calibrated variance.

\textbf{Pure NN is the best $\sigma_Y$ estimator but cannot match the
flow curve.} The nearest-neighbour alone---returning
$\boldsymbol{\theta}^{\mathrm{NN}}$ without CMA refinement---recovers
$\sigma_Y$ with median relative error $0.168$, the best of all
configurations, because $\boldsymbol{\theta}^{\mathrm{NN}}$ lies on
the data manifold by construction. However, its forward NMSE
($3.9\times 10^{-3}$, two to three orders of magnitude worse than
any CMA-refined variant) shows that pure NN does not reproduce the
observation and is therefore useful only as an initialiser.

\textbf{The $\sigma_Y$ prior closes the gap that CMA-ES opens.}
Without the prior, rBCM with warm-start CMA-ES slides along the
identifiability ridge of \S3.3 to
$\mathrm{relE}\,\sigma_Y = 0.459$. Anchoring at
$\sigma_Y^{\mathrm{NN}}$ with $\lambda = 10^{-5}$ recovers
$\mathrm{relE}\,\sigma_Y = 0.298$---a $35\%$ improvement---at the
cost of a forward NMSE increase from $9.5\times 10^{-6}$ to
$1.4\times 10^{-5}$, both far below any perceptually meaningful
discrepancy. The prior trades a negligible amount of fit for a
large gain in identifiability.

\paragraph{Gate granularity.}
We additionally sweep the level-$1$ component count
$K_{\mathrm{geo}} \in \{1, 4, 12, 24\}$ and the level-$2$ count
$K_{\phi}^{(g)} \in \{30, 45\}$. $K_{\mathrm{geo}} = 1$ collapses to
a $\phi$-only single-level gate and loses roughly $2\times$
aggregate MAE relative to the two-level gate; $K_{\mathrm{geo}} = 24$
over-fragments the geometry space and starves individual experts of
training data. The BIC-selected per-geo $K_{\phi}^{(g)}$ (summing to
$540$) lies within $5\%$ of the best scanned setting.


\subsection{Parameter Freshness for Time-Varying Fluids}
\label{sec:freshness}

To validate contribution~(a) of \S1, we construct a controlled
time-varying experiment in which the true parameter vector
$\boldsymbol{\theta}(t)$ evolves during the measurement window. We
drive a linear drift in $\log\sigma_Y$,
$\log\sigma_Y(t) = \log\sigma_{Y,0} + \alpha\,t$, with $\alpha$
chosen so that $\sigma_Y$ doubles over the duration of one
simulation-in-the-loop inverse. At each sample time $t_k$ we compare
two estimators: (i)~a sim-in-loop estimator that returns
$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ describing the material state at the
\emph{beginning} of its hour-long optimisation, and (ii)~our
surrogate-accelerated estimator that returns
$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ essentially at the measurement instant.
We score both against the true $\boldsymbol{\theta}(t_k)$ in
per-parameter log-relative error.

\subsection{Characterising Rheometer-Infeasible Materials}
\label{sec:chunky}

Contribution~(b) addresses materials that conventional
parallel-plate rheometers cannot measure. We captured six such
fluids on a smartphone in our dam-break apparatus: a chunky okonomi

a sweet chilli sauce, a particle-loaded lotion, and a chuno sauce.

when particle size exceeds a few hundred microns. We evaluate
instead by \emph{sim--real replay}: we run MPM with the recovered
$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ and compare the simulated dam-break

\subsection{Multi-Observation Joint Protocol}
\label{sec:multi}

joint inversion. The inverse objective sums Eq.~\eqref{eq:Pprime}

\subsection{End-to-End Capture-to-Parameter Time Budget}
\label{sec:timebudget}

We close the evaluation by looking at \emph{total} time from raw
phone video to a recovered Herschel--Bulkley triple. Two time sinks
dominate: the manual capture-and-calibration workflow that takes an
experimenter's attention, and the automated inverse that takes
compute. Both are substantially shorter in our pipeline than in the
direct predecessor's.

\paragraph{Manual capture and calibration time.}
Table~\ref{tab:calib-time} breaks the human-in-the-loop portion of
the pipeline into its four steps. The predecessor's camera
calibration goes through \texttt{fSpy} (manual vanishing-point
fitting) followed by \texttt{Blender} (manual alignment and
background render), which together consume nearly $16$\,min of
attention per capture and are the dominant source of manual
variance. Our two-stage ChArUco + OpenGL calibration
(\S\ref{sec:calib}) replaces both steps with a single non-interactive
invocation that completes in $1$\,min\,$12$\,s on a consumer laptop,
saving an average of $14$\,min\,$43$\,s per capture. Video editing
(\texttt{Premiere Pro} frame selection) and mask cleanup
(\texttt{Photoshop}) are unchanged, because they operate on the raw
footage and are orthogonal to the calibration method. End-to-end,
the manual workflow drops from $25$\,min to $10$\,min per capture.

\paragraph{Automated inverse time.}
The compute-side contribution is the CMA-ES inverse of
\S\ref{sec:mainresults}. A single-setup inverse through
Algorithm~\ref{alg:invert} takes a median of $12$\,s per video on a
single consumer GPU (Table~\ref{tab:main}), versus several hours
under the simulation-in-the-loop baseline that runs MPM at every
candidate. A joint two-setup inverse through
Algorithm~\ref{alg:joint} adds a second bounded CMA-ES run on the
summed-NMSE objective of~\eqref{eq:Pstar}, bringing the total to a

multi-setup protocol---proposed

the hours-per-setup cost linearly with the number of setups.

\paragraph{End-to-end.}
Combining the two components, our per-material pipeline finishes in
roughly $10$--$11$\,min of wall-clock (manual $\sim\!10$\,min
$+$ automated $\le 1$\,min), versus hours of manual calibration
followed by additional hours of MPM-in-loop inversion under the
predecessor---a difference of more than an order of magnitude in
both the human-attention budget and the compute budget. More
importantly, the automated portion completes within the time window
of a single user session, which is what makes the freshness,
fieldable-capture, and multi-setup capabilities of
\S\ref{sec:freshness}--\ref{sec:rheometer} practically accessible
rather than nominal.


\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{What the Acceleration Unlocks}

The immediate effect of our pipeline is a three-orders-of-magnitude
reduction in per-video inverse cost. The more important effect is
that three capabilities previously out of reach under
simulation-in-the-loop become routine:
\emph{freshness} for time-varying
fluids (\S\ref{sec:freshness}),
practical characterisation of \emph{rheometer-infeasible}
materials (\S\ref{sec:chunky}), and
\emph{multi-observation joint inversion} that approaches
parallel-plate rheometer precision (\S\ref{sec:multi}). The first is
a temporal improvement that turns ``hours to return a past state''
into ``seconds to return the current state.'' The second inherits
the independent value of video rheometry over instrumental
rheometry~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} and brings it to
fieldable speed. The third makes usable a $\sigma_Y$-mitigation path
that the predecessor work identified but left computationally out of
reach.

\subsection{A Reusable Surrogate as a Community Asset}

The separation of a one-time offline training phase from a
seconds-scale online inference phase produces a reusable artefact
that is structurally unavailable to simulation-in-the-loop pipelines.
Our frozen $540$-expert checkpoint was trained once on
$295{,}419$ MPM simulations covering $5.5$--$5.6$ orders of magnitude
in $\eta$ and $\sigma_Y$ and the full $[2, 7]\,\mathrm{cm}$ range of
household containers. Subsequent users---and subsequent research on
video rheometry---can query the same checkpoint without paying the
MPM training cost again. This amortisation property is what turns
our method from a one-shot inverse into a reusable measurement
instrument; it is not available to sim-in-loop pipelines because
they have no surrogate to amortise. We release the checkpoint, the
training corpus, and the inverse code alongside this paper.

\subsection{Why Regime-Aware Surrogate and Data-Honest Prior Must Go
Together}

A takeaway at the core of this work is that neither the surrogate
nor the inverse modifications is sufficient alone. A single global
GP, even if it were accurate enough, would still leave the
$\sigma_Y$-identifiability ridge intact under a vanilla CMA-ES: the
optimiser would slide to $\sigma_Y \to 0$ while inflating $\eta$ to
match the observation. Conversely, the identifiability-aware inverse
of \S\ref{sec:inverse} requires an accurate surrogate to be evaluated
$300$ times per video within seconds; running the same inverse
against a coarse surrogate would reintroduce surrogate bias as a new
source of error. The hierarchical MoGP-rBCM surrogate and the
training-data-anchored prior must be coupled; this coupling is the
technical core.

\subsection{Relation to Differentiable MPM}

Differentiable MPM pipelines~\cite{li2023pacnerf,eastman2024recovery}
obtain gradients through the forward simulator and perform
gradient-based per-scene parameter search. We differ in two
respects. First, gradients through the non-smooth HB return map are
known to be
ill-conditioned~\cite{huang2021plasticinelab}; our gradient-free
inverse sidesteps this issue. Second, differentiable-MPM pipelines
optimise per scene and do not amortise a reusable surrogate, so
their per-material cost remains in the simulation-in-the-loop
regime. Our architectural choice of an offline-trained hierarchical
surrogate is precisely what enables the seconds-scale per-video
latency and the community-asset property of \S6.2.


\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Information-theoretic $\sigma_Y$ limit.}
The identifiability-aware inverse of \S\ref{sec:inverse} resolves
the yield-stress/consistency degeneracy whenever the dam-break
observation carries \emph{some} signal about $\sigma_Y$, but it
cannot recover information that is not in the observation. For
materials in which $\eta\,\dot\gamma^{\,n} \gg \sigma_Y$ across the
entire dam-break shear-rate range, the flow front encodes
essentially no information about $\sigma_Y$, and any inverse---ours
included---returns an estimate determined by the prior and the
training distribution rather than by the data. Among the $10$
held-out samples of Table~\ref{tab:main}, one such material
($\sigma_Y^{\mathrm{true}} \approx 0.39$\,Pa in a low-$\eta$ regime)
pushes the $\sigma_Y$ \emph{mean} to $7.0$ while the nine remaining
samples sit at median $0.298$. This is a task-level bound, not a
method bound; the appropriate mitigation is the multi-setup protocol
of \S\ref{sec:multi}.

\paragraph{Dependence on training-set coverage.}
The surrogate is trustworthy only inside the input box on which it
was trained. Queries outside this box trigger the per-expert bound
tightening (\S\ref{sec:inverse}), which relies on the routed
expert's training inputs as the admissible region and therefore
does not extrapolate. Out-of-distribution materials require an
extension of the training corpus. rBCM predictive variance flags
out-of-distribution queries but does not by itself correct them.

\paragraph{Fidelity of the forward model.}
A surrogate can only be as accurate as the simulator it learns from.
Our MPM forward (\S3.2) models HB rheology, gravity, and no-slip
boundaries, but not surface tension, elastic overshoot, thixotropy,
or viscoelastic memory. Materials exhibiting these phenomena deviate
systematically from the HB forward, and this deviation cannot be
recovered by any HB inverse. Richer constitutive
families---viscoelastic extensions and damage-MPM
variants~\cite{wolper2019cdmpm}---are natural but out-of-scope
directions.

\paragraph{Capture and geometry requirements.}
The level-$1$ gate is trained on rectangular cube-like containers
with $W, H \in [2, 7]$\,cm. Curved or irregular containers require
retraining the geometry gate on a broader container descriptor.
Camera calibration uses a printed ChArUco marker board; markerless
calibration from natural scene content is possible in principle but
not demonstrated here.

\paragraph{Real-time is a direction, not a destination.}
Our median wall-clock is $12$\,s per video on a single consumer
GPU---seconds-scale but not millisecond-scale. The ``real-time'' of
our title should be read as \emph{fast enough to fit within a single
user interaction} rather than strict continuous-time inversion.
Reducing latency further through surrogate distillation, query-time
adaptive $K_{\mathrm{geo}}$ selection, or amortised cross-material
inverse is an open direction.


\section{Conclusion}

We presented a surrogate-accelerated framework for non-Newtonian
video rheometry that compresses the Herschel--Bulkley inverse from
over ten hours per video in simulation-in-the-loop prior
work~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} to a median of $12$\,s per
video on a single consumer GPU. The method rests on two coupled
components: a regime-aware hierarchical Mixture-of-GP surrogate
(HVIMoGP-rBCM) with $540$ specialised experts routed by a two-level
Bayesian Gaussian mixture gate over container geometry and
observation signatures, and an identifiability-aware inverse that
warm-starts CMA-ES from the routed expert's nearest training
neighbour and anchors the $\sigma_Y$ axis with a data-honest soft
prior---a complementary mitigation for the
yield-stress/consistency similarity degeneracy that~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}
treated through Hessian-based similarity analysis, a
Poiseuille-flow proxy, active setup selection, and iterative
multi-setup CMA-ES. Our path trades the Hessian machinery for
training-data priors and joint (rather than sequential) multi-setup
inversion, both made practical by the surrogate.

Beyond runtime reduction, the seconds-scale inverse unlocks three
capabilities inaccessible to simulation-in-the-loop rheometry: fresh
parameter estimates for time-varying fluids, practical
characterisation of materials conventional rheometers cannot
measure, and a multi-observation joint protocol that approaches
parallel-plate rheometer precision from phone videos alone. We
release the trained surrogate, the $295{,}419$-row MPM training
corpus, and the inverse code as a reusable community artefact on
which subsequent video-rheometry research can build without paying
the simulation training cost again.

The broader message is that video rheometry crosses the threshold
from laboratory demonstration to fieldable tool not by replacing
physics-based estimation but by amortising its cost. The surrogate
is an accelerator of a physics-grounded inverse, not a substitute
for physics; what this acceleration enables, at seconds per video,
is what brings the technique within reach of time-sensitive,
iterative, and situated measurement.
