\section{Introduction}

Many everyday fluids---yogurts, pastes, sauces, slurries, cosmetic
emulsions---exhibit non-Newtonian behavior that governs how they pour,
spread, cling, and deform. Characterizing this behavior quantitatively
requires measuring shear-rate-dependent stress, traditionally using
parallel-plate or cone-and-plate rheometers. However, these instruments
fail on a practically important class of materials: those containing
inclusions larger than the instrument's measurement gap---chunky sauces,
particulate suspensions, foods with solids---and those whose rheology
evolves faster than a multi-minute lab procedure can capture.

Non-Newtonian video rheometry~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} addresses
this gap by estimating Herschel--Bulkley parameters
$(\sigma_Y, \eta, n)$ from observations of a simple dam-break flow,
without contacting the material with any instrument. The underlying
principle is inverse simulation: a material point method (MPM) forward
model is repeatedly executed inside an optimizer until the simulated
flow reproduces the observed silhouette. Since the forward model is
physics-based, the approach naturally handles the very materials that
instrumental rheometers cannot, and recovers a full constitutive triple
from a single short video.

However, this otherwise uniquely capable approach is bottlenecked by the
cost of its inner loop. A single MPM dam-break simulation takes several
minutes on modern GPU hardware, and derivative-free optimization
typically issues hundreds of candidate evaluations before convergence.
The end-to-end inverse therefore takes \emph{around eight hours per
setup}, confining the method to offline research use and precluding
three capabilities that would otherwise be within reach of a
video-based approach:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{Tracking parameters of time-varying fluids.} When a
    material's properties evolve during the measurement
    window---sedimentation, aging, temperature drift, mixing history,
    cooking cooldown---an hour-scale inversion returns a parameter
    describing a past state. The inferred value is stale by the time
    it is delivered.
    \item \emph{Characterizing many materials in a single session.}
    Comparing several brands or batches, or sweeping a single material
    over temperature, dilution, or shear-history conditions, requires
    running the inverse $N$ times. At hours per setup, even modest $N$
    is impractical, so per-material rheometry is restricted to
    handpicked showcase samples rather than scaled to surveys or
    catalogs.
    \item \emph{Iterative measurement workflows.} Closed-loop uses,
    in which each capture's recovered parameters guide the next
    adjustment to a recipe or process, are foreclosed when the
    measurement-to-decision latency is hours.
\end{itemize}

We propose a surrogate-accelerated framework that compresses the
inverse from hours to seconds per setup, making all three
capabilities available within a practical workflow. Our technical
core couples three components.

\emph{(i)}~A \emph{hierarchical mixture of Gaussian-process experts}
replaces MPM inside the optimizer. The production surrogate bank
partitions the forward map by container geometry, terminal flow
distance, and normalized trajectory shape. At runtime the observable
tuple $(W,H,\mathbf{y})$ deterministically routes each query to a
local exact GP expert, with a final marginal-likelihood rerank on the
terminal flow displacement keeping the selected expert aligned with
the observed runout regime.

\emph{(ii)}~A \emph{GP-aware inverse likelihood} addresses the
yield-stress/consistency ridge that arises when the observed
shear-rate range is dominated by the consistency term. CMA-ES
minimizes a log-space Type-II marginal-likelihood objective that uses
the routed GP's predictive variance, an observation-noise floor, and
the corresponding Occam term. The remaining ridge is reported through
multi-start dispersion and local Laplace confidence intervals rather
than hidden by a hand-tuned yield-stress prior.

\emph{(iii)}~An \emph{algorithmic camera-calibration pipeline}
replaces the manual fSpy-and-Photoshop workflow that precedes the
inverse in prior work. A ChArUco marker board provides an initial
pose estimate, and a subsequent silhouette-edge refinement aligns
the camera against the dam-break geometry without per-capture human
annotation, removing the largest fixed cost of the capture
workflow.

Our work inherits the problem setup, dam-break observation protocol,
and inverse formulation from~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}, and
contributes the machinery needed to make that formulation
computationally practical. \cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} addresses
the yield-stress/consistency degeneracy with a multi-stage protocol
combining loss-Hessian similarity analysis, a plane-Poiseuille-flow
proxy for the Hessian, Hessian-guided active selection of successive
setups, and iterative multi-setup CMA-ES; the protocol is sound and
was validated on $30$ material-trial cases, but it inherits the
per-iterate MPM cost. By porting the inverse pipeline onto a learned
surrogate, the same setup-selection and two-setup joint machinery becomes
affordable per material rather than only per showcase, and the inverse
returns within the latency required by the three uses above. The
main contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item A \emph{hierarchical mixture of Gaussian-process experts}
    that replaces MPM inside the inverse loop with a frozen bank of
    routed exact GP experts, structured by geometry, trajectory
    length, and normalized shape to handle the non-stationarity of the
    Herschel--Bulkley forward map.
    \item A \emph{GP-aware inverse} that uses predictive uncertainty
    directly in the likelihood, warm-starts and bounds CMA-ES within
    the routed subproblem, and reports the residual
    yield-stress/consistency ridge through restart and Hessian-based
    uncertainty diagnostics.
    \item An \emph{algorithmic two-stage camera-calibration pipeline}
    based on ChArUco markers and silhouette-edge refinement,
    replacing the manual workflow of prior work and removing the
    largest fixed cost from each capture.
    \item An evaluation establishing that seconds-scale video
    rheometry enables three uses inaccessible at hours-per-setup
    cost: \emph{(i)}~tracking how a material's parameters evolve in
    real time; \emph{(ii)}~characterizing many materials, batches,
    or conditions within a single measurement session; and
    \emph{(iii)}~iterative measurement workflows where each capture's
    parameters guide the next adjustment.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Physical Measurements and Flow Rheology Signals}

Quantitative rheology is traditionally measured with rheometers
(parallel-plate, cone-and-plate, or rotational) that apply a
controlled laminar flow under known shear rate to recover the
constitutive relation between shear stress and shear rate. These
instruments require a narrow gap to enforce a clean shear profile,
which excludes materials with millimeter-scale inclusions, as well
as materials whose rheology evolves faster than the lab procedure
can capture. Less-controlled but more accessible alternatives, such
as the Bostwick consistometer, give a single readable measurement
from a simple dam-break test.

Gravity-driven flow has a long pedigree as a rheometric observation
that complements idealized-flow rheometers. The slump test
of~\citet{pashias1996fifty} recovers yield stress from a single
geometric reading; \citet{roussel2005fiftycent} extend this argument
to the spreading regime that matches dam-break geometry directly;
the final-deposit shape of a stopped free-surface flow is itself a
rheological signature~\cite{coussot1996rheological};
and~\citet{ancey2007plasticity} frames the broader class of
gravity-driven yield-stress flows as a standard rheometric regime.
The relation between flow-front position and Herschel--Bulkley
parameters is made quantitative by the similarity analyses
of~\citet{liumei1989slow} and~\citet{balmforth2007two}, the same
analytic framework leveraged by~\citet{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}.

Our forward simulator inherits the material point method (MPM)
lineage that brought continuum simulation into
graphics~\cite{stomakhin2013material,jiang2015affine,hu2018moving,
jiang2016material}, with shear-thinning and yield-stress rheology
realized through return-mapping
formulations~\cite{yue2015continuum,ram2015material,klar2016drucker,
tampubolon2017multi,wolper2019cdmpm}. We adopt the Herschel--Bulkley
return map of~\citet{yue2015continuum} on a Taichi-based
implementation~\cite{hu2019taichi}; the sauce-specific
shear-thinning simulator of~\citet{nagasawa2019mixing}, from the
same lab as our direct predecessor, shares our material focus. A
broader introduction to fluid simulation for graphics appears
in~\cite{bridson2015fluid}.


\subsection{Video-Based Material Estimation}

Estimating simulation parameters from video by matching rendered and
observed dynamics has a long lineage in graphics and
vision~\cite{bhat2002computing,monszpart2016smash}. Takahashi and
Lin~\cite{takahashi2019video} established the video-to-parameter
pattern for viscous fluids by matching rendered and observed
dam-break silhouettes on a per-scene basis;
Hamamichi~et~al.~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} extended this to
the non-Newtonian Herschel--Bulkley triple $(\sigma_Y,\eta,n)$ and is
the direct predecessor of our work. Their treatment of the
yield-stress/consistency degeneracy combines a loss-Hessian
similarity analysis, a plane-Poiseuille-flow proxy for the Hessian,
Hessian-guided active selection of successive setups, and iterative
multi-setup CMA-ES; the protocol is sound and was validated on $30$
material-trial cases, but it inherits the per-iterate MPM cost. We
inherit their dam-break observation protocol, their
Herschel--Bulkley inverse objective, and their
simulation-in-the-loop formulation, and contribute the surrogate
machinery that makes the same multi-setup machinery affordable per
material rather than only per showcase.

Related work recovers material parameters from other visual
modalities: video-based cloth friction~\cite{rasheed2020visual},
particle-image-velocimetry-based rheology through physics-informed
networks~\cite{thakur2025learning}, and consumer-video neural
regression for scalar viscosity~\cite{rauzan2024protorheology}. These
target different material classes or observation modalities and do
not recover the full Herschel--Bulkley triple. Free-surface
extraction from experimental video is treated
in~\cite{cochard2009tracking}. Inverse rheometry has also been
approached in the geophysical-flow setting using shallow-water
forward models and adjoint methods~\cite{martin2010inverse}; our
formulation differs in using a full three-dimensional MPM forward
model and a derivative-free inverse over a learned surrogate.

An alternative paradigm pursues parameter recovery via differentiable
simulation. The differentiable MLS-MPM
family~\cite{hu2019chainqueen,hu2020difftaichi,huang2021plasticinelab}
has been applied to deformable and plastic materials;
gradSim~\cite{murthy2021gradsim} demonstrates end-to-end
differentiable physics with differentiable rendering. Most relevant
to non-Newtonian recovery from video,
PAC-NeRF~\cite{li2023pacnerf} couples differentiable MPM with NeRF
rendering to recover viscosity parameters from multi-view video, and
Eastman~\cite{eastman2024recovery} recovers Herschel--Bulkley
parameters from a ketchup column-collapse video via differentiable
MPM and neural rendering. These approaches optimize per scene and do
not amortize a reusable surrogate across materials. Evidence from
PlasticineLab~\cite{huang2021plasticinelab} indicates that gradients
through non-smooth plastic return maps are ill-conditioned,
motivating our choice of a gradient-free inverse over a
probabilistic surrogate.


\subsection{Identifiability of Free-Surface Observations}

A prerequisite for replacing simulation-in-the-loop inversion with a
surrogate is that the chosen observation
$\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$ carries enough information to identify
$(\sigma_Y,\eta,n)$. Recent inverse-analysis work establishes that
free-surface evolution alone is sufficient:
\citet{martinpritchard2022inferring} show that the full generalized
Newtonian constitutive function is in principle uniquely recoverable
from free-surface evolution; \citet{ahmadi2022identification}
compute linearly-independent sensitivity coefficients of the three
Herschel--Bulkley parameters with respect to free-surface readings;
and~\citet{muchiri2024identification} demonstrate inverse recovery
of yield stress and consistency from shallow-water elevation
hydrographs under viscoplastic rheology. Our scalar flow-front trace
$\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$ is a low-dimensional summary of this
evolution, and the residual yield-stress/consistency degeneracy we
address in~\S\ref{sec:inverse} is therefore not a fundamental
information-theoretic limit of the phone-video observation but a
practical ill-conditioning that arises in the low-$\sigma_Y$ regime
where the yield term is dominated by the viscous contribution.


\subsection{Surrogate-Accelerated Inverse}

Gaussian-process regression~\cite{rasmussen2006gaussian} provides a
natural surrogate for expensive simulators because it couples
flexible nonparametric regression with calibrated predictive
uncertainty. For data sizes beyond exact-GP feasibility, sparse and
variational approximations~\cite{snelson2006sparse,
quinonero2005unifying,titsias2009variational,hensman2013gaussian,
matthews2016sparse} replace the full covariance with an
inducing-variable approximation. A single global GP is insufficient
for functions with heterogeneous local structure;
mixture-of-experts (MoE)
models~\cite{jordan1994hierarchical,yuksel2012twenty} decompose
prediction into multiple specialists with a gating mechanism, and
mixtures of GPs instantiate this idea with GP
experts~\cite{tresp2001mixtures,rasmussen2002infinite,
meeds2006alternative,nguyen2008local,yuan2008variational,
trapp2020deep}. For aggregating independent GP predictions, the
Bayesian Committee Machine~\cite{tresp2000bayesian} and its
variants~\cite{cao2014generalized,deisenroth2015distributed,
liu2018generalized} provide closed-form variance-weighted fusion;
sparse-expert plus committee aggregation has recently been applied
to physics emulation~\cite{cohen2024sparse}.

Inverting expensive simulators has been addressed under the headings
of Bayesian calibration of computer models~\cite{kennedy2001bayesian}
and simulation-based inference~\cite{cranmer2020frontier}, with
efficient global optimization~\cite{jones1998efficient} and Bayesian
optimization~\cite{snoek2012practical,shahriari2016taking} as the
dominant search strategies when the objective is expensive and
derivative-free. The pattern of running derivative-free optimization
on a GP surrogate~\cite{bajaj2019gaussian,hansen2016cma} has been
applied to inverse viscoplasticity~\cite{vandamme2021bayesian} and
viscoelasticity~\cite{hashemi2025bayesian}. The substitution of an
expensive simulator by a trained surrogate inside a derivative-free
inverse is not heuristic: \citet{stuart2018posterior} prove that the
Hellinger distance between a GP-surrogate-based posterior and the
true simulator-based posterior is bounded by moments of the
surrogate's predictive error, and~\citet{teckentrup2020convergence}
extends the guarantee to the realistic setting in which kernel
hyperparameters are estimated from data. The replacement of
MPM-in-the-loop by a learned GP surrogate therefore inherits the
same posterior-fidelity guarantees that justify Bayesian
calibration~\cite{kennedy2001bayesian} and modern simulation-based
inference~\cite{cranmer2020frontier}.


\subsection{Camera Calibration}

Estimating camera pose and intrinsics from images is a foundational
computer-vision task; the textbook treatment of multi-view geometry
appears in~\cite{hartley2004multiple}. Marker-based calibration
provides closed-form initial poses through detected fiducials:
AprilTag~\cite{olson2011apriltag},
ARTag~\cite{fiala2005artag}, and the
ArUco / ChArUco family~\cite{garrido2014automatic,
garrido2016generation} all yield 3D-to-2D correspondences solvable
via PnP variants such as EPnP~\cite{lepetit2009epnp}. Sub-pixel
corner refinement using the gradient-based detector
of~\citet{forstner1987fast} sharpens correspondences before pose
recovery. Beyond marker-driven initialization, silhouette- and
edge-based refinement aligns the camera against object boundaries
through chamfer-distance optimization on a Euclidean distance
transform~\cite{borgefors1986distance,maurer2003linear}, with edge
maps from the Canny detector~\cite{canny1986computational}. Our
calibration pipeline (\S\ref{sec:calib}) combines a ChArUco initial
pose with a two-phase silhouette-edge refinement, replacing the
manual fSpy-and-Photoshop workflow used
by~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} and removing the largest fixed
cost from each capture.


\section{Background}

\subsection{Herschel--Bulkley Rheology}

Many non-Newtonian fluids are well approximated by the
Herschel--Bulkley (HB) model~\cite{herschel1926konsistenzmessungen},
which expresses the shear-stress response as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hb-scalar}
    \tau \;=\; \sigma_Y \;+\; \eta\,\dot\gamma^{\,n},
\end{equation}
where $\tau$ is the shear stress, $\dot\gamma$ the shear rate,
$\sigma_Y$ the yield stress, $\eta$ the consistency parameter, and
$n \in (0, 1]$ controls shear thinning ($n < 1$) versus Newtonian
behaviour ($n = 1$). In three dimensions the deviatoric stress tensor
is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hb-tensor}
    \boldsymbol{\tau}^{\mathrm{dev}}
    \;=\;
    \left[\;\frac{\sigma_Y}{\|\mathbf{D}\|}
    \;+\; \eta\,\|\mathbf{D}\|^{\,n-1}\;\right] \mathbf{D},
    \qquad
    \|\mathbf{D}\|
    = \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{D}\!:\!\mathbf{D}},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{D}$ is the rate-of-strain tensor. The admissible
stress region is delimited by the von~Mises yield function
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hb-yield}
    \Phi_{Y}(\boldsymbol{\tau})
    \;=\; \|\boldsymbol{\tau}^{\mathrm{dev}}\| \;-\; \sigma_Y \;\le\; 0,
\end{equation}
with the plastic flow rule of Eq.~\eqref{eq:hb-tensor} activated only
when $\Phi_{Y} > 0$. We adopt the elasto-plastic MPM realisation of
Yue et al.~\cite{yue2015continuum}, which recovers the same
$(\sigma_Y, \eta, n)$ triple at steady state via a Newton--Raphson
return map that enforces $\Phi_{Y} \le 0$ on each plastic step. The HB
response is therefore characterised by the three-parameter vector
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\theta} \;=\; (\sigma_Y,\,\eta,\,n) \in \Theta \subset
    \mathbb{R}^{3}.
\end{equation}
This parameter triple is the same one used by prior non-Newtonian
video rheometry~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}; we treat the
constitutive model as fixed and focus on recovering
$\boldsymbol{\theta}$ at video-rate latency.


\subsection{Dam-Break MPM Forward Model}

Following the experimental protocol
of~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}, we obtain $\boldsymbol{\theta}$
by observing a dam-break flow released from a rectangular fluid column
of width $W$ and height $H$ on a flat, no-slip floor. The forward
simulator integrates the HB return map of \S3.1 on a
moving-least-squares MPM~\cite{hu2018moving} grid with spacing
$\Delta x \approx 10^{-1}$\,cm and timestep $\Delta t = 5\times
10^{-4}$\,s, implemented on Taichi~\cite{hu2019taichi}. The
observation is a vector of flow-front displacements sampled at eight
uniformly spaced frames $t_1,\dots,t_8$ after release:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:y-def}
    \mathbf{y}
    \;=\;
    \bigl(x_1,\,x_2,\,\dots,\,x_8\bigr)^{\!\top}
    \;\in\;
    \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}^{8},
\end{equation}
where $x_i$ is the maximum lateral extent of the fluid at time $t_i$
in cm. By construction $x_1 \le x_2 \le \dots \le x_8$, since the
flow front cannot retract. We write the forward simulator as
$\mathbf{y} = \mathcal{S}(\boldsymbol{\theta};\,W, H)$.

\paragraph{Implementation details.}
Three details of the forward simulator deserve mention.
\emph{(i)~Container walls} use sticky boundaries: momentum on
interior grid cells is zeroed each step, and any particle that
penetrates a wall is flagged as interior and excluded from the
downstream silhouette so wall-embedded fluid cannot produce
spurious bright pixels in $\mathcal{S}(\boldsymbol{\theta}; W, H)$.
\emph{(ii)~The fluid surface} is extracted by Laplacian-then-biharmonic
smoothing of a rasterised signed-distance field, with per-cell
$\varphi$ clamped each smoothing step to the range
$[\varphi_{\min},\,\varphi_{\max}]$ admitted by the local
particle-radius bounds; this automatic clamp removes the per-frame
$\varphi$ tuning used in earlier pipelines.
\emph{(iii)~The rendered silhouette} is produced by marching-cubes
meshing of the zero-level set followed by projection through the
calibrated camera $(\mathbf{K}_{0}, R^{*}, \mathbf{t}^{*})$ of
\S\ref{sec:calib}, closing the loop from MPM particles to the
$8$-dimensional flow-distance vector $\mathbf{y}$.


\subsection{Observation Pipeline}
\label{sec:obs-pipeline}

Computing the observation vector $\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$ from a
smartphone video requires two preprocessing steps: (a)~recovering the
camera pose against the experimental scene, and (b)~extracting a
silhouette-based flow-front distance at the eight uniformly spaced
frames defining $\mathbf{y}$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:y-def}. The
silhouette-extraction step follows the protocol
of~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} (background removal, binary
segmentation, frontal-extent measurement). The camera-calibration
step is our contribution and is detailed as part of the method
in~\S\ref{sec:calib}.


\subsection{Inverse Video Rheometry as Simulation-in-the-Loop Optimization}

Given an observation $\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$ captured from a real
experiment at geometry $(W, H)$, video rheometry recovers
$\boldsymbol{\theta}$ by matching simulator output against the
observation,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:inverse-generic}
    \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}
    \;=\;
    \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}\in\Theta}\;
    \mathcal{L}\bigl(\boldsymbol{\theta};\,
    \mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}},\,W,\,H\bigr),
\end{equation}
where
$\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \|\mathcal{S}(\boldsymbol{\theta};\,W,H)
- \mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}\|^{2}$ is an observation-matching loss.
Because $\mathcal{S}$ is defined by an MPM integration,
$\mathcal{L}$ is expensive---several minutes per query on modern
GPU hardware---and non-smooth through the plastic return map. The
simulation-in-the-loop formulation of~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}
evaluates $\mathcal{S}$ directly inside a derivative-free optimiser,
paying the cost of a full MPM simulation at every candidate
$\boldsymbol{\theta}$.

The loss landscape carries a well-known identifiability pathology.
Define the \emph{characteristic shear rate} that separates the
yield-dominated from the viscous-dominated regime,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gammastar}
    \dot\gamma^{\star}(\sigma_Y, \eta, n)
    \;=\; \bigl(\sigma_Y / \eta\bigr)^{1/n}.
\end{equation}
Rewriting Eq.~\eqref{eq:hb-scalar} as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hb-factored}
    \tau(\dot\gamma)
    \;=\; \eta\,\dot\gamma^{\,n}\left[\,1
    \;+\; \left(\frac{\dot\gamma^{\star}}{\dot\gamma}\right)^{\!n}\,\right],
\end{equation}
shows that as $\dot\gamma / \dot\gamma^{\star} \to \infty$ the
second bracket tends to $1$ and the stress becomes asymptotically
governed by $(\eta, n)$ alone; formally
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ridge}
    \left.
      \frac{\partial \tau}{\partial \sigma_Y}
    \right/\!
    \left.
      \frac{\partial \tau}{\partial \eta}
    \right|_{\dot\gamma \gg \dot\gamma^{\star}}
    \;=\;
    \frac{1}{\dot\gamma^{\,n}}
    \;\longrightarrow\; 0.
\end{equation}
If the observed dam-break regime lies largely above
$\dot\gamma^{\star}$, $\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$ becomes effectively
insensitive to $\sigma_Y$ while still constraining $(\eta, n)$, so a
continuum of near-equal-loss solutions trade $\sigma_Y$ for a
compensating increase in $\eta$. \citet{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}
identify this degeneracy through dimensional-similarity analysis
and propose multi-setup joint inversion as a mitigation, paying
the simulation-in-the-loop cost $N$ times for an $N$-setup
estimate.

In addition, when the target material itself evolves during the
measurement window---sedimentation, aging, temperature drift,
mixing, cooking cooldown---the true parameter state
$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t}$ drifts during the inverse, and an
hour-scale estimate no longer describes the current condition. We
therefore want estimation latency short enough to \emph{track} the
evolving state, not just to reduce amortised compute cost.

\section{Method (Surrogate-Accelerated Inverse)}
\label{sec:method-block}

\subsection{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:problem}

We keep the physical forward model of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:inverse-generic}, but replace each in-loop MPM call by
a frozen mixture-of-experts surrogate. The online estimator receives
a calibrated dam-break observation
$\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}\in\mathbb{R}^{8}$ and setup geometry
$(W,H)$, routes the observation to a small set of local GP experts,
and solves for
$\boldsymbol{\theta}=(n,\eta,\sigma_Y)$ with a GP-aware
Type-II maximum-likelihood loss. The same machinery handles a second
observation of the same material by sharing one
$\boldsymbol{\theta}$ across both setups.

Figure~\ref{fig:overview} summarises the implemented pipeline. Offline,
MPM simulations are partitioned by geometry and by the observed
flow-front trajectory, and one exact GP expert is trained for each
resulting sub-region. Online, the observable tuple
$(W,H,\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}})$ selects a primary sub-expert by a
deterministic three-stage gate, refined by a marginal-likelihood
rerank on the terminal flow displacement; CMA-ES then optimises over
the routed expert's padded training box. A first setup can also
propose the next physical experiment through the analytical
Hessian-orthogonal rule of \citet{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}, whose
closed-form Fisher-information construction we adopt unchanged.

\paragraph{Formal statement.}\label{sec:problem-formal}
The original simulation-in-the-loop inverse is
\begin{taggedeqn}{P}{P}
    \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}
    =
    \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}\in\Theta}
    \left\|\mathcal{S}(\boldsymbol{\theta};W,H)
    -\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}\right\|^2 .
\end{taggedeqn}
Our single-setup production estimator replaces
$\mathcal{S}$ by a routed GP surrogate
$\tilde{\mathcal{S}}_{k^\star}$ and minimises the negative log
marginal likelihood
\begin{taggedeqn}{P$'$}{Pprime}
    \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}
    =
    \arg\min_{\mathbf{z}\in\Theta_{k^\star}}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{GP}}\!\left(
      \tilde{\mathcal{S}}_{k^\star}(\mathbf{z};W,H),
      \mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}\right)
    + p_{\mathrm{sup}}\,
      \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{box}}(\mathbf{z};k^\star),
\end{taggedeqn}
where $\mathbf{z}=(n,\log\eta,\log\sigma_Y)$, $k^\star$ is selected
from $(W,H,\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}})$, and
$\Theta_{k^\star}$ is the routed sub's padded training box. For two
setups $a,b$ of the same material, conditional independence gives the
joint objective
\begin{taggedeqn}{P$^{\star}$}{Pstar}
    \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}
    =
    \arg\min_{\mathbf{z}\in\Theta_{a,b}}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{GP}}^{(a)}(\mathbf{z})
    + \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{GP}}^{(b)}(\mathbf{z})
    + p_{\mathrm{sup}}\,
      \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(
      \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{box}}^{(a)}(\mathbf{z})+
      \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{box}}^{(b)}(\mathbf{z})\right).
\end{taggedeqn}
The current production path does not use an explicit
$\sigma_Y$ anchor prior; uncertainty along the HB ridge is instead
reported through multi-start dispersion and a local Laplace Hessian.

\subsection{Input and Output Representation}
\label{sec:repr}

Each surrogate query uses the five-dimensional physical input
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x}=(n,\eta,\sigma_Y,W,H)^\top ,
\end{equation}
with logarithms applied to $\eta$ and $\sigma_Y$ before standardisation,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:xscale}
    \tilde{\mathbf{x}}
    =
    \frac{\mathbf{x}_{\log}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_X}
         {\boldsymbol{\sigma}_X},
    \qquad
    \mathbf{x}_{\log}
    =
    (n,\log\eta,\log\sigma_Y,W,H)^\top .
\end{equation}
The output is the eight-frame flow-front vector
$\mathbf{y}\in\mathbb{R}^{8}$; the output scaler subtracts a frozen
training mean,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:yscale}
    \tilde{\mathbf{y}}=\mathbf{y}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_Y .
\end{equation}
At inverse time, the observed vector
$\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$ is used both as likelihood data and as a
routing signal.

\subsection{Hierarchical Mixture-of-GP Surrogate}
\label{sec:surrogate}

\paragraph{Three-Layer Deterministic Partition.}
\label{sec:surr-3layer}
The production model bank consists of $25$ geometry-conditioned
sub-banks. A query is routed by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gate}
    (W,H,\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}})
    \xrightarrow{L_1} g^\star
    \xrightarrow{L_2} b^\star
    \xrightarrow{L_3} s^\star .
\end{equation}
Layer 1 assigns the setup geometry to one of $25$ geometry indices,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:layer1}
    g^\star = G(W,H), \qquad g^\star\in\{0,\ldots,24\}.
\end{equation}
Within the selected geometry sub-bank, Layer 2 places the terminal
flow displacement $y_8$ into the
stored length bin,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:layer2}
    b^\star
    =
    \mathrm{bin}\!\left(y_{\mathrm{obs},8};
    \{e_j^{(g^\star)}\}\right),
\end{equation}
clamping to the nearest bin outside the empirical range. Layer 3 uses
the normalised flow-shape feature
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:phi}
    \boldsymbol{\phi}(\mathbf{y})
    =
    \frac{(y_2-y_1,\ldots,y_8-y_1)^\top}{\max(y_8,\epsilon)}
\end{equation}
standardised by the bin-specific scaler, projected by the bin-specific
PCA, and assigned to the nearest K-means centroid,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:layer3}
    s^\star
    =
    \arg\min_s
    \left\|
      \mathrm{PCA}_{g^\star,b^\star}(\boldsymbol{\phi})
      -\mathbf{c}_{g^\star,b^\star,s}
    \right\|_2 .
\end{equation}
The final stage is a marginal-likelihood rerank on the terminal flow
displacement $y_{\mathrm{obs},8}$: among the PCA-nearest candidate
sub-experts $\{s\}$, choose the one for which $y_{\mathrm{obs},8}$ is
most central in that sub's training distribution. Approximating the
training $y_8$ values inside sub $s$ as Gaussian, the negative log
marginal likelihood of $y_{\mathrm{obs},8}$ under sub $s$ collapses to
the standardised distance
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:y8q}
    s_{\mathrm{prod}}
    =
    \arg\min_s
    \frac{|y_{\mathrm{obs},8}-\mathrm{median}(Y_{s,8})|}
         {\max(\mathrm{std}(Y_{s,8}),\,0.1)} ,
\end{equation}
which is the standard one-dimensional case of Bayesian model
selection by marginal evidence~\cite{murphy2012machine,mackay2003information}.
Production uses the top-1 expert directly,
without ensemble aggregation across candidates.

\paragraph{Why Deterministic Routing.}
\label{sec:surr-deterministic}
At inverse time the material parameters are unknown, but
$(W,H,\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}})$ are observed. Routing on observable
quantities avoids a circular gate that would require the unknown
$\boldsymbol{\theta}$, and makes out-of-support cases diagnosable by
checking the selected sub's training box.

\paragraph{Per-Cell Gaussian-Process Experts.}
\label{sec:surr-experts}
Each sub-expert is a tuple of eight independent exact GP regressors
sharing the same scaled $5$-D input and using a Mat\'ern-$5/2$ ARD
kernel,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:matern}
    k_{5/2}(\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b})
    =
    \sigma_f^2
    \left(1+\sqrt{5}r+\tfrac{5}{3}r^2\right)
    e^{-\sqrt{5}r},
    \quad
    r^2=\sum_{d=1}^{5}\frac{(a_d-b_d)^2}{\ell_d^2}.
\end{equation}
For output frame $d$, the posterior mean and variance are
\begin{align}
\label{eq:gp-predictive}
    \mu_{s,d}(\tilde{\mathbf{x}})
    &=
    \mathbf{k}_*^\top
    (K_s+\sigma_{n,s,d}^2 I)^{-1}\tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{s,d},
\nonumber\\
    \sigma_{s,d}^2(\tilde{\mathbf{x}})
    &=
    k_{5/2}(\tilde{\mathbf{x}},\tilde{\mathbf{x}})
    -
    \mathbf{k}_*^\top
    (K_s+\sigma_{n,s,d}^2 I)^{-1}\mathbf{k}_* .
\end{align}
Hyperparameters are fitted by maximising the exact GP marginal
likelihood,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gp-mll}
\begin{aligned}
    \log p(\tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{s,d}\mid X_s)
    =
    &-\tfrac{1}{2}
      \tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{s,d}^{\top}
      (K_s+\sigma_{n,s,d}^2I)^{-1}
      \tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{s,d}
      -\tfrac{1}{2}\log|K_s+\sigma_{n,s,d}^2I| \\
    &-\tfrac{|X_s|}{2}\log 2\pi .
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Top-1 Prediction.}
\label{sec:surr-top1}
The predictor uses the routed primary sub directly,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:top1}
    \tilde{\mathcal{S}}(\boldsymbol{\theta};W,H)
    =
    \mu_{s_{\mathrm{prod}}}(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}).
\end{equation}
Because the deterministic gate of Eqs.~\eqref{eq:gate}--\eqref{eq:y8q}
already selects a single locally well-supported expert, no inter-expert
aggregation is needed: top-$K$ ensembling on top of routing only adds
variance from regions the gate has already excluded.

\paragraph{Monotonic Post-Processing.}
\label{sec:surr-mono}
The physical flow-front distance is non-decreasing, so the decoded GP
mean is monotonised by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mono}
    \hat{y}_d\leftarrow \max_{j\le d}\hat{y}_j,
    \qquad d=1,\ldots,8 .
\end{equation}

\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
\caption{Offline construction of the trajectory-shape GP bank.}
\label{alg:train}
\KwData{MPM corpus $\{(\boldsymbol{\theta}_i,W_i,H_i,\mathbf{y}_i)\}$.}
\KwOut{Frozen bank of $25$ geometry-conditioned sub-banks, each
holding scalers, route metadata, sub assignments, and per-sub
exact-GP regressors.}
\BlankLine
Fit global input/output scalers (Eqs.~\eqref{eq:xscale}, \eqref{eq:yscale})\;
\ForEach{geometry sub-bank $g$}{
  build length bins from $y_8$ and, within each bin, PCA/K-means
  shape clusters from Eq.~\eqref{eq:phi}\;
  merge undersized clusters and freeze the assignment table\;
  \ForEach{sub $s$}{
    train eight independent exact-GP regressors (one per frame) by
    Eq.~\eqref{eq:gp-mll}\;
    store training inputs/outputs, validation error, and the per-sub
    summary statistics $\mathrm{median}(Y_{s,8})$, $\mathrm{std}(Y_{s,8})$
    used by the marginal-likelihood rerank\;
  }
}
\Return{production model bank.}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
\caption{Online forward pass
$\hat{\mathbf{y}}=\tilde{\mathcal{S}}(\boldsymbol{\theta};W,H)$.}
\label{alg:forward}
\KwIn{$\boldsymbol{\theta}$, geometry $(W,H)$, observed routing vector
$\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$.}
\KwOut{Monotone predicted flow-front vector $\hat{\mathbf{y}}$.}
\BlankLine
Route $(W,H,\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}})$ through
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:gate}--\eqref{eq:y8q} to select $s_{\mathrm{prod}}$\;

(Eq.~\eqref{eq:gp-predictive})\;

\Return{$\hat{\mathbf{y}}$ and GP predictive std.}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{GP-Aware Inverse}
\label{sec:inverse}

\paragraph{Within-Sub Warm-Start.}
\label{sec:inv-nn}
CMA-ES is initialised at the routed sub's nearest training point in
observation space,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:nn}
    \mathbf{z}^{\mathrm{NN}}
    =
    \mathbf{z}_{i^\star},\quad
    i^\star
    =
    \arg\min_{i\in\mathcal{D}_{s_{\mathrm{prod}}}}
    \frac{1}{8}\sum_{d=1}^{8}
    \alpha_d^2\left(y_{i,d}-y_{\mathrm{obs},d}\right)^2 .
\end{equation}
The frame weights $\boldsymbol{\alpha}=(\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_8)
=(0.2,0.2,0.35,0.5,0.8,1.0,1.4,2.0)$ progressively emphasise the
late frames. The schedule is empirical, motivated by the
\emph{real--sim gap} on the early gate-release frames: at the moment
of release, the rendered cube silhouette and the captured fluid
silhouette disagree by amounts the surrogate cannot model
(finite-time gate withdrawal, release-timing jitter, transient
free-surface effects, and surface tension that the MPM forward
omits). Late frames have settled into the steady spread regime in
which the surrogate matches reality cleanly, so weighting late
residuals more heavily aligns the loss with where the surrogate is
trustworthy. We treat this schedule as a practical
implementation choice rather than a methodological contribution.

\paragraph{Per-Sub Bound Tightening.}
\label{sec:inv-box}
The search box is the union of the selected top-$K$ sub supports
(top-$K=1$ in production), padded in $z$-space and clipped to the
global HB bounds,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tight-box}
    \Theta_{k^\star}
    =
    \Theta\cap
    [\mathbf{z}_{\min}-p\mathbf{s},\,
     \mathbf{z}_{\max}+p\mathbf{s}],
    \qquad p=0.10 .
\end{equation}
The objective also adds a soft support penalty with weight
$p_{\mathrm{sup}}=0.25$ outside the unpadded sub support.

\paragraph{Ridge Diagnosis Without an Explicit $\sigma_Y$ Prior.}
\label{sec:inv-sy}
Herschel--Bulkley observations can remain weakly identified along a
$(\eta,\sigma_Y)$ ridge. The production estimator does not break this
ridge with a hand-written $\sigma_Y$ prior; instead, it exposes the
remaining ambiguity through five CMA-ES restarts and an optional
finite-difference Hessian/Laplace confidence interval. This keeps the
reported uncertainty tied to the GP likelihood rather than to a
user-chosen anchor.

\paragraph{GP-Aware Type-II ML Loss.}
\label{sec:inv-loss}
For a candidate $\mathbf{z}$, the selected GP provides both
$\hat{y}_d(\mathbf{z})$ and predictive standard deviation
$\sigma_{\mathrm{GP},d}(\mathbf{z})$. We work in log-space and add a
measurement-noise floor $\sigma_{\mathrm{obs,log}}=0.20$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gp-var}
    v_d(\mathbf{z})
    =
    \left(
    \frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{GP},d}(\mathbf{z})}
         {\max(\hat{y}_d(\mathbf{z}),\epsilon)}
    \right)^2
    + \sigma_{\mathrm{obs,log}}^2 .
\end{equation}
The per-setup negative log marginal likelihood is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:obs-loss}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{GP}}(\mathbf{z})
    =
    \frac{1}{8}\sum_{d=1}^{8}
    \left[
      \frac{\alpha_d^2\, r_d(\mathbf{z})^2}{2v_d(\mathbf{z})}
      +\frac{1}{2}\log v_d(\mathbf{z})
    \right],
    \qquad
    r_d=\log\hat{y}_d-\log y_{\mathrm{obs},d}.
\end{equation}
The log-variance term is the Occam factor: it prevents the optimiser
from preferring regions only because the GP is uncertain there. The
production noise floor $\sigma_{\mathrm{obs,log}}=0.20$ is large
enough to keep the per-sample precision $1/v_d$ bounded across
$\boldsymbol{\theta}$, so the Occam term does not dominate the
likelihood; we report this as a frozen hyperparameter rather than as
a per-material tuning knob.

\paragraph{CMA-ES Configuration and Uncertainty Output.}
\label{sec:inv-cma}
We optimise Eq.~\eqref{eq:Pprime} with CMA-ES~\cite{hansen2016cma}
on $\mathbf{z}=(n,\log\eta,\log\sigma_Y)$ using population $\lambda=12$,
initial step $0.25$, at most $30$ generations, and five deterministic
restarts. The best restart is reported as the MAP estimate, while
the standard deviation across restart optima is retained as a
truth-blind ridge diagnostic. When enabled, a numerical Hessian of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:obs-loss} at the MAP gives a local Laplace 95\%
interval for $(n,\eta,\sigma_Y)$.

\paragraph{Batched Per-Generation Evaluation.}
\label{sec:inv-batch}
Every CMA-ES generation queries the surrogate at the
$\lambda=12$ candidate $\mathbf{z}$'s emitted by
\texttt{ask()}. A naive implementation issues twelve sequential GP
forward calls, each paying a fixed CUDA launch overhead that
dominates the per-call cost. Instead, we collect the whole
generation into a single batched forward call, which exact GPs
support natively for batched test inputs
(Eq.~\eqref{eq:gp-predictive} applied to a stacked
$\mathbf{X}_*$)~\cite{rasmussen2006gaussian}. The Mahalanobis,
Occam, and box-support terms of Eq.~\eqref{eq:obs-loss} are then
evaluated per-candidate in vectorised numpy outside the GPU. This
amortises the launch overhead across the generation and yields an
empirical $8$--$13\times$ wall-clock speedup on the joint
two-setup inverse, with no change to the optimisation trajectory.

\paragraph{Joint Two-Setup Inverse.}
\label{sec:inv-joint}
For two observations $a,b$ of the same material, the two routed
setups retain their own GP experts but share the same
$\boldsymbol{\theta}$. The production implementation minimises
Eq.~\eqref{eq:Pstar}; if the two sub boxes overlap, the intersection
is used, otherwise their padded union provides a conservative search
box. The estimate from setup~$a$ is used as a warm start for the
joint stage, so that setup~$b$ contributes information \emph{on top
of} the single-setup posterior rather than restarting the search.

\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
\caption{Online single-setup inverse.}
\label{alg:invert}
\KwIn{$\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$, $(W,H)$, production GP bank.}
\KwOut{$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$, posterior diagnostics.}
\BlankLine
Route $(W,H,\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}})$ to the primary sub
(Eq.~\eqref{eq:y8q})\;
construct the padded box $\Theta_{k^\star}$ and nearest-neighbour
warm start (Eqs.~\eqref{eq:tight-box}, \eqref{eq:nn})\;
run five-restart CMA-ES on Eq.~\eqref{eq:Pprime}\;
optionally evaluate the Hessian interval at the best point\;
\Return{$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ and diagnostics.}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Hessian-Orthogonal Setup-2 Proposal (Inherited)}
\label{sec:proposer}

The choice of the second container geometry $(W_2,H_2)$ is not part
of our methodological contribution. We use the closed-form,
Fisher-information-based proposer of
\citet{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} (DOI \texttt{10.1145/3618310})
verbatim, and summarise it here only so that the joint algorithm
that follows is self-contained.

Given the single-setup estimate
$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_1$ obtained from the first geometry
$(W_1,H_1)$, \citet{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} approximate the dam
break by plane-Poiseuille flow under the Herschel--Bulkley
constitutive law and derive an analytical Hessian
$\mathbf{H}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_1;W,H)$ of their similarity
loss with respect to $\boldsymbol{\theta}$. Let
$\mathbf{q}_1$ be the unit eigenvector of
$\mathbf{H}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_1;W_1,H_1)$ associated with the
smallest eigenvalue: this is the direction in
$\boldsymbol{\theta}$-space along which the first setup carries the
\emph{least} information. A second setup is informative to the joint
estimator precisely when its principal least-informative direction
$\mathbf{q}_2(W,H)$ is orthogonal to $\mathbf{q}_1$, since the joint
Fisher information is then full-rank in the
$(\mathbf{q}_1,\mathbf{q}_2)$ subspace. The proposer therefore
selects
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:proposer}
    (W_2^\star,H_2^\star)
    \;=\;
    \arg\min_{(W,H)\in[2,7]^2\,\text{cm}}
    \bigl\langle \mathbf{q}_1,\,\mathbf{q}_2(W,H)\bigr\rangle^2 ,
\end{equation}
on a half-centimetre grid. Because the Hessian is closed-form, no
forward simulations or surrogate calls are required to evaluate
Eq.~\eqref{eq:proposer}.

We adopt this proposer without modification: our contribution is to
make the joint estimate that consumes its output affordable per
material rather than per showcase. The implementation is a faithful
port of the formulas in \citet{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}, and any
material-independent derivation steps---definition of the similarity
loss, the plane-Poiseuille reduction, and the analytical Hessian---are
properly attributed to that paper.
\label{alg:joint}
\KwIn{Two observations of the same material,
$(\mathbf{y}^{(a)},W_a,H_a)$ and
$(\mathbf{y}^{(b)},W_b,H_b)$.}
\KwOut{Shared $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$.}
\BlankLine
Route both setups independently to their primary subs\;
form the joint search box from the two sub supports\;
minimise the summed GP-aware likelihood of Eq.~\eqref{eq:Pstar}
with five-restart CMA-ES\;
\Return{shared $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ and two-setup diagnostics.}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Camera Calibration}
\label{sec:calib}

To run the inverse on a real experiment, we need to convert a
smartphone video of the dam-break flow into the observation vector
$\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}} = (x_{1}, \dots, x_{8}) \in
\mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}^{8}$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:y-def}. We use a two-stage
camera calibration followed by silhouette-based flow-front
extraction; the full pipeline is summarised in
Algorithm~\ref{alg:calib}.

\paragraph{Stage 1: ChArUco prior.}
A printed ChArUco
board~\cite{garrido2014automatic,garrido2016generation} is placed in
the experimental scene alongside the fluid container. The standard
pinhole projection~\cite{hartley2004multiple} sends a world point
$\mathbf{P}_{w}$ to pixels via
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pinhole}
    \mathbf{P}_{c} \;=\; \mathbf{R}\,\mathbf{P}_{w} + \mathbf{t},
    \qquad
    \mathbf{p} \;=\; \tfrac{1}{Z_{c}}\,\mathbf{K}\,\mathbf{P}_{c},
    \quad
    \mathbf{K} = \begin{pmatrix}
          f_{x} & 0     & c_{x} \\
          0     & f_{y} & c_{y} \\
          0     & 0     & 1
        \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
Rather than optimising $(K, R, \mathbf{t})$ as $11$ free scalars, we
exploit the fact that our rig is level and the camera looks at a
fixed target, and parameterise the whole camera by a compact
$6$-vector
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:theta-cam}
    \boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{cam}}
    \;=\;
    (l_{x},\,l_{z},\,\theta_{E},\,\phi_{E},\,
    \mathrm{fov},\,d_{\mathrm{eye}}),
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{l} = (l_{x}, 0, l_{z})^{\!\top}$ is the look-at point
on the rig floor, $(\theta_{E}, \phi_{E})$ are the polar and
azimuthal angles of the eye direction, $d_{\mathrm{eye}}$ is the
eye--target distance, and $\mathrm{fov}$ is the vertical field of
view. The camera roll is held fixed by construction (the rig is
level and the renderer enforces a horizontal $\mathbf{C}_{x}$ axis,
Eq.~\eqref{eq:basis}). The eye position and
an orthonormal camera basis are then reconstructed deterministically
from $\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{cam}}$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:eye}
    \mathbf{E}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{cam}})
    \;=\; d_{\mathrm{eye}}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \sin\theta_{E}\cos\phi_{E} \\
        \cos\theta_{E} \\
        -\sin\theta_{E}\sin\phi_{E}
    \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:basis}
    \mathbf{C}_{z} = \frac{\mathbf{E} - \mathbf{l}}
                           {\|\mathbf{E} - \mathbf{l}\|},
    \quad
    \mathbf{C}_{x} = \frac{(C_{z,3},\,0,\,-C_{z,1})^{\!\top}}
                           {\|(C_{z,3},\,0,\,-C_{z,1})\|},
    \quad
    \mathbf{C}_{y} = \mathbf{C}_{z} \times \mathbf{C}_{x},
\end{equation}
so that $R = [\mathbf{C}_{x}\,|\,\mathbf{C}_{y}\,|\,\mathbf{C}_{z}]^{\!\top}$ and
$\mathbf{t} = -R\,\mathbf{E}$; the $\mathbf{C}_{x}$ construction
enforces a horizontal camera up-axis. A world point $\mathbf{P}_{i}$
thus maps first to camera coordinates
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:screen}
    \mathbf{Y}_{i}
    \;=\;
    \bigl(
      (\mathbf{P}_{i} - \mathbf{E})\!\cdot\!\mathbf{C}_{x},\;
      (\mathbf{P}_{i} - \mathbf{E})\!\cdot\!\mathbf{C}_{y},\;
      (\mathbf{P}_{i} - \mathbf{E})\!\cdot\!\mathbf{C}_{z}
    \bigr),
\end{equation}
and then to pixels by perspective division. Matching the renderer's
vertical-field-of-view convention $f_{y} = H/(2\tan(\mathrm{fov}/2))$
gives the scale factor
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:scale}
    s \;=\; \frac{1}{2\,\tan(\mathrm{fov}/2)},
\end{equation}
and the full reprojection of $\mathbf{P}_{i}$ into the image plane
$[0, W]\times[0, H]$ reads
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:project}
    \pi(\mathbf{P}_{i};\,\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{cam}})
    \;=\;
    \left(
        \frac{W}{2} + s\,H\,\frac{[\mathbf{Y}_{i}]_{1}}{|[\mathbf{Y}_{i}]_{3}|},\;
        \frac{H}{2} - s\,H\,\frac{[\mathbf{Y}_{i}]_{2}}{|[\mathbf{Y}_{i}]_{3}|}
    \right).
\end{equation}

After sub-pixel corner refinement~\cite{forstner1987fast} on the
detected ChArUco board, Stage~1 solves
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:stage1}
    \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\mathrm{cam}}
    \;=\;
    \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{cam}}}\,
    \frac{1}{|\mathcal{I}|}
    \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}}
    \rho_{\delta}\!\bigl(
      \|\mathbf{u}_{i} - \pi(\mathbf{P}_{i};\,\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{cam}})\|
    \bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{I}$ indexes detected corners with pixel observations
$\mathbf{u}_{i}$ and known board coordinates $\mathbf{P}_{i}$, and
$\rho_{\delta}$ is the Huber loss with a $\delta = 2$\,px threshold,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:huber}
    \rho_{\delta}(e)
    \;=\;
    \begin{cases}
        \tfrac{1}{2}e^{2}, & e < \delta, \\
        \delta\bigl(e - \tfrac{1}{2}\delta\bigr), & e \ge \delta,
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
which prevents any single mis-refined corner from dominating the fit.
The minimisation uses Nelder--Mead~\cite{nelder1965simplex} because
$\pi$ is cheap to evaluate but awkward to differentiate under the
$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{cam}}$ parameterisation; the adaptive
variant of~\citet{gao2012implementing} is reserved for the
non-smooth Phase~B below, where its larger contraction step helps
escape pixel-grid plateaus of the IoU objective. Stage~1
returns an intrinsic matrix $\mathbf{K}_{0}$ (derived from
$\mathrm{fov}$ and the image size) and an initial extrinsic
$(R_{0}, \mathbf{t}_{0})$ (derived from $\mathbf{E}, \mathbf{l}$
via Eq.~\eqref{eq:basis}).

\paragraph{Stage 2: silhouette-guided extrinsic refinement.}
Stage~1 is typically accurate to a few pixels but can leave a small
residual offset, since the ChArUco board is not rigidly coupled to
the container. Stage~2 \emph{fixes} $\mathbf{K}_{0}$ and refines only the
extrinsic parameters so that a rendered container silhouette
aligns with the reference frame $I_{0}$ captured at the instant
of dam release. We use a two-phase schedule.

\emph{Phase~A (smooth chamfer).}
A Canny edge detector~\cite{canny1986computational} applied to
$I_{0}$ yields a target-edge pixel set $E$, from which we
precompute an exact Euclidean distance
transform~\cite{maurer2003linear,borgefors1986distance}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:edt}
    D_{E}(p) \;=\;
    \min_{q \in E}\,\|\,p - q\,\|_{2}
    \qquad \text{for each pixel } p \in \Omega.
\end{equation}
For candidate extrinsics $(R, \mathbf{t})$, let $P_{f}(R, \mathbf{t})$
denote the projected edge pixels of the $f$-th visible cube face
and $\mathcal{F}$ the set of visible faces. The per-face-normalised
weighted chamfer cost is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:chamfer}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cham}}(R, \mathbf{t})
    \;=\;
    \frac{1}{|\mathcal{F}|}
    \sum_{f \in \mathcal{F}}
    \frac{\sum_{p \in P_{f}(R,\mathbf{t})}\, w(p)\, D_{E}(p)}
         {\sum_{p \in P_{f}(R,\mathbf{t})}\, w(p)},
\end{equation}
with $D_{E}(p)$ bilinearly interpolated from the EDT and
$w(p)$ a product of three side-emphasis weights
$w_{\mathrm{left}}(p_{x})$, $w_{\mathrm{top}}(p_{y})$, and
$w_{\mathrm{bottom}}(p_{y})$.
Per-face normalisation prevents long edges (e.g.\ the bottom wall)
from dominating short ones. We minimise $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cham}}$
with L-BFGS-B~\cite{byrd1995limited,zhu1997algorithm} over the six
DoF of a Rodrigues rotation vector~\cite{rodrigues1840lois} and a
translation, box-clamped to $\pm 2.5^{\circ}$ and $\pm 2$\,cm around
the Stage-$1$ prior $(\mathbf{r}_{0}, \mathbf{t}_{0})$.

\emph{Phase~B (non-smooth IoU).}
Starting from the Phase-$A$ solution, we maximise a weighted Jaccard
similarity / pixel-IoU~\cite{jaccard1912distribution} between the
rendered silhouette $M(R, \mathbf{t})$ and the target foreground $T$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:iou}
    \mathrm{IoU}_{w}(R, \mathbf{t})
    \;=\;
    \frac{\sum_{p}\, w(p)\,[\,M(p)\wedge T(p)\,]}
         {\sum_{p}\, w(p)\,[\,M(p)\vee T(p)\,]},
\end{equation}
with an adaptive Nelder--Mead~\cite{gao2012implementing} simplex of
size $0.15^{\circ}/1.5$\,mm. Because $\mathrm{IoU}_{w}$ is
discretised on the pixel grid it is non-differentiable; the
L-BFGS-B phase on the smooth chamfer cost provides the warm start
needed to avoid the combinatorial local minima of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:iou}.

\paragraph{Safety clamp.}
After refinement we check the deviation from the Stage-$1$ prior,
$\Delta_{\mathrm{rot}} = \|\mathbf{r}^{*} - \mathbf{r}_{0}\|$ and
$\Delta_{t} = \|\mathbf{t}^{*} - \mathbf{t}_{0}\|$; if either
exceeds the safety threshold ($3^{\circ}$ or $3$\,cm by default) we
fall back to the ChArUco extrinsic. The safety threshold is set
slightly looser than the Phase-$A$ box ($2.5^{\circ}/2$\,cm), so
that the unbounded Phase-$B$ Nelder--Mead can in principle move
beyond the box but only mild excursions are accepted; this guards
against strong local minima in either phase, particularly when the
ChArUco board placement is sloppy.

\paragraph{Flow-front extraction.}
Each subsequent frame $I_{1}, \dots, I_{8}$ captured at
$t_{1}, \dots, t_{8}$
is binarised by HSV-intensity thresholding (threshold $128$, black
foreground by default) followed by morphological opening and closing
on a $3\times 3$ kernel~\cite{gonzalez2018digital}; only the largest
connected foreground component is retained to reject spurious
specks. We then extract the outer contour $\partial\Omega_{i}$ of
the foreground mask at frame $i$ and back-project every contour
pixel $\mathbf{u} = (u, v)$ to the world ground plane $y = 0$
through the calibrated pinhole camera. With
$\mathbf{C}_{w} = -R^{\!\top}\,\mathbf{t}$ the world-frame camera
centre and
$\mathbf{d}(\mathbf{u}) = R^{\!\top}\,\mathbf{K}_{0}^{-1}\,(u, v, 1)^{\!\top}$
the world-frame ray direction, the ray
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:backproj}
    \mathbf{P}_{w}(\tau)
    \;=\; \mathbf{C}_{w} + \tau\,\mathbf{d}(\mathbf{u})
\end{equation}
meets the floor at
$\tau^{\star} = -[\mathbf{C}_{w}]_{y}/[\mathbf{d}(\mathbf{u})]_{y}$.
Let $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^{2}$ be the unit flow direction in
the world $xz$-plane ($\mathbf{v} = (1, 0)$ for our apparatus), and
let $x^{\mathrm{front}}_{\mathrm{box}}$ be the container's
front-wall position read from the experiment manifest. The flow
distance at frame $t_{i}$ is the farthest signed projection of
$\partial\Omega_{i}$ onto $\mathbf{v}$ relative to that front wall,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:xi}
    x_{i} \;=\;
    \max_{\mathbf{u} \in \partial\Omega_{i}}
    \Bigl[\mathbf{v}^{\!\top}
          \bigl(\mathbf{P}_{w}(\tau^{\star}(\mathbf{u}))\bigr)_{xz}\Bigr]
    \;-\; x^{\mathrm{front}}_{\mathrm{box}}.
\end{equation}
Finally, residual binarisation noise can introduce occasional
backward jumps across frames; we guard against this with a monotone
pass $x_{i} \leftarrow \max(x_{i}, x_{i-1})$ (equivalent to
Eq.~\eqref{eq:mono} applied in physical space), producing the
observation
$\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}} = (x_{1}, \dots, x_{8})
\in \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}^{8}$ that the inverse of
Algorithms~\ref{alg:invert} and~\ref{alg:joint} consumes.

\caption{Stage 1 reprojection oracle.}

\KwIn{$6$-vector $\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{cam}}{=}

\KwOut{Projected pixels $\{\pi(\mathbf{P}_{i};

\BlankLine

\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
\caption{Two-stage camera calibration.}
\label{alg:calib}
\KwIn{Background photo $I_{\mathrm{bg}}$ with ChArUco board;

  Huber reprojection loss $\mathcal{L}_{1}$ (Eqs.~\eqref{eq:stage1},
  \eqref{eq:huber}), using Algorithm~\ref{alg:reproj} for $\pi$\;
Extract $(\mathbf{K}_{0},R_{0},\mathbf{t}_{0})$ from

  (Eqs.~\eqref{eq:eye}--\eqref{eq:scale})\;

\tcp*[h]{Stage 2: silhouette-guided refinement (fix $\mathbf{K}_{0}$).}
Compute Canny edge set $E$ on $I_{0}$ and EDT $D_{E}$
  (Eq.~\eqref{eq:edt})\;
\emph{Phase A:} L-BFGS-B on chamfer cost
  $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cham}}$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:chamfer}) over
  $(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{t})$, box-clamped to
  $\pm\Delta_{\mathrm{rot}}^{\mathrm{box}}/\pm\Delta_{t}^{\mathrm{box}}$
  around $(\mathbf{r}_{0},\mathbf{t}_{0})$~\cite{byrd1995limited,
  zhu1997algorithm}\;

}

\end{algorithm}

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

We evaluate HVIMoGP-rBCM and the identifiability-aware inverse
against four questions: (1) how faithfully does the surrogate
approximate the MPM forward model, (2) how accurately does the full
inverse recover $\boldsymbol{\theta} = (\sigma_Y, \eta, n)$ on
held-out samples, (3) what wall-clock reduction does the surrogate
deliver relative to simulation-in-the-loop inversion, and (4) do the
three contributions of \S1---freshness, rheometer-infeasible
materials, and multi-observation precision---hold under experimental
validation?

\paragraph{Datasets.}
We use three evaluation sets. \emph{$T_{\mathrm{sim}}$} contains
$22{,}209$ MPM samples held out from the $295{,}419$-row training
corpus, sampled stratified over
$(n, \log\eta, \log\sigma_Y, W, H)$. \emph{$T_{\mathrm{inv}}$} is a
synthetic benchmark of $30$ materials drawn from within the training
box, each rendered via MPM at three container sizes (total $90$
clips). \emph{$T_{\mathrm{real}}$} consists of six to eight standard
non-Newtonian fluids (honey, ketchup, mayonnaise, shampoo, Carbopol
gel, and a lotion sample) captured on a smartphone in our dam-break
apparatus and accompanied by parallel-plate rheometer sweeps.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
\emph{Baseline A} is the simulation-in-the-loop pipeline
of~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}: identical observation, identical
CMA-ES hyperparameters, but every candidate evaluated by a fresh MPM
run rather than by the surrogate. Baseline~A defines the accuracy
upper bound and wall-time lower bound of the simulation-in-the-loop
family. \emph{Baseline B} is a reduced version of our own pipeline
that isolates the contribution of hierarchical gating and the
identifiability-aware inverse: it uses a flat Bayesian GMM over
$(W, H)$ with $K = 12$ and no second-level $\phi$ gate ($12$ experts
total), top-$2$ hard routing, and a CMA-ES inverse without warm-start
or $\sigma_Y$ prior.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
For the forward surrogate we report per-output mean absolute error
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mae}
    \mathrm{MAE}(\hat{\mathbf{y}}, \mathbf{y})
    \;=\; \frac{1}{D_Y}\sum_{d=1}^{D_Y}|\hat y_d - y_d|,
\end{equation}
coefficient of determination $R^{2} = 1 - \sum_{i}
\|\hat{\mathbf{y}}_i - \mathbf{y}_i\|^2 / \sum_i \|\mathbf{y}_i -
\bar{\mathbf{y}}\|^2$, and the row-max error distribution
($p_{50}, p_{95}, p_{99}, p_{999}$). For the inverse we report
per-parameter relative error
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:relE}
    \mathrm{relE}(\hat\theta_d;\,\theta_d^{\mathrm{gt}})
    \;=\; \frac{|\hat\theta_d - \theta_d^{\mathrm{gt}}|}
               {|\theta_d^{\mathrm{gt}}|},
    \qquad d \in \{n, \eta, \sigma_Y\},
\end{equation}
the forward NMSE at the recovered parameter as a sanity check on
fit, and the per-sample wall-clock. For comparisons against
parallel-plate reference sweeps (\S\ref{sec:rheometer}) we further
report the integrated log-stress error over the experimentally valid
shear-rate interval $\Gamma = [\dot\gamma_{\min}, \dot\gamma_{\max}]$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:E}
    \mathcal{E}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}, \boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{gt}})
    \;=\;
    \left(\frac{1}{|\Gamma|}
    \int_{\Gamma}
      \bigl[\log\hat\tau(\dot\gamma)
          - \log\tau_{\mathrm{gt}}(\dot\gamma)\bigr]^{2}
    \,d\log\dot\gamma\right)^{\!1/2}.
\end{equation}
All inverse runs use the frozen hyperparameters of
Table~\ref{tab:hyper} and a fixed seed.


\subsection{Surrogate Fidelity}
\label{sec:fidelity}

We first verify that the trained surrogate predicts MPM outputs
accurately enough to justify replacing the simulator inside the
inverse. On $T_{\mathrm{sim}}$, the full HVIMoGP-rBCM surrogate
attains aggregate MAE $0.042$\,cm and $R^2 > 0.999$ across all
eight flow-front dimensions. Errors concentrate near partition
boundaries of the hierarchical gate---where a slightly mis-routed
query lands between specialised experts---and in sparsely sampled
regions of the input box. The degree-$2$ polynomial residual
correction (Eq.~the legacy polynomial-residual step) is specifically designed to suppress
the former, reducing the $p_{999}$ row-max error by roughly a factor
of two.

\subsection{Parameter Freshness for Time-Varying Fluids}
\label{sec:freshness}

The headline payoff of compressing the inverse from hours to seconds
is that the recovered parameters describe the material at the
\emph{instant} of measurement, not at the start of a multi-hour
optimisation run. We test this claim directly on materials whose
Herschel--Bulkley parameters evolve on a sub-hour timescale, and
recover their full kinetic curves from dam-break video alone.

\paragraph{Material panel: starch-network kinetics with concentration sweeps.}
The classical reference experiment for time-resolved rheology is
the build-up of yield stress $\sigma_Y(t)$ during starch
retrogradation~\cite{miles1985gelation,karim2000methods,hoover2001composition}.
We instantiate this as a $3 \times 3$ panel in which three
supermarket starch sources are each prepared at three concentrations
(Table~\ref{tab:fresh-mats}): instant rolled oats and potato starch
(katakuriko) cover amylose-rich and amylopectin-rich starches with
documented concentration scaling
$\sigma_{Y,\infty}\propto c^{\alpha}$ with $\alpha\in[2,5]$
\cite{hoover2001composition,roos1995phase}, and a Japanese pancake
mix adds a composite wheat starch + leavener system whose
network-formation rate differs from pure starch
\cite{larson1999structure}. Concentrations were chosen so that the
asymptotic yield stress in each material spans the surrogate's
training box, $\sigma_Y\in[10,100]$\,Pa, by the analytical
$\sigma_{Y,\infty}\propto c^{\alpha}$ scaling.

\paragraph{Capture protocol.}
For each of the nine $($material$,$ concentration$)$ conditions, the
same batch is sampled at six time points $t_i\in\{15,30,60,90,150,
240\}$\,min after preparation, kept at $25^{\circ}$C between
captures. At each $t_i$ we run our two-setup dam-break protocol of
\S\ref{sec:method-block}: a setup-1 geometry $(W_1,H_1)$ is drawn
once at the beginning of each material session by uniform random
sampling from the calibrated training box $[2,7]^2$\,cm; the
setup-2 geometry $(W_2,H_2)$ is then re-derived at every $t_i$ from
the running estimate $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_1(t_i)$ via the
Hessian-orthogonal proposer of
\S\ref{sec:proposer}~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}. The
two captures of a $t_i$ pair are taken approximately $14$\,min apart
($\sim 7$\,min before and after the nominal $t_i$), so that within a
single pair the material state has changed by less than the
single-setup confidence half-width under the kinetic model below.
Across the full session, each condition therefore yields six joint
estimates $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(t_i)$ with full Laplace 95\% CIs
from \S\ref{sec:inv-cma}, recovered in seconds per time point on the
same surrogate bank used everywhere else in the paper.

\paragraph{Kinetic fit and timescale recovery.}
For each condition, the recovered yield-stress trajectory is fit to
the first-order Avrami / build-up
form~\cite{avrami1939kinetics,mewis2009thixotropy,bates1988nonlinear}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:avrami}
    \sigma_Y(t)
    \;=\;
    \sigma_{Y,\infty}\,\bigl(1 - e^{-t/T_k}\bigr),
\end{equation}
by weighted nonlinear least squares with the per-time-point Laplace
half-widths from Eq.~(\ref{eq:obs-loss}) as observation
uncertainties (Bates and Watts~\cite{bates1988nonlinear}). The
estimated relaxation time $T_k$ and asymptote
$\sigma_{Y,\infty}$ are reported with $1\sigma$ intervals from the
fit covariance. If the exponential residual exceeds twice the
observation band on more than $30\%$ of the time points, we
report the result of an Avrami-$n>1$
sigmoid~\cite{avrami1939kinetics} or a two-component
amylose+amylopectin model~\cite{miles1985gelation} as well, and
flag the condition as having a non-first-order kinetic mechanism.
This single, falsifiable kinetic
hypothesis~\cite{box1987empirical,roache1998verification} replaces
ad-hoc per-material fits.

\paragraph{Validation against independent anchors.}
At the per-condition level we score recovered trajectories against
three independent anchors (Table~\ref{tab:fresh-anchors}):
\emph{(A)}~the simulation-in-the-loop inverse
of~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} run on the same
$\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$ exports of the dam-break video,
quantifying the cost of replacing MPM-in-loop with our surrogate;
\emph{(B)}~a parallel-plate rheometer sweep at $t=24$\,h on the same
batch, which captures the kinetic asymptote
$\sigma_{Y,\infty}$~\cite{macosko1994rheology}; and \emph{(D)}~visual
sim-real overlay, where MPM forward simulation under the recovered
$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(t_i)$ is rendered in the calibrated camera
and compared frame-by-frame against the captured dam-break video,
directly visualising the freshness benefit. Cross-condition checks
\emph{(G)} (concentration scaling: $T_k(c)$ decreasing,
$\sigma_{Y,\infty}(c)$ increasing per
Hoover~\cite{hoover2001composition} and Roos~\cite{roos1995phase})
and \emph{(H)} (mechanism ranking across the three starch sources)
test the \emph{shape} of the recovered curves rather than any single
number, which is what an inverse-rheometry method must ultimately
deliver.

\paragraph{Why freshness is the headline contribution.}
At $\sim 8$\,h per setup, a simulation-in-the-loop inverse can
return at most one estimate per condition before
$\sigma_Y(t)$ has changed by an order of magnitude; the recovered
parameter is then a smeared average over the kinetic window rather
than a state estimate. At $\sim 15$\,s per setup, our inverse
returns six time-resolved $\hat{\sigma}_Y(t_i)$ within the same
$240$\,min window, with each estimate fresher than the next time
sample. The kinetic curves of Fig.~\ref{fig:fresh-traj} are
therefore not merely a denser version of the prior result; they are
qualitatively new information that an hour-scale inverse cannot
produce on the same materials.


\subsection{Characterising Rheometer-Infeasible Materials}

\subsection{Multi-Observation Joint Protocol}
\label{sec:multi}

Contribution~(c) addresses the $\sigma_Y$-identifiability

\subsection{End-to-End Capture-to-Parameter Time Budget}
\label{sec:timebudget}

We close the evaluation by looking at \emph{total} time from raw
phone video to a recovered Herschel--Bulkley triple. Two time sinks
dominate: the manual capture-and-calibration workflow that takes an
experimenter's attention, and the automated inverse that takes
compute. Both are substantially shorter in our pipeline than in the
direct predecessor's.

\paragraph{Manual capture and calibration time.}
Table~\ref{tab:calib-time} breaks the human-in-the-loop portion of
the pipeline into its four steps. The predecessor's camera
calibration goes through \texttt{fSpy} (manual vanishing-point
fitting) followed by \texttt{Blender} (manual alignment and
background render), which together consume nearly $16$\,min of
attention per capture and are the dominant source of manual
variance. Our two-stage ChArUco + OpenGL calibration
(\S\ref{sec:calib}) replaces both steps with a single non-interactive
invocation that completes in $1$\,min\,$12$\,s on a consumer laptop,
saving an average of $14$\,min\,$43$\,s per capture. Video editing
(\texttt{Premiere Pro} frame selection) and mask cleanup
(\texttt{Photoshop}) are unchanged, because they operate on the raw
footage and are orthogonal to the calibration method. End-to-end,
the manual workflow drops from $25$\,min to $10$\,min per capture.

in~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} as the identifiability
mitigation but left computationally infeasible in practice---scales
the hours-per-setup cost linearly with the number of setups.

\paragraph{End-to-end.}
Combining the two components, our per-material pipeline finishes in
roughly $10$--$11$\,min of wall-clock (manual $\sim\!10$\,min
$+$ automated $\le 1$\,min), versus hours of manual calibration
followed by additional hours of MPM-in-loop inversion under the
predecessor---a difference of more than an order of magnitude in
both the human-attention budget and the compute budget. More
importantly, the automated portion completes within the time window
of a single user session, which is what makes the freshness,
fieldable-capture, and multi-setup capabilities of
\S\ref{sec:freshness}--\ref{sec:rheometer} practically accessible
rather than nominal.


\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{What the Acceleration Unlocks}

The immediate effect of our pipeline is a three-orders-of-magnitude
reduction in per-video inverse cost. The more important effect is
that three capabilities previously out of reach under
simulation-in-the-loop become routine:
\emph{freshness} for time-varying
fluids (\S\ref{sec:freshness}),
practical characterisation of \emph{rheometer-infeasible}
materials (\S\ref{sec:chunky}), and
\emph{multi-observation joint inversion} that approaches
parallel-plate rheometer precision (\S\ref{sec:multi}). The first is
a temporal improvement that turns ``hours to return a past state''
into ``seconds to return the current state.'' The second inherits
the independent value of video rheometry over instrumental
rheometry~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} and brings it to
fieldable speed. The third makes usable a $\sigma_Y$-mitigation path
that the predecessor work identified but left computationally out of
reach.

\subsection{A Reusable Surrogate as a Community Asset}

The separation of a one-time offline training phase from a
seconds-scale online inference phase produces a reusable artefact
that is structurally unavailable to simulation-in-the-loop pipelines.
Our frozen $540$-expert checkpoint was trained once on
$295{,}419$ MPM simulations covering $5.5$--$5.6$ orders of magnitude
in $\eta$ and $\sigma_Y$ and the full $[2, 7]\,\mathrm{cm}$ range of
household containers. Subsequent users---and subsequent research on
video rheometry---can query the same checkpoint without paying the
MPM training cost again. This amortisation property is what turns
our method from a one-shot inverse into a reusable measurement
instrument; it is not available to sim-in-loop pipelines because
they have no surrogate to amortise. We release the checkpoint, the
training corpus, and the inverse code alongside this paper.

\subsection{Why Regime-Aware Surrogate and Data-Honest Prior Must Go
Together}

A takeaway at the core of this work is that neither the surrogate
nor the inverse modifications is sufficient alone. A single global
GP, even if it were accurate enough, would still leave the
$\sigma_Y$-identifiability ridge intact under a vanilla CMA-ES: the
optimiser would slide to $\sigma_Y \to 0$ while inflating $\eta$ to
match the observation. Conversely, the identifiability-aware inverse
of \S\ref{sec:inverse} requires an accurate surrogate to be evaluated
$300$ times per video within seconds; running the same inverse
against a coarse surrogate would reintroduce surrogate bias as a new
source of error. The hierarchical MoGP-rBCM surrogate and the
training-data-anchored prior must be coupled; this coupling is the
technical core.

\subsection{Relation to Differentiable MPM}

Differentiable MPM pipelines~\cite{li2023pacnerf,eastman2024recovery}
obtain gradients through the forward simulator and perform
gradient-based per-scene parameter search. We differ in two
respects. First, gradients through the non-smooth HB return map are
known to be
ill-conditioned~\cite{huang2021plasticinelab}; our gradient-free
inverse sidesteps this issue. Second, differentiable-MPM pipelines
optimise per scene and do not amortise a reusable surrogate, so
their per-material cost remains in the simulation-in-the-loop
regime. Our architectural choice of an offline-trained hierarchical
surrogate is precisely what enables the seconds-scale per-video
latency and the community-asset property of \S6.2.


\subsection{Conclusion}

We presented a surrogate-accelerated framework for non-Newtonian
video rheometry that compresses the Herschel--Bulkley inverse from
around eight hours per setup in simulation-in-the-loop prior
work~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} to a median of $12$\,s per
video on a single consumer GPU. The method rests on two coupled
components: a regime-aware hierarchical Mixture-of-GP surrogate
(HVIMoGP-rBCM) with $540$ specialised experts routed by a two-level
Bayesian Gaussian mixture gate over container geometry and
observation signatures, and an identifiability-aware inverse that
warm-starts CMA-ES from the routed expert's nearest training
neighbour and anchors the $\sigma_Y$ axis with a data-honest soft
prior---a complementary mitigation for the
yield-stress/consistency similarity degeneracy that~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}
treated through Hessian-based similarity analysis, a
Poiseuille-flow proxy, active setup selection, and iterative
multi-setup CMA-ES. Our path trades the Hessian machinery for
training-data priors and joint (rather than sequential) multi-setup
inversion, both made practical by the surrogate.

Beyond runtime reduction, the seconds-scale inverse unlocks three
capabilities inaccessible to simulation-in-the-loop rheometry: fresh
parameter estimates for time-varying fluids, practical
characterisation of materials conventional rheometers cannot
measure, and a multi-observation joint protocol that approaches
parallel-plate rheometer precision from phone videos alone. We
release the trained surrogate, the $295{,}419$-row MPM training
corpus, and the inverse code as a reusable community artefact on
which subsequent video-rheometry research can build without paying
the simulation training cost again.

The broader message is that video rheometry crosses the threshold
from laboratory demonstration to fieldable tool not by replacing
physics-based estimation but by amortising its cost. The surrogate
is an accelerator of a physics-grounded inverse, not a substitute
for physics; what this acceleration enables, at seconds per video,
is what brings the technique within reach of time-sensitive,
iterative, and situated measurement.


\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}

\subsection{Information-Theoretic $\sigma_Y$ Limit}
The identifiability-aware inverse of \S\ref{sec:inverse} resolves
the yield-stress/consistency degeneracy whenever the dam-break
observation carries \emph{some} signal about $\sigma_Y$, but it
cannot recover information that is not in the observation. For
materials in which $\eta\,\dot\gamma^{\,n} \gg \sigma_Y$ across the
entire dam-break shear-rate range, the flow front encodes
essentially no information about $\sigma_Y$, and any inverse---ours
included---returns an estimate determined by the prior and the
training distribution rather than by the data. Among the $10$
held-out samples of Table~\ref{tab:main}, one such material
($\sigma_Y^{\mathrm{true}} \approx 0.39$\,Pa in a low-$\eta$ regime)
pushes the $\sigma_Y$ \emph{mean} to $7.0$ while the nine remaining
samples sit at median $0.298$. This is a task-level bound, not a
method bound; the appropriate mitigation is the multi-setup protocol
of \S\ref{sec:multi}.

\subsection{Dependence on Training-Set Coverage}
The surrogate is trustworthy only inside the input box on which it
was trained. Queries outside this box trigger the per-expert bound
tightening (\S\ref{sec:inverse}), which relies on the routed
expert's training inputs as the admissible region and therefore
does not extrapolate. Out-of-distribution materials require an
extension of the training corpus. rBCM predictive variance flags
out-of-distribution queries but does not by itself correct them.

\subsection{Fidelity of the Forward Model}
A surrogate can only be as accurate as the simulator it learns from.
Our MPM forward (\S3.2) models HB rheology, gravity, and no-slip
boundaries, but not surface tension, elastic overshoot, thixotropy,
or viscoelastic memory. Materials exhibiting these phenomena deviate
systematically from the HB forward, and this deviation cannot be
recovered by any HB inverse. Richer constitutive
families---viscoelastic extensions and damage-MPM
variants~\cite{wolper2019cdmpm}---are natural but out-of-scope
directions.

\subsection{Capture and Geometry Requirements}
The level-$1$ gate is trained on rectangular cube-like containers
with $W, H \in [2, 7]$\,cm. Curved or irregular containers require
retraining the geometry gate on a broader container descriptor.
Camera calibration uses a printed ChArUco marker board; markerless
calibration from natural scene content is possible in principle but
not demonstrated here.

\subsection{Real-Time as a Direction}
Our median wall-clock is $12$\,s per video on a single consumer
GPU---seconds-scale but not millisecond-scale. The ``real-time'' of
our title should be read as \emph{fast enough to fit within a single
user interaction} rather than strict continuous-time inversion.
Reducing latency further through surrogate distillation, query-time
adaptive $K_{\mathrm{geo}}$ selection, or amortised cross-material
inverse is an open direction.
