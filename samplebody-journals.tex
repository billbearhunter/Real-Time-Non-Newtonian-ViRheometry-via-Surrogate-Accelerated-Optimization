\section{Introduction}

Many everyday fluids---yogurts, pastes, sauces, slurries, cosmetic
emulsions---exhibit non-Newtonian behavior that governs how they pour,
spread, cling, and deform. Characterizing this behavior quantitatively
requires measuring shear-rate-dependent stress, traditionally using
parallel-plate or cone-and-plate rheometers. However, these instruments
fail on a practically important class of materials: those containing
inclusions larger than the instrument's measurement gap---chunky sauces,
particulate suspensions, foods with solids---and those whose rheology
evolves faster than a multi-minute lab procedure can capture.

Non-Newtonian video rheometry~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} addresses
this gap by estimating Herschel--Bulkley parameters
$(\sigma_Y, \eta, n)$ from observations of a simple dam-break flow,
without contacting the material with any instrument. The underlying
principle is inverse simulation: a material point method (MPM) forward
model is repeatedly executed inside an optimizer until the simulated
flow reproduces the observed silhouette. Since the forward model is
physics-based, the approach naturally handles the very materials that
instrumental rheometers cannot, and recovers a full constitutive triple
from a single short video.

However, this otherwise uniquely capable approach is bottlenecked by the
cost of its inner loop. A single MPM dam-break simulation takes several
minutes on modern GPU hardware, and derivative-free optimization
typically issues hundreds of candidate evaluations before convergence.
The end-to-end inverse therefore takes \emph{hours per video}, confining
the method to offline research use and precluding three capabilities
that would otherwise be within reach of a video-based approach:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{Freshness for time-varying fluids.} When a material's
    properties evolve during the measurement window---sedimentation,
    aging, temperature drift, mixing history, cooking
    cooldown---an hour-scale inversion returns a parameter describing a
    past state. The inferred value is stale by the time it is delivered.
    \item \emph{Practical use on rheometer-infeasible materials.} The
    class of materials for which video rheometry has independent value
    over instrumental rheometry---chunky sauces, particulate
    suspensions---remains confined to offline demonstration rather than
    fieldable tool so long as each measurement costs hours.
    \item \emph{Multi-observation precision recovery.} The prior
    work~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} itself identifies a similarity
    degeneracy between yield stress and consistency, and proposes
    multi-setup joint inversion as the mitigation. At hours per setup,
    $N$-setup inversion is computationally infeasible in practice.
\end{itemize}

We propose a surrogate-accelerated framework that compresses the
inverse from hours to \emph{seconds per video}, making all three
capabilities available within a practical workflow. Our technical core
couples two components.
\emph{(i)}~A \emph{regime-aware hierarchical Mixture-of-GP surrogate}
(HVIMoGP-rBCM) replaces MPM inside the optimizer: a two-level Bayesian
Gaussian mixture gate routes each query---first by container geometry
$(W, H)$, then by observation signature---through one of 540 specialized
sparse Gaussian-process experts, aggregated at prediction time by the
robust Bayesian Committee Machine. This hierarchical structure is
motivated by the fact that the Herschel--Bulkley inverse landscape is
not globally homogeneous: the flow regime changes morphologically with
container shape and discretely across the yield threshold, so a single
global surrogate cannot cover the $5.5$ orders of magnitude in $\eta$
and $5.6$ orders in $\sigma_Y$ at the accuracy required for optimization.
\emph{(ii)}~An \emph{identifiability-aware inverse} in which CMA-ES is
warm-started from the routed expert's nearest training neighbor in
observation space, and anchored by a soft log-$\sigma_Y$ prior at that
neighbor. The prior is \emph{data-honest} in that its center comes from
a training point of the surrogate rather than from a user-chosen guess.
Together, the warm-start and anchored prior collapse the
yield-stress/consistency ridge, recovering a physically plausible
$\sigma_Y$ where vanilla CMA-ES would slide to $\sigma_Y \to 0$.

Our work inherits the problem setup, dam-break observation protocol, and
inverse formulation from~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}, and
contributes the machinery needed to make that formulation
computationally practical and to resolve an identifiability limit that
prior work had identified but could not practically mitigate. The main
contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item A \emph{hierarchical Mixture-of-GP surrogate} for the
    Herschel--Bulkley inverse landscape that covers
    $5.5$--$5.6$ orders of magnitude in $\eta$ and $\sigma_Y$ using 540
    specialized experts routed by a two-level Bayesian Gaussian mixture
    gate and aggregated via robust BCM.
    \item An \emph{identifiability-aware inverse loop} that combines
    training-nearest-neighbor warm-starting with a data-honest
    log-$\sigma_Y$ soft prior, resolving the yield-stress/consistency
    similarity degeneracy without injecting user-chosen bias.
    \item An evaluation establishing that seconds-scale video rheometry
    unlocks three capabilities inaccessible to simulation-in-the-loop
    inversion: \emph{(a)} fresh parameter estimates for time-varying
    fluids, \emph{(b)} practical characterization of materials
    conventional rheometers cannot measure, and \emph{(c)} a
    multi-observation joint protocol that approaches parallel-plate
    rheometer precision from phone videos alone.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Non-Newtonian Fluid Simulation}

Our forward model inherits the material point method (MPM) lineage that
brought continuum simulation into graphics~\cite{stomakhin2013material,
jiang2015affine,hu2018moving,jiang2016material}. Within this family,
shear-thinning and yield-stress rheology---captured by the
Herschel--Bulkley model~\cite{herschel1926konsistenzmessungen}---has been
realized through return-mapping formulations that encompass viscous,
viscoplastic, and granular regimes under a common MPM
substrate~\cite{yue2015continuum,ram2015material,klar2016drucker,
tampubolon2017multi,wolper2019cdmpm}. We adopt the Herschel--Bulkley
return map of Yue et al.~\cite{yue2015continuum}; the sauce-specific
shear-thinning simulator of Nagasawa et al.~\cite{nagasawa2019mixing},
from the same lab as our direct predecessor, shares our material focus.
The forward simulator is implemented on Taichi~\cite{hu2019taichi}; a
broader introduction to fluid simulation for graphics
appears in~\cite{bridson2015fluid}.


\subsection{Video-Based Inverse Rheometry}

Estimating simulation parameters from video by matching rendered and
observed dynamics has a long lineage in graphics and
vision~\cite{bhat2002computing,monszpart2016smash}. Takahashi and
Lin~\cite{takahashi2019video} established the video-to-parameter pattern
for viscous fluids by matching rendered and observed dam-break
silhouettes on a per-scene basis; Hamamichi et
al.~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} extended this to the non-Newtonian
Herschel--Bulkley triple $(\sigma_Y, \eta, n)$ via dimensional-similarity
analysis over tabulated MPM outputs, and is the direct predecessor of
our work. We inherit their dam-break observation protocol, their
Herschel--Bulkley inverse objective, and their simulation-in-the-loop
formulation, and contribute the machinery needed to make that
formulation practical at seconds-scale latency and to resolve the
yield-stress/consistency identifiability degeneracy that
\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} identifies but leaves mitigated only at
prohibitive computational cost.

Related work recovers material parameters from other visual modalities:
video-based cloth friction~\cite{rasheed2020visual},
particle-image-velocimetry-based rheology through physics-informed
networks~\cite{thakur2025learning}, and consumer-video neural regression
for scalar viscosity~\cite{rauzan2024protorheology}. These target
different material classes or observation modalities and do not recover
the full Herschel--Bulkley triple. The physical basis for encoding
rheology in dam-break fronts was established analytically by
Balmforth et al.~\cite{balmforth2007two}, and free-surface extraction
from experimental video is treated in~\cite{cochard2009tracking}. The
inverse-rheometry problem has also been approached in the
geophysical-flow setting using shallow-water forward models and adjoint
methods~\cite{martin2010inverse}; our formulation differs in using a
full three-dimensional MPM forward model and a derivative-free inverse
over a learned surrogate.


\subsection{Differentiable MPM for Inverse Problems}

An alternative paradigm pursues parameter recovery via differentiable
simulation, using gradients through the forward model for gradient-based
inverse search. The differentiable MLS-MPM
family~\cite{hu2019chainqueen,hu2020difftaichi,huang2021plasticinelab}
has been applied to deformable and plastic materials, and end-to-end
differentiable physics with differentiable rendering has been
demonstrated by gradSim~\cite{murthy2021gradsim}. Most relevant to
non-Newtonian parameter recovery from video,
PAC-NeRF~\cite{li2023pacnerf} couples differentiable MPM with NeRF
rendering to recover viscosity parameters from multi-view video, and
Eastman~\cite{eastman2024recovery} recovers Herschel--Bulkley parameters
from a ketchup column-collapse video via differentiable MPM and neural
rendering. These approaches optimize per scene and do not amortize a
reusable surrogate across materials. Further, evidence from
PlasticineLab~\cite{huang2021plasticinelab} indicates that gradients
through non-smooth plastic return maps are ill-conditioned, motivating
our choice of a gradient-free inverse over a probabilistic surrogate.


\subsection{Gaussian-Process Surrogates and Mixtures of Experts}

Gaussian-process regression~\cite{rasmussen2006gaussian} provides a
natural surrogate model for expensive simulators because it couples
flexible nonparametric regression with calibrated predictive
uncertainty. For data sizes beyond exact-GP feasibility, variational and
sparse approximations~\cite{snelson2006sparse,quinonero2005unifying,
titsias2009variational,hensman2013gaussian,matthews2016sparse} replace
the full covariance with an inducing-variable approximation; each expert
in our surrogate is an exact GP trained on a gate-routed subset, with
SVGP backends available at larger scales.

A single global GP is insufficient for functions with heterogeneous
local structure. Mixture-of-experts (MoE)
models~\cite{jordan1994hierarchical,yuksel2012twenty} decompose
prediction into multiple specialists with a gating mechanism, and
mixtures of GPs instantiate this idea with GP
experts~\cite{tresp2001mixtures,rasmussen2002infinite,
meeds2006alternative,nguyen2008local,yuan2008variational,trapp2020deep}.
Among these, Meeds and Osindero~\cite{meeds2006alternative} (a Gaussian
mixture over inputs coupled with GP outputs) and Nguyen-Tuong et
al.~\cite{nguyen2008local} (hard input-space partitioning with
independent per-region GPs and nearest-neighbor fusion) are the closest
architectural ancestors of our surrogate. We extend this pattern with a
two-level Bayesian Gaussian mixture gate---first on container geometry
$(W, H)$, then on an observation signature---and replace
nearest-neighbor fusion with robust committee-machine aggregation.

For aggregating independent GP predictions, the Bayesian Committee
Machine~\cite{tresp2000bayesian} and its modern variants---generalized
Product of Experts~\cite{cao2014generalized}, robust BCM
(rBCM)~\cite{deisenroth2015distributed}, and generalized robust BCM
(GRBCM)~\cite{liu2018generalized}---provide closed-form
variance-weighted fusion. We adopt
rBCM~\cite{deisenroth2015distributed} with top-1 routing; our ablations
(\S\ref{sec:ablation}) show that the additional communication expert of
GRBCM does not justify its inference-time overhead given our peaked
gate posterior. Sparse-expert plus BCM aggregation has recently been
applied to physics emulation~\cite{cohen2024sparse}, providing modern
precedent for our approach.


\subsection{Surrogate-Accelerated Inverse and Derivative-Free Optimization}

Inverting expensive simulators has been addressed under the headings of
Bayesian calibration of computer models~\cite{kennedy2001bayesian} and
simulation-based inference~\cite{cranmer2020frontier}, with efficient
global optimization~\cite{jones1998efficient} and Bayesian
optimization~\cite{snoek2012practical,shahriari2016taking} providing the
dominant search strategies when the objective is expensive and
derivative-free. Our inverse is closest to the ``CMA-ES on a GP
surrogate'' pattern~\cite{bajaj2019gaussian,hansen2016cma}, which has
been applied to inverse
viscoplasticity~\cite{vandamme2021bayesian} and
viscoelasticity~\cite{hashemi2025bayesian}. We depart from this pattern
in two respects: first, each inverse is routed through a hierarchical
surrogate rather than a single global GP; second, we introduce an
observation-space nearest-neighbor warm-start and a
training-data-anchored log-$\sigma_Y$ prior to address the yield-stress
identifiability degeneracy that is specific to Herschel--Bulkley
inversion and not resolved by generic BO machinery.


\section{Background}

\subsection{Herschel--Bulkley Rheology}

Many non-Newtonian fluids are well approximated by the
Herschel--Bulkley (HB) model~\cite{herschel1926konsistenzmessungen},
which expresses the shear-stress response as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hb-scalar}
    \tau \;=\; \sigma_Y \;+\; \eta\,\dot\gamma^{\,n},
\end{equation}
where $\tau$ is the shear stress, $\dot\gamma$ the shear rate,
$\sigma_Y$ the yield stress, $\eta$ the consistency parameter, and
$n \in (0, 1]$ controls shear thinning ($n < 1$) versus Newtonian
behaviour ($n = 1$). In three dimensions the deviatoric stress tensor
is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hb-tensor}
    \boldsymbol{\tau}^{\mathrm{dev}}
    \;=\;
    \left[\;\frac{\sigma_Y}{\|\mathbf{D}\|}
    \;+\; \eta\,\|\mathbf{D}\|^{\,n-1}\;\right] \mathbf{D},
    \qquad
    \|\mathbf{D}\|
    = \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{D}\!:\!\mathbf{D}},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{D}$ is the rate-of-strain tensor. Below the yield
surface $\|\boldsymbol{\tau}^{\mathrm{dev}}\| \le \sigma_Y$ the
material is treated as rigid; above it, the flow rule of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:hb-tensor} applies. We adopt the elasto-plastic MPM
realisation of Yue et al.~\cite{yue2015continuum}, which recovers the
same $(\sigma_Y, \eta, n)$ triple at steady state via a
Newton--Raphson return map. The HB response is therefore characterised
by the three-parameter vector
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\theta} \;=\; (\sigma_Y,\,\eta,\,n) \in \Theta \subset
    \mathbb{R}^{3}.
\end{equation}
This parameter triple coincides with that of prior non-Newtonian video
rheometry~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}; our goal is not to
propose a new constitutive model, but to recover $\boldsymbol{\theta}$
from visual observations at a latency compatible with real-world use.


\subsection{Dam-Break MPM Forward Model}

Following the experimental protocol
of~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}, we obtain $\boldsymbol{\theta}$
by observing a dam-break flow released from a rectangular fluid column
of width $W$ and height $H$ on a flat, no-slip floor. The forward
simulator integrates the HB return map of \S3.1 on a
moving-least-squares MPM~\cite{hu2018moving} grid with spacing
$\Delta x \approx 10^{-1}$\,cm and timestep $\Delta t = 5\times
10^{-4}$\,s, implemented on Taichi~\cite{hu2019taichi}. The
observation is a vector of flow-front displacements sampled at eight
uniformly spaced frames $t_1,\dots,t_8$ after release:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:y-def}
    \mathbf{y}
    \;=\;
    \bigl(x_1,\,x_2,\,\dots,\,x_8\bigr)^{\!\top}
    \;\in\;
    \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}^{8},
\end{equation}
where $x_i$ is the maximum lateral extent of the fluid at time $t_i$
in cm. By construction $x_1 \le x_2 \le \dots \le x_8$, since the
flow front cannot retract. We write the forward simulator as
$\mathbf{y} = \mathcal{S}(\boldsymbol{\theta};\,W, H)$.


\subsection{Inverse Video Rheometry as Simulation-in-the-Loop Optimization}

Given an observation $\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$ captured from a real
experiment at geometry $(W, H)$, video rheometry recovers
$\boldsymbol{\theta}$ by matching simulator output against the
observation,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:inverse-generic}
    \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}
    \;=\;
    \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}\in\Theta}\;
    \mathcal{L}\bigl(\boldsymbol{\theta};\,
    \mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}},\,W,\,H\bigr),
\end{equation}
where
$\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \|\mathcal{S}(\boldsymbol{\theta};\,W,H)
- \mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}\|^{2}$ is an observation-matching loss.
Because $\mathcal{S}$ is defined by an MPM integration,
$\mathcal{L}$ is expensive---several minutes per query on modern
GPU hardware---and non-smooth through the plastic return map. The
simulation-in-the-loop formulation of~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}
evaluates $\mathcal{S}$ directly inside a derivative-free optimiser,
paying the cost of a full MPM simulation at every candidate
$\boldsymbol{\theta}$.

The loss landscape carries a well-known identifiability pathology.
From Eq.~\eqref{eq:hb-scalar}, the term
$\eta\,\dot\gamma^{\,n}$ dominates the HB response once the shear
rate exceeds $\dot\gamma^{\star} \approx
(\sigma_Y / \eta)^{1/n}$; above this threshold, the stress is nearly
independent of $\sigma_Y$. If the observed dam-break regime lies
largely above $\dot\gamma^{\star}$, the observation
$\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$ becomes effectively insensitive to
$\sigma_Y$ while still constraining $(\eta, n)$. This produces a ridge
in the $(\sigma_Y, \eta)$ subspace of the loss surface: a continuum of
nearly-equal-loss solutions trading $\sigma_Y$ for a compensating
increase in $\eta$. Hamamichi et al.~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}
identify this degeneracy by dimensional-similarity analysis and
propose joint inversion over multiple $(W, H)$ setups as the
mitigation; however, under their simulation-in-the-loop cost,
multi-setup inversion is $N$ times more expensive than the already
hours-long single-setup inverse.

Finally, in many practical scenarios the target material itself
evolves during the measurement window (sedimentation, aging,
temperature drift, mixing history, cooking cooldown), so that the
true parameter state $\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t}$ drifts during the
inverse. A delayed estimate may therefore no longer correspond to the
current material condition, motivating estimation latency short
enough to track the evolving state rather than merely reduce
amortised compute cost.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Overview}

Our goal is to compress the inverse of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:inverse-generic} from hours to seconds without altering
the physics of the forward model. We replace expensive MPM queries
with predictions from a trained hierarchical Mixture-of-GP surrogate
$\tilde{\mathcal{S}}$, and augment the derivative-free inverse with
two pieces of problem-specific structure---an observation-space
warm-start and a training-data-anchored prior---that together resolve
the identifiability degeneracy discussed in \S3.3. The pipeline has
three phases, summarised in Figure~\ref{fig:overview}. In the
\emph{offline} phase we generate a library of MPM training runs and
fit the hierarchical surrogate (\S\ref{sec:surrogate}). In the
\emph{online forward} phase each query
$(\boldsymbol{\theta}, W, H)$ is routed through the hierarchical gate
to a specialised expert and predicted in milliseconds. In the
\emph{online inverse} phase each observation is routed to its expert
and the surrogate is driven by an identifiability-aware CMA-ES loop
(\S\ref{sec:inverse}).


\subsection{Input and Output Representation}
\label{sec:repr}

Each training sample pairs a parameter-geometry vector with its
forward observation
$\mathbf{y} = \mathcal{S}(\boldsymbol{\theta}; W, H) \in
\mathbb{R}^{8}$. We assemble the five-dimensional input
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x} \;=\; (n,\,\eta,\,\sigma_Y,\,W,\,H)^{\!\top}
    \in \mathbb{R}^{5},
\end{equation}
and apply per-component log transforms to $\eta$ and $\sigma_Y$
(which span $5.5$ and $5.6$ orders of magnitude respectively),
followed by per-dimension $z$-score standardisation,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:xscale}
    \tilde{\mathbf{x}}
    \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{x}_{\log} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_X}{\boldsymbol{\sigma}_X},
    \qquad
    \mathbf{x}_{\log}
    = \bigl(n,\;\log(\eta + \varepsilon),\;
    \log(\sigma_Y + \varepsilon),\;W,\;H\bigr)^{\!\top},
\end{equation}
with $\varepsilon = 10^{-8}$. For outputs we subtract the training
mean but keep the raw scale, preserving the monotone ordering in
physical units:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:yscale}
    \tilde{\mathbf{y}} \;=\; \mathbf{y} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_Y.
\end{equation}
The statistics
$(\boldsymbol{\mu}_X, \boldsymbol{\sigma}_X, \boldsymbol{\mu}_Y)$ are
fit once on the full training set and frozen.


\subsection{Hierarchical Mixture-of-GP Surrogate}
\label{sec:surrogate}

A single global GP cannot cover the $5.5$--$5.6$-order-of-magnitude
input box at the accuracy required for inversion: the dam-break
morphology changes discretely with container geometry and again
across the yield threshold, so the input-to-output map is not locally
stationary. We therefore partition the space hierarchically and fit a
specialised expert per partition cell.

\paragraph{Two-level Bayesian Gaussian mixture gate.}
The gate is a hard, top-1 routing over
$\sum_{g} K_{\phi}^{(g)}$ partition cells. The level-1 gate clusters
the geometry subspace,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:level1}
    g^{\star}(W, H)
    \;=\;
    \arg\max_{g \in \{1,\dots,K_{\mathrm{geo}}\}}\,
    p^{\mathrm{geo}}_{g}\!\bigl(W, H\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $p^{\mathrm{geo}}$ is a Bayesian Gaussian mixture with a
Dirichlet-process weight prior and full $2\times 2$ covariances, fit
on standardised $(W, H)$ pairs. The level-2 gate further clusters
each geo group by an 18-dimensional observation signature
$\boldsymbol{\phi}$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:level2}
    k^{\star}\bigl(\boldsymbol{\phi}, g^{\star}\bigr)
    \;=\;
    \arg\max_{k \in \{1,\dots,K_{\phi}^{(g^{\star})}\}}\,
    p^{\phi\mid g^{\star}}_{k}\bigl(\boldsymbol{\phi}\bigr).
\end{equation}
We use $K_{\mathrm{geo}} = 12$ globally; the level-2 component count
is chosen per geo group by the Bayesian information criterion,
yielding $K_{\phi}^{(g)} \in \{30, 40, 45, 50\}$ and
$\sum_{g} K_{\phi}^{(g)} = 540$ cells in total (cf.~the hard-partition
GMM-gated pattern of~\cite{meeds2006alternative,nguyen2008local}).

\paragraph{Observation signature.}
The level-2 gate is driven by an 18-dimensional feature
$\boldsymbol{\phi}(\mathbf{y}, W, H)$ that summarises the shape of
the observed flow curve and the container regime:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:phi}
    \boldsymbol{\phi}
    = \Bigl(\,
        \underbrace{\mathbf{y}/y_8}_{8},\;
        \underbrace{\Delta(\mathbf{y}/y_8)}_{7},\;
        \underbrace{\log(y_8 + \varepsilon)}_{1},\;
        \underbrace{\log\!\sqrt{WH}}_{1},\;
        \underbrace{\log(W/H)}_{1}
    \,\Bigr)^{\!\top}\!\!.
\end{equation}
The first eight coordinates normalise the flow curve by its final
displacement, so materials with similar \emph{shape} but different
\emph{magnitude} cluster together; the next seven are first
differences; the last three encode container scale and aspect ratio.
At inverse time the gate uses
$\boldsymbol{\phi}(\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}, W, H)$; during training,
$\mathbf{y}$ is the MPM output.

\paragraph{Per-cell Gaussian-process experts.}
Each partition cell $(g, k)$ owns an exact Gaussian-process expert.
The standardised output is modelled by eight independent
one-dimensional GPs that share the same standardised input
$\tilde{\mathbf{x}}$, each with a Mat\'ern-$5/2$ kernel with
automatic relevance determination
(ARD)~\cite{rasmussen2006gaussian},
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:matern}
    k_{5/2}(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b})
    = \sigma_f^{\,2}\!\left(
    1 + \sqrt{5}\,r + \tfrac{5}{3}r^{2}\right) e^{-\sqrt{5}\,r},
    \qquad
    r^{2}\!=\!\sum_{d=1}^{5}\frac{(a_d - b_d)^{2}}{\ell_d^{\,2}},
\end{equation}
and a constant mean. The kernel variance $\sigma_f^{\,2}$, the
observation noise, and the five ARD lengthscales
$\{\ell_d\}_{d=1}^{5}$ are learned per expert and per output
dimension by maximising the exact marginal likelihood with Adam
(learning rate $5\cdot 10^{-2}$, $100$ iterations). Experts are
trained independently in parallel across cells.

\paragraph{Robust Bayesian Committee Machine aggregation.}
Given a query $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}$ routed to an expert set
$\mathcal{R}$, we aggregate per-expert predictive moments
$(\mu_k, \sigma_k^{2})$ via the robust Bayesian Committee
Machine~\cite{deisenroth2015distributed}:
\begin{align}
\label{eq:rbcm}
    \sigma_\star^{-2}
    &= \Bigl(\,1 - \!\!\sum_{k\in\mathcal{R}}\! \beta_k\Bigr)\,
        \sigma_{\mathrm{prior}}^{-2}
       + \sum_{k\in\mathcal{R}}\! \beta_k\,\sigma_k^{-2},
\nonumber\\
    \mu_\star
    &= \sigma_\star^{\,2}\sum_{k\in\mathcal{R}}
       \beta_k\,\sigma_k^{-2}\,\mu_k,
    \qquad
    \beta_k = \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left[\log\sigma_{\mathrm{prior}}^{2}
                                  - \log\sigma_k^{2}\right]_{+},
\end{align}
where $\sigma_{\mathrm{prior}}^{2}$ is the empirical variance of the
expert's training outputs and $[\cdot]_{+}$ clips at zero. In our
top-1 configuration ($|\mathcal{R}| = 1$), rBCM reduces to the
single-expert predictive mean with a variance-weighted calibration;
we retain the full rBCM formula because the same code path supports
soft multi-expert aggregation for ablation
(\S\ref{sec:ablation}).

\paragraph{Polynomial residual correction.}
After all $540$ experts are trained, we fit a small global correction
to further suppress residuals near partition boundaries without
retraining the GPs. For each expert $(g, k)$ and output dimension
$d$, define the raw-parameter degree-$2$ monomial basis
\begin{equation}
    \Phi(\mathbf{x})
    = \bigl(1,\,n,\,\eta,\,\sigma_Y,\,W,\,H,\,
      n^{2},\,n\eta,\,\ldots,\,H^{2}\bigr)^{\!\top}
      \in \mathbb{R}^{21},
\end{equation}
and solve a ridge regression on the GP residuals,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:poly}
    \mathbf{c}_{(g,k),d}
    \;=\; \arg\min_{\mathbf{c}}\,
    \bigl\|\tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{d} - \mu_{(g,k),d} - \Phi\,\mathbf{c}\bigr\|^{2}
    + \alpha\,\|\mathbf{c}_{\backslash 0}\|^{2},
    \qquad \alpha = 10^{-3},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{c}_{\backslash 0}$ excludes the intercept from
regularisation. At inference the corrected mean is
$\mu_{(g,k),d} + \Phi(\mathbf{x})^{\!\top}\mathbf{c}_{(g,k),d}$.

\paragraph{Monotonic post-processing.}
The dam-break observation is non-decreasing by construction, but
aggregation and numerical noise can introduce mild violations. After
inverse-standardisation we enforce monotonicity by a cumulative
maximum in physical space,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mono}
    \hat{y}_{d}
    \;\leftarrow\; \max_{j\le d}\,\hat{y}_{j},
    \qquad d = 1,\dots,8.
\end{equation}
We denote the full surrogate pipeline
(standardise $\to$ route $\to$ aggregate $\to$ correct $\to$
de-standardise $\to$ monotonise) by
$\tilde{\mathcal{S}}(\boldsymbol{\theta}; W, H)$.


\subsection{Identifiability-Aware Inverse}
\label{sec:inverse}

Replacing $\mathcal{S}$ with $\tilde{\mathcal{S}}$ in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:inverse-generic} reduces per-candidate cost from
minutes to milliseconds, but does not by itself resolve the
identifiability ridge of \S3.3. A vanilla CMA-ES running on the
surrogate can still slide to $\sigma_Y \to 0$ while inflating $\eta$
to match the observation. We inject three pieces of problem
structure.

\paragraph{Nearest-neighbour warm-start in observation space.}
Let $\mathcal{D}_{k^{\star}}$ denote the training set of the routed
expert. We initialise CMA-ES at
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:nn}
    \boldsymbol{\theta}^{\mathrm{NN}}
    \;=\; \boldsymbol{\theta}_{i^{\star}},
    \qquad
    i^{\star}
    \;=\;
    \arg\min_{i\,\in\,\mathcal{D}_{k^{\star}}}\;
    \frac{\|\mathbf{y}_i - \mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}\|^{2}}
         {\max\!\bigl(\overline{\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}^{\,2}},\,10^{-12}\bigr)},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{y}_i$ is the monotonised flow curve of training
sample $i$ and the denominator normalises by observation magnitude.
Since $\boldsymbol{\theta}^{\mathrm{NN}}$ is a training point, its
$\sigma_Y$ component lies on the data manifold, giving CMA-ES an
initial $\sigma_Y$ that is physically plausible rather than arbitrary.

\paragraph{Data-honest log-$\sigma_Y$ prior.}
We augment the surrogate loss with a soft quadratic prior on
$\log\sigma_Y$ anchored at the NN sample:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:loss}
    \tilde{\mathcal{L}}(\boldsymbol{\theta})
    \;=\;
    \underbrace{\frac{\|\tilde{\mathcal{S}}(\boldsymbol{\theta};\,W,H)
                      - \mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}\|^{2}}
                     {\overline{\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}^{\,2}}}}_{\mathrm{NMSE}}
    \;+\;
    \lambda\!\left(\frac{\log\sigma_Y - \log\sigma_Y^{\mathrm{NN}}}
                        {w}\right)^{\!2},
\end{equation}
with $\lambda = 10^{-5}$ and $w = 1$ (log-std). A factor-of-$e$
deviation from $\sigma_Y^{\mathrm{NN}}$ contributes $10^{-5}$ to the
loss---on the same order as a typical converged NMSE---so the prior
becomes active at the physical identifiability scale. It is
\emph{data-honest} in that its centre $\sigma_Y^{\mathrm{NN}}$ is
a training sample of $\mathcal{D}_{k^{\star}}$ rather than a
user-specified guess. Removing the prior inflates $\sigma_Y$ error by
roughly an order of magnitude on low-flow cases; over-weighting it
($\lambda \gtrsim 10^{-3}$) pulls $\sigma_Y$ directly to
$\sigma_Y^{\mathrm{NN}}$ and overrides the data term. See the
ablation in \S\ref{sec:ablation}.

\paragraph{Per-expert bound tightening.}
We restrict CMA-ES search to the axis-aligned bounding box of the
routed expert's training inputs (plus a $2\%$ log-space pad on
$\eta$ and $\sigma_Y$, linear pad on $n$), intersected with the
global bounds $\Theta$. This prevents the optimiser from entering
regions where the expert has no support and would extrapolate into
spurious near-zero-flow minima.

\paragraph{CMA-ES configuration.}
We run CMA-ES~\cite{hansen2016cma,bajaj2019gaussian} with population
size $12$, initial step size $\sigma_0 = 0.25$ (log-space), and a
hard budget of $25$ generations---$300$ surrogate evaluations total.
Parameter bounds are enforced through CMA's boundary handler. The
algorithm returns the best-ever candidate
$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ together with the surrogate prediction
$\hat{\mathbf{y}} = \tilde{\mathcal{S}}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}};
W, H)$ and the converged loss. On a single consumer GPU, the
entire per-video inverse---routing, NN warm-start, $25$ CMA
generations, and the final forward---takes a median of $12$\,s.


\subsection{Algorithms}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:train} summarises offline training.
Algorithms~\ref{alg:forward} and~\ref{alg:invert} summarise the online
forward and inverse passes, respectively.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Offline training of HVIMoGP-rBCM.}
\label{alg:train}
\KwIn{Training set
      $\{(\boldsymbol{\theta}_i, W_i, H_i, \mathbf{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$,
      $N = 295{,}419$; gate granularity
      $K_{\mathrm{geo}} = 12$ and $\{K_{\phi}^{(g)}\}_{g=1}^{12}$ from
      BIC scan.}
\KwOut{Hierarchical surrogate checkpoint.}
Fit input/output scalers
  $(\boldsymbol{\mu}_X, \boldsymbol{\sigma}_X, \boldsymbol{\mu}_Y)$
  \tcp*{Eqs.~\eqref{eq:xscale}--\eqref{eq:yscale}}
Fit level-1 BGM $p^{\mathrm{geo}}$ on
  $\{(W_i, H_i)\}$, $K = K_{\mathrm{geo}}$
  \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:level1}}
\ForEach{geo group $g = 1,\dots,K_{\mathrm{geo}}$}{
  Compute $\boldsymbol{\phi}_i = \boldsymbol{\phi}(\mathbf{y}_i, W_i, H_i)$
    for $i \in g$\;
  Fit level-2 BGM $p^{\phi\mid g}$, $K = K_{\phi}^{(g)}$
    \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:level2}}
}
\ForEach{cell $(g, k)$}{
  Collect routed subset $\mathcal{D}_{(g,k)}$ via
    Eqs.~\eqref{eq:level1}--\eqref{eq:level2}\;
  \For{$d = 1, \dots, 8$}{
    Train Mat\'ern-$5/2$ ARD exact GP on $\mathcal{D}_{(g,k)}$
      (Adam, $100$ iter)
      \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:matern}}
  }
  Fit degree-$2$ polynomial residual
    $\mathbf{c}_{(g,k), \cdot}$ on GP residuals
    \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:poly}}
}
\Return{model checkpoint
  (scalers, BGM gates, 540 experts, poly residuals).}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Online forward pass
  $\tilde{\mathcal{S}}(\boldsymbol{\theta}; W, H)$.}
\label{alg:forward}
\KwIn{Parameter $\boldsymbol{\theta} = (n, \eta, \sigma_Y)$,
      geometry $(W, H)$, observation signature
      $\boldsymbol{\phi}$ from
      $\boldsymbol{\phi}(\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}, W, H)$.}
\KwOut{Predicted flow curve $\hat{\mathbf{y}} \in \mathbb{R}^{8}$.}
Standardise $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:xscale}\;
$g^{\star} \leftarrow \arg\max_g p^{\mathrm{geo}}_{g}(W, H)$
  \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:level1}}
$k^{\star} \leftarrow
  \arg\max_k p^{\phi\mid g^{\star}}_{k}(\boldsymbol{\phi})$
  \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:level2}}
Query expert $(g^{\star}, k^{\star})$ for
  $(\mu_{k^{\star}}, \sigma_{k^{\star}}^{2})$\;
Aggregate via rBCM to get $(\mu_\star, \sigma_\star^{2})$
  \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:rbcm}}
$\mu_\star \leftarrow \mu_\star +
  \Phi(\mathbf{x})^{\!\top}\mathbf{c}_{(g^{\star}, k^{\star})}$
  \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:poly}}
$\hat{\mathbf{y}} \leftarrow \mu_\star + \boldsymbol{\mu}_Y$
  \tcp*{de-standardise}
Enforce $\hat{y}_d \leftarrow \max_{j \le d}\,\hat{y}_j$
  \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:mono}}
\Return{$\hat{\mathbf{y}}$}\;
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Online inverse
  $(\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}, W, H) \mapsto \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$.}
\label{alg:invert}
\KwIn{Observed flow curve $\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}$,
      geometry $(W, H)$, trained surrogate
      $\tilde{\mathcal{S}}$.}
\KwOut{Estimated parameters
  $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}} = (\hat{n}, \hat{\eta}, \hat{\sigma}_Y)$.}
$\boldsymbol{\phi} \leftarrow \boldsymbol{\phi}(\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{obs}}, W, H)$
  \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:phi}}
Route
  $(g^{\star}, k^{\star})$ via
  Eqs.~\eqref{eq:level1}--\eqref{eq:level2}\;
Compute tightened bounds
  $\Theta_{k^{\star}} \subseteq \Theta$
  (\S\ref{sec:inverse})\;
$\boldsymbol{\theta}^{\mathrm{NN}},\,\sigma_Y^{\mathrm{NN}}
  \leftarrow$ NN sample in $\mathcal{D}_{k^{\star}}$
  \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:nn}}
Initialise CMA-ES on $\Theta_{k^{\star}}$ with
  $\mathbf{x}_0 = \boldsymbol{\theta}^{\mathrm{NN}}$,
  $\sigma_0 = 0.25$, popsize $= 12$\;
\For{$t = 1, \dots, 25$}{
  Sample population $\{\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(p)}\}_{p=1}^{12}$\;
  Evaluate
    $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(p)})$
    via Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss} on $\tilde{\mathcal{S}}$\;
  Update CMA-ES state\;
}
$\hat{\mathbf{y}} \leftarrow
  \tilde{\mathcal{S}}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}; W, H)$\;
\Return{$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}},\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}$.}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

We evaluate HVIMoGP-rBCM and the identifiability-aware inverse
against four questions: (1) how faithfully does the surrogate
approximate the MPM forward model, (2) how accurately does the full
inverse recover $\boldsymbol{\theta} = (\sigma_Y, \eta, n)$ on
held-out samples, (3) what wall-clock reduction does the surrogate
deliver relative to simulation-in-the-loop inversion, and (4) do the
three contributions of \S1---freshness, rheometer-infeasible
materials, and multi-observation precision---hold under experimental
validation?

\paragraph{Datasets.}
We use three evaluation sets. \emph{$T_{\mathrm{sim}}$} contains
$22{,}209$ MPM samples held out from the $295{,}419$-row training
corpus, sampled stratified over
$(n, \log\eta, \log\sigma_Y, W, H)$. \emph{$T_{\mathrm{inv}}$} is a
synthetic benchmark of $30$ materials drawn from within the training
box, each rendered via MPM at three container sizes (total $90$
clips). \emph{$T_{\mathrm{real}}$} consists of six to eight standard
non-Newtonian fluids (honey, ketchup, mayonnaise, shampoo, Carbopol
gel, and a lotion sample) captured on a smartphone in our dam-break
apparatus and accompanied by parallel-plate rheometer sweeps.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
\emph{Baseline A} is the simulation-in-the-loop pipeline
of~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian}: identical observation, identical
CMA-ES hyperparameters, but every candidate evaluated by a fresh MPM
run rather than by the surrogate. Baseline~A defines the accuracy
upper bound and wall-time lower bound of the simulation-in-the-loop
family. \emph{Baseline B} is a reduced version of our own pipeline
that isolates the contribution of hierarchical gating and the
identifiability-aware inverse: it uses a flat Bayesian GMM over
$(W, H)$ with $K = 12$ and no second-level $\phi$ gate ($12$ experts
total), top-$2$ hard routing, and a CMA-ES inverse without warm-start
or $\sigma_Y$ prior.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
For the forward surrogate we report per-output mean absolute error
(MAE), coefficient of determination $R^2$, and the row-max error
distribution ($p_{50}$, $p_{95}$, $p_{99}$, $p_{999}$). For the
inverse we report per-parameter relative error
$\mathrm{relE}(\cdot) = |\hat{\cdot} - (\cdot)_{\mathrm{gt}}| /
|(\cdot)_{\mathrm{gt}}|$ on $(n, \eta, \sigma_Y)$, the forward NMSE
at the recovered parameter as a sanity check on fit, and the
per-sample wall-clock. All inverse runs use the frozen hyperparameters
of Table~\ref{tab:hyper} and a fixed seed.


\subsection{Surrogate Fidelity}
\label{sec:fidelity}

We first verify that the trained surrogate predicts MPM outputs
accurately enough to justify replacing the simulator inside the
inverse. On $T_{\mathrm{sim}}$, the full HVIMoGP-rBCM surrogate
attains aggregate MAE $0.042$\,cm and $R^2 > 0.999$ across all
eight flow-front dimensions. Errors concentrate near partition
boundaries of the hierarchical gate---where a slightly mis-routed
query lands between specialised experts---and in sparsely sampled
regions of the input box. The degree-$2$ polynomial residual
correction (Eq.~\eqref{eq:poly}) is specifically designed to suppress
the former, reducing the $p_{999}$ row-max error by roughly a factor
of two.

\subsection{Ablations}
\label{sec:ablation}

We ablate the three design choices that distinguish our inverse from
a vanilla CMA-ES-on-GP: the aggregation scheme, the
nearest-neighbour warm-start, and the data-honest $\sigma_Y$ prior.
All rows in Table~\ref{tab:ablation} use identical gate routing,
identical experts, the same $10$-sample inverse set, and the frozen
CMA-ES budget ($12\times 25 = 300$ surrogate calls).

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\small
\caption{Ablation of inverse-side design choices; medians over
$10$ test samples. ``Fwd NMSE'' is the residual at the recovered
parameter; ``dt'' is per-sample wall-clock.}
\label{tab:ablation}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lccccc@{}}
\toprule
Config & $\mathrm{relE}\,n$ & $\mathrm{relE}\,\eta$ & $\mathrm{relE}\,\sigma_Y$ & Fwd NMSE & dt\,(s) \\
\midrule
Pure NN (no CMA)              & 0.115 & 0.153 & \textbf{0.168} & $3.9\times 10^{-3}$ & \textbf{0.0} \\
GRBCM $+$ warm                 & \textbf{0.076} & 0.198 & 0.366 & $1.5\times 10^{-5}$ & 21.6 \\
rBCM $+$ warm, no prior        & 0.109 & 0.201 & 0.459 & $\mathbf{9.5\times 10^{-6}}$ & 5.9 \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

Three findings.

\textbf{rBCM outperforms GRBCM at top-$1$ routing.}
GRBCM~\cite{liu2018generalized} augments each cluster with a
geometry-wide communication expert, doubling inference cost
($21.6$\,s vs.\ $12.0$\,s). On our peaked gate posterior the extra
expert contributes no independent information and the baseline blend
slightly worsens forward NMSE. We therefore adopt pure rBCM with
top-$1$ routing, which in our setting reduces to the single-expert
predictive mean with calibrated variance.

\textbf{Pure NN is the best $\sigma_Y$ estimator but cannot match the
flow curve.} The nearest-neighbour alone---returning
$\boldsymbol{\theta}^{\mathrm{NN}}$ without CMA refinement---recovers
$\sigma_Y$ with median relative error $0.168$, the best of all
configurations, because $\boldsymbol{\theta}^{\mathrm{NN}}$ lies on
the data manifold by construction. However, its forward NMSE
($3.9\times 10^{-3}$, two to three orders of magnitude worse than
any CMA-refined variant) shows that pure NN does not reproduce the
observation and is therefore useful only as an initialiser.

\textbf{The $\sigma_Y$ prior closes the gap that CMA-ES opens.}
Without the prior, rBCM with warm-start CMA-ES slides along the
identifiability ridge of \S3.3 to
$\mathrm{relE}\,\sigma_Y = 0.459$. Anchoring at
$\sigma_Y^{\mathrm{NN}}$ with $\lambda = 10^{-5}$ recovers
$\mathrm{relE}\,\sigma_Y = 0.298$---a $35\%$ improvement---at the
cost of a forward NMSE increase from $9.5\times 10^{-6}$ to
$1.4\times 10^{-5}$, both far below any perceptually meaningful
discrepancy. The prior trades a negligible amount of fit for a
large gain in identifiability.

\paragraph{Gate granularity.}
We additionally sweep the level-$1$ component count
$K_{\mathrm{geo}} \in \{1, 4, 12, 24\}$ and the level-$2$ count
$K_{\phi}^{(g)} \in \{30, 45\}$. $K_{\mathrm{geo}} = 1$ collapses to
a $\phi$-only single-level gate and loses roughly $2\times$
aggregate MAE relative to the two-level gate; $K_{\mathrm{geo}} = 24$
over-fragments the geometry space and starves individual experts of
training data. The BIC-selected per-geo $K_{\phi}^{(g)}$ (summing to
$540$) lies within $5\%$ of the best scanned setting.


\subsection{Parameter Freshness for Time-Varying Fluids}
\label{sec:freshness}

To validate contribution~(a) of \S1, we construct a controlled
time-varying experiment in which the true parameter vector
$\boldsymbol{\theta}(t)$ evolves during the measurement window. We
drive a linear drift in $\log\sigma_Y$,
$\log\sigma_Y(t) = \log\sigma_{Y,0} + \alpha\,t$, with $\alpha$
chosen so that $\sigma_Y$ doubles over the duration of one
simulation-in-the-loop inverse. At each sample time $t_k$ we compare
two estimators: (i)~a sim-in-loop estimator that returns
$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ describing the material state at the
\emph{beginning} of its hour-long optimisation, and (ii)~our
surrogate-accelerated estimator that returns
$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ essentially at the measurement instant.
We score both against the true $\boldsymbol{\theta}(t_k)$ in
per-parameter log-relative error.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/splash_evolution_strip.png}
    \caption{Tracking error for a $\sigma_Y(t)$ drift experiment.
    Top: true $\sigma_Y(t)$ (black) overlaid with the sim-in-loop
    estimate (orange, delayed by one inference cycle) and our
    surrogate estimate (blue, at measurement time). Bottom:
    per-measurement $|\log\hat\sigma_Y - \log\sigma_Y(t)|$.}
    \label{fig:freshness}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:freshness} shows the tracking error over $20$
consecutive measurements. The sim-in-loop baseline systematically
lags the true state by a full inference cycle, accumulating bias
proportional to $\alpha \cdot t_{\mathrm{infer}}$; the
surrogate-accelerated estimator tracks the true state to within the
per-sample recovery error of Table~\ref{tab:main}. Freshness is
therefore not a rhetorical claim: under realistic drift rates, the
temporal gap introduced by hour-scale inference dominates the
per-sample recovery error of a seconds-scale inverse.


\subsection{Characterising Rheometer-Infeasible Materials}
\label{sec:chunky}

Contribution~(b) addresses materials that conventional
parallel-plate rheometers cannot measure. We captured six such
fluids on a smartphone in our dam-break apparatus: a chunky okonomi
sauce, a tonkatsu sauce with visible solids, a congee suspension,
a sweet chilli sauce, a particle-loaded lotion, and a chuno sauce.
No rheometer ground truth is available for these samples \emph{by
design}: the parallel-plate geometry cannot maintain a clean gap
when particle size exceeds a few hundred microns. We evaluate
instead by \emph{sim--real replay}: we run MPM with the recovered
$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ and compare the simulated dam-break
silhouette against the original phone video frame by frame.

viscous dominance. Under the sim-in-loop pipeline of the direct
predecessor, the same protocol costs several hours per additional
setup---which is why multi-setup inversion was identified as the
$\sigma_Y$ mitigation but never adopted as a routine protocol in
prior work.

On materials for which a clean rheometer sweep is available, we
overlay the recovered HB flow curve against the rheometer reference.
Each material is captured at two to three $(W, H)$ setups per
\S\ref{sec:multi} and inverted jointly.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{What the Acceleration Unlocks}

The immediate effect of our pipeline is a three-orders-of-magnitude
reduction in per-video inverse cost. The more important effect is
that three capabilities previously out of reach under
simulation-in-the-loop become routine:
\emph{freshness} for time-varying
fluids (\S\ref{sec:freshness}),
practical characterisation of \emph{rheometer-infeasible}
materials (\S\ref{sec:chunky}), and
\emph{multi-observation joint inversion} that approaches
parallel-plate rheometer precision (\S\ref{sec:multi}). The first is
a temporal improvement that turns ``hours to return a past state''
into ``seconds to return the current state.'' The second inherits
the independent value of video rheometry over instrumental
rheometry~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} and brings it to
fieldable speed. The third makes usable a $\sigma_Y$-mitigation path
that the predecessor work identified but left computationally out of
reach.

\subsection{A Reusable Surrogate as a Community Asset}

The separation of a one-time offline training phase from a
seconds-scale online inference phase produces a reusable artefact
that is structurally unavailable to simulation-in-the-loop pipelines.
Our frozen $540$-expert checkpoint was trained once on
$295{,}419$ MPM simulations covering $5.5$--$5.6$ orders of magnitude
in $\eta$ and $\sigma_Y$ and the full $[2, 7]\,\mathrm{cm}$ range of
household containers. Subsequent users---and subsequent research on
video rheometry---can query the same checkpoint without paying the
MPM training cost again. This amortisation property is what turns
our method from a one-shot inverse into a reusable measurement
instrument; it is not available to sim-in-loop pipelines because
they have no surrogate to amortise. We release the checkpoint, the
training corpus, and the inverse code alongside this paper.

\subsection{Why Regime-Aware Surrogate and Data-Honest Prior Must Go
Together}

A takeaway at the core of this work is that neither the surrogate
nor the inverse modifications is sufficient alone. A single global
GP, even if it were accurate enough, would still leave the
$\sigma_Y$-identifiability ridge intact under a vanilla CMA-ES: the
optimiser would slide to $\sigma_Y \to 0$ while inflating $\eta$ to
match the observation. Conversely, the identifiability-aware inverse
of \S\ref{sec:inverse} requires an accurate surrogate to be evaluated
$300$ times per video within seconds; running the same inverse
against a coarse surrogate would reintroduce surrogate bias as a new
source of error. The hierarchical MoGP-rBCM surrogate and the
training-data-anchored prior must be coupled; this coupling is the
technical core.

\subsection{Relation to Differentiable MPM}

Differentiable MPM pipelines~\cite{li2023pacnerf,eastman2024recovery}
obtain gradients through the forward simulator and perform
gradient-based per-scene parameter search. We differ in two
respects. First, gradients through the non-smooth HB return map are
known to be
ill-conditioned~\cite{huang2021plasticinelab}; our gradient-free
inverse sidesteps this issue. Second, differentiable-MPM pipelines
optimise per scene and do not amortise a reusable surrogate, so
their per-material cost remains in the simulation-in-the-loop
regime. Our architectural choice of an offline-trained hierarchical
surrogate is precisely what enables the seconds-scale per-video
latency and the community-asset property of \S6.2.


\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Information-theoretic $\sigma_Y$ limit.}
The identifiability-aware inverse of \S\ref{sec:inverse} resolves
the yield-stress/consistency degeneracy whenever the dam-break
observation carries \emph{some} signal about $\sigma_Y$, but it
cannot recover information that is not in the observation. For
materials in which $\eta\,\dot\gamma^{\,n} \gg \sigma_Y$ across the
entire dam-break shear-rate range, the flow front encodes
essentially no information about $\sigma_Y$, and any inverse---ours
included---returns an estimate determined by the prior and the
training distribution rather than by the data. Among the $10$
held-out samples of Table~\ref{tab:main}, one such material
($\sigma_Y^{\mathrm{true}} \approx 0.39$\,Pa in a low-$\eta$ regime)
pushes the $\sigma_Y$ \emph{mean} to $7.0$ while the nine remaining
samples sit at median $0.298$. This is a task-level bound, not a
method bound; the appropriate mitigation is the multi-setup protocol
of \S\ref{sec:multi}.

\paragraph{Dependence on training-set coverage.}
The surrogate is trustworthy only inside the input box on which it
was trained. Queries outside this box trigger the per-expert bound
tightening (\S\ref{sec:inverse}), which relies on the routed
expert's training inputs as the admissible region and therefore
does not extrapolate. Out-of-distribution materials require an
extension of the training corpus. rBCM predictive variance flags
out-of-distribution queries but does not by itself correct them.

\paragraph{Fidelity of the forward model.}
A surrogate can only be as accurate as the simulator it learns from.
Our MPM forward (\S3.2) models HB rheology, gravity, and no-slip
boundaries, but not surface tension, elastic overshoot, thixotropy,
or viscoelastic memory. Materials exhibiting these phenomena deviate
systematically from the HB forward, and this deviation cannot be
recovered by any HB inverse. Richer constitutive
families---viscoelastic extensions and damage-MPM
variants~\cite{wolper2019cdmpm}---are natural but out-of-scope
directions.

\paragraph{Capture and geometry requirements.}
The level-$1$ gate is trained on rectangular cube-like containers
with $W, H \in [2, 7]$\,cm. Curved or irregular containers require
retraining the geometry gate on a broader container descriptor.
Camera calibration uses a printed ChArUco marker board; markerless
calibration from natural scene content is possible in principle but
not demonstrated here.

\paragraph{Real-time is a direction, not a destination.}
Our median wall-clock is $12$\,s per video on a single consumer
GPU---seconds-scale but not millisecond-scale. The ``real-time'' of
our title should be read as \emph{fast enough to fit within a single
user interaction} rather than strict continuous-time inversion.
Reducing latency further through surrogate distillation, query-time
adaptive $K_{\mathrm{geo}}$ selection, or amortised cross-material
inverse is an open direction.


\section{Conclusion}

We presented a surrogate-accelerated framework for non-Newtonian
video rheometry that compresses the Herschel--Bulkley inverse from
hours per video in simulation-in-the-loop prior
work~\cite{hamamichi2023nonnewtonian} to a median of $12$\,s per
video on a single consumer GPU. The method rests on two coupled
components: a regime-aware hierarchical Mixture-of-GP surrogate
(HVIMoGP-rBCM) with $540$ specialised experts routed by a two-level
Bayesian Gaussian mixture gate over container geometry and
observation signatures, and an identifiability-aware inverse that
warm-starts CMA-ES from the routed expert's nearest training
neighbour and anchors the $\sigma_Y$ axis with a data-honest soft
prior---resolving the yield-stress/consistency similarity degeneracy
that prior work identified but could not practically mitigate.

Beyond runtime reduction, the seconds-scale inverse unlocks three
capabilities inaccessible to simulation-in-the-loop rheometry: fresh
parameter estimates for time-varying fluids, practical
characterisation of materials conventional rheometers cannot
measure, and a multi-observation joint protocol that approaches
parallel-plate rheometer precision from phone videos alone. We
release the trained surrogate, the $295{,}419$-row MPM training
corpus, and the inverse code as a reusable community artefact on
which subsequent video-rheometry research can build without paying
the simulation training cost again.

The broader message is that video rheometry crosses the threshold
from laboratory demonstration to fieldable tool not by replacing
physics-based estimation but by amortising its cost. The surrogate
is an accelerator of a physics-grounded inverse, not a substitute
for physics; what this acceleration enables, at seconds per video,
is what brings the technique within reach of time-sensitive,
iterative, and situated measurement.
